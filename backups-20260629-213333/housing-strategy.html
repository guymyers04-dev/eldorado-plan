North El Dorado Housing Development Strategy

Address housing affordability crisis while capturing growth from FDI. Support 1,000+ net new jobs from economic development initiatives with workforce housing.

The Housing Crisis: Why It Matters

El Dorado Problem

Median home price/income ratio: 3.46x

This is ABOVE healthy rural Kansas norms (2.5–3.0x). Housing is overpriced relative to local wages.

Competitor Advantage

Emporia (2.97x) and Dodge City (2.94x) have better home affordability.

This is a COMPETITIVE WEAKNESS when recruiting young workers and families.

Strategic Implication: Every major FDI recruitment (500+ new jobs) REQUIRES housing expansion to accommodate workforce influx. Without new housing development, El Dorado will struggle to attract and retain workers for advanced manufacturing, data centers, and tech jobs.

Phase 1: North El Dorado Development District (Years 1–3)

Target: 200–300 new housing units (mixed-income)

Entry-Level Homes ($130–$160K)

Target audience: First-time homebuyers, young workers (age 25–35)
Size: 1,200–1,600 sq ft
Target: 100 units
Monthly mortgage: $850–$1,100 (affordable on $45K+ annual salary)

Mid-Range Homes ($180–$250K)

Target audience: Established workers, growing families
Size: 1,800–2,200 sq ft
Target: 100 units
Monthly mortgage: $1,200–$1,650 (affordable on $60K+ salary)

Premium/Executive Homes ($280–$400K+)

Target audience: Managers, executives, relocating professionals
Size: 2,200–3,500 sq ft
Target: 50–100 units
Monthly mortgage: $1,850–$2,650+ (for $75K+ professionals)

Infrastructure Requirements

  • Roads: 5–7 miles of new/upgraded residential streets ($2–3M)
  • Utilities: Water, sewer, stormwater ($1.5–2M)
  • Schools: USD 490 capacity for 200–300 new students (assess expansion needs)
  • Parks/Recreation: Neighborhood green space, playground ($500K–$1M)
  • Commercial: Small retail, services, coffee shops to support residents ($500K–$1M)

Total municipal investment required: $5–8M (can be funded via housing development fees, EDA grants, CDBG funds)

Financing & Incentive Strategy

Developer Incentives

  • Tax abatement (5–10 years, 30–50%)
  • Infrastructure cost-sharing (roads, utilities)
  • Density bonuses (higher densities allowed)
  • Fee waivers (building permit, inspection fees)

Homebuyer Incentives

  • Down-payment assistance ($5K–$15K per home)
  • FDI-worker housing grants ($10K–$20K for relocating employees)
  • Closing cost assistance ($2K–$5K)
  • First-time homebuyer programs (low-interest loans)

Funding Sources

  • EDA Community Development Block Grant (CDBG): $1–2M (federal)
  • Kansas Housing Resources Board (KHRB): $500K–$1M
  • Private Developer Financing: Remaining $3–5M (with tax abatement)
  • Philanthropic Grants: $300–500K (community foundation grants)
  • Homebuyer Assistance Fund: $250–500K (city general fund)

Phase 2: South El Dorado Expansion (Years 3–7)

If Phase 1 succeeds and FDI momentum continues, expand to South El Dorado district with:

  • 300–500 additional units (continuation of mixed-income strategy)
  • Commercial district: Retail, dining, services (50K–100K sq ft)
  • Workforce housing clusters: Dedicated housing for specific FDI employers (e.g., precision ag tech jobs)
  • Recreation amenities: Community center, aquatic center, expanded parks

Critical Success Factors

🎯 Coordinate with FDI Recruitment

Break ground on Phase 1 housing BEFORE or SIMULTANEOUSLY with FDI LOI signing. Companies want assurance that housing will be ready for workforce.

🎯 Prioritize Youth/Young Families

Entry-level homes ($130–$160K) target the 25–35 age group that's leaving El Dorado. Affordable housing = better youth retention.

🎯 Partner with Developers

Tax abatement + infrastructure support makes North El Dorado attractive for regional builders. RFP process to select qualified developer.

🎯 Market Aggressively

Use housing development as competitive advantage in FDI pitches. Tell every prospect: "We're building workforce housing NOW to support your expansion."
