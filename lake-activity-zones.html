Lake Activity Zones: Current vs. Proposed

Strategic Recreation & Conservation Framework

Comprehensive zoning of El Dorado Lake activities: recreation, conservation, commercial use, infrastructure, seasonal capacity planning

Lake Zoning Strategy: Managing Multiple Uses

The Challenge

El Dorado Lake supports diverse, sometimes competing activities:

  • Recreation: Sailing, fishing, swimming, boating, paddling
  • Conservation: Wildlife habitat, prairie preservation, water quality
  • Commercial: Marinas, watersports rentals, camping, dining
  • Infrastructure: Water intake, wastewater discharge, emergency access
  • Challenge: Balance growth demand with environmental/safety constraints

📊 Lake Baseline

  • Lake size: 3,570 acres
  • Shoreline: ~28 miles
  • Current marinas: 2–3 (50–80 boat slips)
  • Annual visitors: ~40,000–50,000
  • Peak season: May–September (80% of annual visits)
  • Current zoning: Mixed use (no formal activity zones)

Zoning Framework: Five Activity Zones

  • Zone A: Water Sports: Sailing, windsurfing, paddleboarding focus
  • Zone B: Family Recreation: Swimming, fishing, casual boating
  • Zone C: Commercial: Marinas, watersports rentals, food service
  • Zone D: Conservation: Wildlife habitat, prairie preservation
  • Zone E: Infrastructure: Water intake, wastewater, emergency access, maintenance

Current Lake Use: Baseline Assessment

Sailing & Water Sports

  • Current: Main marina at north shore; sailboat racing on weekends
  • Demand: Growing; new paddleboarders and kayakers increasing annually
  • Season: May–September (peak); year-round potential with proper facilities

Fishing

  • Current: Bank fishing and boat fishing; channel catfish, walleye primary species
  • Infrastructure: 2–3 boat ramps; limited bank access
  • Demand: Stable; tournament fishing growing (regional appeal)

Family Recreation

  • Current: Swimming beach area; picnic facilities; playground
  • Capacity: ~500–800 daily visitors during peak season
  • Demand: High; limited parking/facilities creating bottlenecks

Conservation

  • Current: Limited formal conservation zones; wildlife habitat pressures
  • Concern: Recreational pressure on shoreline; erosion; nesting habitat disturbance

Proposed Lake Activity Zones: 15-Year Vision

Zone A: Water Sports (North & West Shore)

  • Primary use: Sailing, windsurfing, paddleboarding, kayaking
  • Current infrastructure: Marina (80 slips); boat ramp; parking
  • Proposed improvements:
    • Expand marina to 120–150 slips (add 50 slips)
    • Watersports rental fleet (kayaks, paddleboards, sailboat charters)
    • Watersports school (instruction, certification programs)
    • Designated sailing race course (marked buoys; weekend racing)
  • Capacity: 1,000–1,500 daily peak-season visitors

Zone B: Family Recreation (South & East Shore)

  • Primary use: Swimming, fishing, picnicking, family boating
  • Current infrastructure: Swimming beach; picnic areas; playground
  • Proposed improvements:
    • Expand beach area (seasonal capacity to 1,000+ visitors)
    • Modern restrooms, changing facilities, lifeguard station
    • Boat ramp improvements (wider, better surface, dedicated trailer parking)
    • Bank fishing access improvements (accessible piers, lighting)
    • Picnic pavilions, group gathering areas
  • Capacity: 2,000+ daily peak-season visitors

Zone C: Commercial Services (South Shore)

  • Primary use: Marina operations, watersports rentals, dining, retail
  • Current infrastructure: Marina store; limited food service
  • Proposed improvements:
    • Expand restaurant/cafe (outdoor waterfront dining)
    • Watersports equipment rental shop
    • Gift shop, outdoor retail (marine gear, sun protection, etc.)
    • RV park expansion (100–120 sites with hookups)
    • Cabin/glamping rentals (20–30 units)
  • Employment: 80–120 seasonal jobs

Zone D: Conservation (West & North Wildlife Areas)

  • Primary use: Wildlife habitat, prairie preservation, ecological function
  • Protections:
    • No development; nesting habitat closure (March–July)
    • Limited public access (designated trails only)
    • Water quality monitoring; invasive species management
    • Partnership with Kansas Parks & Wildlife
  • Education: Interpretive signage; guided nature walks

Zone E: Infrastructure (East Shore)

  • Primary use: Water intake, wastewater treatment, emergency access, maintenance
  • Infrastructure:
    • Water intake facilities (protected, no recreation)
    • Wastewater discharge site (meeting EPA standards)
    • Emergency boat launch (Fire, EMS, law enforcement)
    • Equipment storage (maintenance docks, vehicle parking)
  • Public access: Limited; restricted to authorized personnel

💡 Zoning Governance: Who Manages What?

  • City Parks/Recreation: Zone B (family rec), Zone C (commercial);
  • El Dorado Lake Authority: All zones (coordination, water quality, safety)
  • Kansas Parks & Wildlife: Zone D (conservation partnerships)
  • Public Works: Zone E (infrastructure management)

Seasonal Capacity & Infrastructure Planning

Peak Season (May–September) Capacity

  • Zone A (Water Sports): 1,000–1,500 daily visitors
  • Zone B (Family Rec): 2,000+ daily visitors
  • Zone C (Commercial): 200–300 employees/operators
  • Total peak capacity: 3,500–4,000 daily visitors
  • Parking requirement: ~400–500 spaces (distributed across zones)

Infrastructure Needs

  • Parking: 400–500 spaces (Zone B = 200, Zone A = 150, Zone C = 100)
  • Restrooms/Facilities: 4–6 modern facilities (ADA accessible)
  • Emergency Access: 3–4 boat launch points (EMS, Fire response)
  • Wastewater: Capacity planning for 5,000+ people peak occupancy
  • Water Quality: Monitoring station; invasive species management

Implementation: Governance & Timeline

Phase 1: Zoning Adoption (Years 1–2)

  • City & Lake Authority adopt formal lake zoning map
  • Clarify management roles (Parks, Lake Authority, K-Parks & Wildlife)
  • Begin infrastructure capacity assessment

Phase 2: Infrastructure Investment (Years 3–8)

  • Zone A: Marina expansion, watersports rental fleet
  • Zone B: Beach expansion, restroom facilities, improved boat ramps
  • Zone C: Commercial development (restaurant, RV park, cabin rentals)
  • Zone D: Conservation area protection; wildlife habitat restoration
  • Zone E: Water treatment upgrades; emergency access improvements

Phase 3: Management & Programming (Years 8–15)

  • Event programming (regattas, fishing tournaments, swimming races)
  • Conservation monitoring; adaptive management based on environmental data
  • Safety enforcement; emergency response protocols
  • Seasonal capacity management; visitor experience optimization

🎯 Year 1 Action Items

  1. El Dorado City Commission adopts Lake Activity Zones map
  2. Lake Authority convenes stakeholder committee (Parks, Parks & Wildlife, Marina operators)
  3. Begin infrastructure capacity planning study
  4. Develop conservation partnership agreement with K-Parks & Wildlife
