Strategic Framework for Post-2040 Prosperity

Vision 2040 is not an endpoint but a foundation. By 2040, El Dorado will have established diversified industry bases, modernized infrastructure, and a talented resident workforce. The 2040 Beyond framework extends this momentum by positioning El Dorado as a model for sustainable, innovation-driven regional leadership in the Great Plains—a place where climate adaptation, technological advancement, and human flourishing align. The post-2040 era demands that El Dorado think beyond incremental growth toward transformational resilience and competitive advantage in a rapidly changing global economy.

🌱

Sustainability First

By 2050, El Dorado operates at net-zero carbon emissions in city operations and incentivizes carbon-neutral growth. Water stewardship, renewable energy, and regenerative agriculture define the regional economy. El Dorado becomes a model for sustainable rural development in America's heartland.

🚀

Innovation Leadership

El Dorado hosts a thriving ecosystem of research institutions, innovation labs, and emerging technology ventures. From autonomous agriculture to precision manufacturing to clean energy, the city is a testing ground for technologies that define the 21st-century economy.

🌍

Global Connectivity

Despite its rural Kansas location, El Dorado is digitally and logistically integrated into global value chains. Advanced telecommunications, logistics infrastructure, and talent drawn from worldwide networks make the city competitive in international markets.

Growth is designed to benefit all generations: young people find opportunity and can build wealth; working-age adults have competitive wages and career pathways; elders have dignity, healthcare, and meaningful community participation. No demographic is left behind.

Learning Economy

💪

Community Resilience

Social networks, civic participation, and mutual aid define El Dorado's character. The city is prepared for shocks—climate disruption, economic displacement, technological disruption—because residents have built deep relationships and institutions of trust.

Adaptation Strategy

Climate Resilience: Building for 2050 Weather

El Dorado's location in central Kansas will experience more volatile weather patterns by 2050: more intense summer heat, more extreme rainfall events, longer droughts interspersed with flooding. Vision 2040 Beyond positions the city to thrive despite these changes through adaptive infrastructure, regenerative practices, and proactive planning.

💧

Water Resilience Infrastructure

Goal by 2050: El Dorado achieves 80% water independence through rainwater harvesting, aquifer recharge, and wastewater reclamation. Key initiatives: (1) City-wide green infrastructure program—permeable pavements, bioswales, and retention ponds in every district reduce flooding and recharge groundwater; (2) El Dorado Lake is positioned as a resilience reserve, supplying irrigation and industrial needs during droughts while supporting recreation and wildlife during wet periods; (3) Walnut River corridor is restored as a functioning floodplain ecosystem rather than a constrained channel, reducing downstream flood risk to Wichita while creating habitat and recreation; (4) Every new development includes water neutrality requirements—rainfall captured on-site must balance site water demand. Implementation: $45M public + private investment over 15 years. Lead agency: Walnut River Watershed Partnership + El Dorado City Government.

Phase 4–5 (2040–2055) · Budget: $45M total
🌿

Urban Forest & Cooling Strategy

Goal by 2050: Triple El Dorado's tree canopy coverage from ~12% to 35% of the city, creating cooler neighborhoods that reduce heat-island effect and lower cooling costs. Key initiatives: (1) Street tree program plants 10,000 native trees, prioritizing east-side neighborhoods and industrial corridors; (2) Incentive program for private property tree planting—offer tax credits for landowners who establish native tree groves; (3) Rooftop garden and green roof mandate on all new commercial and multifamily development—reduces building cooling loads and absorbs stormwater; (4) Walnut River linear park and trail system becomes El Dorado's "cool corridor," shaded by native trees and accessible to all neighborhoods. Implementation: $12M over 15 years. Lead agency: City Parks + USD 490 + Chamber of Commerce.

Phase 3–5 (2035–2055) · Budget: $12M total
☀️

100% Renewable Energy Transition

Goal by 2050: El Dorado operates on 100% renewable energy—municipal operations (water, power, vehicles) plus a grid-wide commitment to wind and solar. Key initiatives: (1) City-led renewable energy procurement—El Dorado signs long-term PPAs with Kansas wind and solar operators to supply city facilities and residents; (2) Distributed solar deployment—rooftop solar on public buildings, community solar gardens accessible to renters, and an open rooftop marketplace where residents and businesses can sell excess generation back to the grid; (3) Energy storage—battery and thermal storage systems stabilize intermittent renewable supply; (4) Electric vehicle transition—by 2050, all city vehicles and transit are electric, and EV charging stations are ubiquitous in public parking; (5) Energy efficiency mandate—all buildings built or substantially renovated after 2035 meet Passive House standards, reducing heating and cooling demand by 75%. Implementation: $80M public + private over 20 years. Lead agency: City Utilities + Kansas Energy Council.

Phase 4–5 (2040–2060) · Budget: $80M total
🌾

Regenerative Agriculture Ecosystem

Goal by 2050: El Dorado's surrounding agricultural region becomes a model for regenerative practices—rebuilding soil carbon, increasing biodiversity, and improving farmer profitability while sequestering atmospheric carbon. Key initiatives: (1) Regional carbon farming cooperative—farmers are paid for carbon credits generated by cover-cropping, reduced tillage, and crop rotation practices; (2) Agritech innovation hub—startup incubation and university research focused on soil health, precision ag, and climate-resilient crop varieties based in El Dorado; (3) Direct-to-consumer food system—El Dorado's farmers markets and farm-to-table restaurants create premium markets for regenerative crops; (4) Soil health testing infrastructure—accessible soil labs where farmers can test carbon, biology, and nutrient status and receive custom recommendations. Implementation: $25M public + $60M private over 15 years. Lead agency: Butler CC + K-State Research + El Dorado Inc.

Phase 4–5 (2040–2055) · Budget: $85M total
🛡️

Disaster Resilience Planning

Goal by 2050: El Dorado's critical infrastructure, supply chains, and community support systems are hardened against climate shocks—extreme heat events, severe weather, and drought. Key initiatives: (1) Cooling centers and emergency shelters in every district with backup power and water; (2) Critical infrastructure resilience review—hospitals, water treatment, power, communications audited for climate risk and upgraded with redundancy; (3) Community early warning system—real-time data on heat, flooding, and air quality delivered to all residents via SMS, app, and community radio; (4) Regional mutual aid network—El Dorado coordinates with Butler County communities on resource sharing during emergencies; (5) Indigenous knowledge integration—El Dorado partners with tribal nations on traditional ecological knowledge that informed adaptation in Kansas for millennia. Implementation: $15M over 15 years. Lead agency: FEMA + El Dorado Emergency Management + Kansas Division of Emergency Management.

Phase 3–5 (2035–2055) · Budget: $15M total

Regional Strategy 2045–2055

Key infrastructure: (1) Regional warehouse and distribution hub serving a 5-state region; (2) Multimodal terminal (rail + truck + air) coordinating cargo movement; (3) Last-mile delivery network for e-commerce and rural logistics; (4) Autonomous vehicle testing corridor—I-35 to I-49 becomes an AV testbed region. El Dorado's geography—equidistant from Kansas City, Tulsa, and Colorado—makes it a natural regional distribution center. Target: $1B+ in logistics and supply chain investment; 1,000+ logistics jobs.

🏥

Regional Medical & Wellness Hub

By 2050: El Dorado's Susan B. Allen Memorial Hospital and expanded Butler Community College serve as the health and wellness anchor for the entire region. Expansion includes: (1) Specialty surgical centers (orthopedics, cardiac, cancer); (2) Rural health clinic network across Butler County and neighboring counties; (3) Behavioral health campus (addiction treatment, mental health, psychiatric services); (4) Senior living and long-term care (100+ facilities); (5) Sports medicine and wellness centers. Investment: $200M+ over 15 years. Target: 1,500+ healthcare jobs; serving 150,000+ residents across 5-county region.

🎓

Regional Education & Workforce Development

By 2050: USD 490 and Butler Community College coordinate to create a world-class education pipeline for the entire region. Key initiatives: (1) K-12 Excellence—USD 490 becomes a model district for rural excellence; satellite schools established in smaller Butler County towns; (2) Early Childhood Centers—accessible pre-K and daycare in every Butler County community; (3) 4-Year University Campus—Butler CC transitions to a full regional university offering engineering, ag tech, and health programs; (4) Workforce Development—continuous training programs for manufacturing, healthcare, agriculture, technology; (5) Lifelong Learning—community colleges offering skills training from age 5 to 85. Long-Term Viability 2040–2100 Vision

Sustainability Framework: Regenerative Growth Model

Vision 2040 Beyond advances a regenerative growth model—one where economic growth actively improves environmental and social systems rather than depleting them. El Dorado by 2050 leaves the region healthier, more prosperous, more connected, and more resilient than it was in 2026.

Environmental Sustainability

Net Positive Outcomes by 2050: (1) Carbon—El Dorado operates at net-zero carbon emissions in city operations; surrounding region sequesters more carbon than it emits through regenerative agriculture and forest restoration; (2) Water—groundwater levels are rising, not falling; water quality is improving through reduced pollution; (3) Biodiversity—native species populations are recovering; habitat connectivity is improving; (4) Soil—soil organic matter is increasing; erosion has stopped; soil health is visibly improving; (5) Air quality—cleaner than 2026 due to eliminated fossil fuel use and reduced industrial emissions.

Economic Sustainability

Inclusive, Distributed Prosperity by 2050: (1) Wage growth—median household income in El Dorado exceeds state and national averages; wealth distribution is more equitable; (2) Job quality—jobs offer benefits, career growth, and dignity; not just subsistence wages; (3) Small business—locally owned businesses thrive; 50% of retail and service sectors are locally owned; (4) Cooperative enterprises—worker ownership increases to 15–20% of economy; (5) Wealth building—homeownership, small business ownership, and investment participation are accessible to all income levels; no community is systematically excluded from wealth-building opportunities.

Social Sustainability

Thriving Communities by 2050: (1) Health—life expectancy increases; chronic disease rates decrease; mental health and substance abuse services are accessible and effective; (2) Education—K-12 graduation rates exceed 95%; higher education participation exceeds 70%; (3) Civic participation—75%+ of adults volunteer, vote, or participate in community leadership; (4) Diversity & inclusion—all demographic groups are represented in leadership; disparities in outcomes by race, gender, and socioeconomic status have narrowed significantly; (5) Trust—community members trust each other and institutions; civic engagement is high; conflict is resolved constructively; (6) Family stability—family incomes are sufficient; housing is affordable; childcare is accessible; single parents can thrive.

Long-Term Impact

Multigenerational Legacy: What We Leave Behind

Vision 2040 Beyond is ultimately about what El Dorado leaves to future generations. Not just economic assets but environmental health, civic institutions, human relationships, and spiritual inheritance. By 2050 and beyond, El Dorado has built something that lasts—a model for how rural communities can thrive in the 21st century.

For Young People: Opportunity & Home

By 2050, El Dorado youth do not have to leave to find opportunity. Whether your passion is agriculture, technology, healthcare, arts, or business, there are pathways to meaningful work with competitive wages and career growth. The city invests in your education and development. Homeownership is achievable on professional salaries. You can start a business without moving to a metropolis. You can build a life and a family in your hometown. Some will still choose to leave and explore—that's healthy. But those who stay or return are not making a sacrifice; they're choosing a place that offers opportunity, community, and quality of life.

For Working-Age Adults: Dignity & Growth

El Dorado's economy by 2050 offers middle-class wages for skilled work across many sectors. You can support a family on one income if you choose. Benefits—healthcare, retirement, paid time off—are standard, not luxury. There are clear pathways for advancement and skill development. Wage growth keeps pace with productivity and cost of living. If you lose a job, the community supports retraining and transition. Entrepreneurs are not just tolerated but celebrated. By 2050, work in El Dorado means dignity, security, and the possibility of building wealth.

For Elders: Respect & Integration

By 2050, aging in El Dorado means choice, respect, and continued contribution. You can age in your own home with support services available. Senior housing options range from active communities to assisted living to memory care. Healthcare is accessible and affordable. But more importantly, you are not isolated or invisible. The community values your experience and wisdom. There are mentorship programs, lifelong learning opportunities, and volunteer roles where you contribute meaningfully. You can be with grandchildren and extended family. El Dorado honors its elders and creates space for them to flourish in their final chapters.

For All Communities: Equity & Belonging

By 2050, El Dorado is genuinely welcoming to people of all racial and ethnic backgrounds, faiths, gender identities, and national origins. Representation in leadership and opportunities matches community demographics. Disparities in income, health, education, and housing have narrowed significantly. Historic discrimination is acknowledged and repaired. The city actively recruits and retains talent from communities that have been excluded from rural opportunity. Immigrants and their children feel at home and can build lives and legacies. By 2050, diversity strengthens El Dorado—culturally, economically, and morally.

Environmental Inheritance: A Healed Landscape

The greatest gift El Dorado can leave future generations is a healed, thriving natural environment. By 2050, the Walnut River is clean and flowing, supporting fisheries and recreation. Groundwater levels are stable or rising. Soil is alive—rich with microorganisms, storing carbon, holding water. Native plants and animals have returned. The air is clean. The landscape that sustained El Dorado in the past sustains it in the future. This is not nostalgia; it's regeneration. El Dorado proves that human prosperity and environmental health are not opposites but partners.

Institutional Legacy: Knowledge & Capability

El Dorado by 2050 has built institutions—research centers, educational systems, cultural organizations, civic networks—that continue generating knowledge and capability for generations. These institutions are endowed and sustainable. They attract talent and capital. They partner with global networks. But they are rooted in El Dorado and accountable to the community. They exist not as extractive forces but as generative forces—creating knowledge, training the next generation, solving problems. This institutional capital is as valuable as financial capital and more lasting.

Civic Legacy: Relationships & Trust

The deepest legacy is relational. By 2050, El Dorado is a place where people know each other and trust each other. Neighbors help neighbors. Civic institutions work. People participate. Conflict is navigated constructively. Newcomers are welcomed. Relationships transcend demographic lines. This relational wealth—built through decades of intentional community building—is the most resilient asset a city can have. It survives recessions, climate disruption, and technological change. El Dorado's legacy is a community that works because people are invested in each other's flourishing.

Model for the World: A Proof of Concept

Perhaps most importantly, El Dorado by 2050 is a living proof of concept. It demonstrates that rural communities can thrive in the 21st century. That prosperity is possible in places far from major metros. That communities can be economically dynamic while environmentally regenerative. That diversity strengthens rather than threatens community. That technology can serve human flourishing rather than replacing it. Students, leaders, and innovators from around the world come to El Dorado to learn and replicate what's been built. The city becomes a beacon—proof that another rural future is possible, practical, and desirable.
