Competitive Intelligence Report 2026

El Dorado's competitive positioning relative to similar-sized Kansas and Oklahoma cities, regional economic trends affecting development strategy, and opportunities for differentiation in the I-35 corridor and Wichita MSA.

Scope: Primary competitors: Emporia (KS), Dodge City (KS), Salina (KS), McPherson (KS), Ponca City (OK), Hutchinson (KS). Secondary analysis: Newton (KS), Wichita sprawl dynamics. Research date: June 2026.

Executive Summary: The Competitive Landscape

El Dorado's Position

El Dorado sits in a surprisingly strong competitive position relative to similar-sized Kansas towns, despite smaller population (12,500 vs. 13K–47K competitors). The city's AMAZONE FDI win (Dec. 2025) proves El Dorado can attract German foreign investment — a rare accomplishment in rural Kansas. However, competitors like Emporia, Dodge City, and Salina are actively recruiting in overlapping sectors, and Wichita's economic gravity continues to pull investment northward.

Key Finding: El Dorado's hidden advantages (water, I-35 direct access, FTZ #28, lake tourism) are severely undermarketed. Competitors are winning FDI deals not because they have better locations, but because they have better storytelling and more aggressive international recruitment.

Detailed Competitor Analysis

Emporia, KS — The Animal Health Corridor

Population: ~24,500
County: Lyon | County Seat
Distance from El Dorado: ~80 mi north (I-35)
Primary Industries: Animal Health, Ag Processing, Manufacturing
Major Employer(s): Merck Animal Health, Hills Pet Nutrition, Diamond Pet
Recent FDI/Win: Natrium Reactor (Oct. 2025, endorsement)
Competitive Strengths
  • Animal Health Corridor dominance: >50% of worldwide animal health diagnostics and pet food manufactured in Emporia region
  • RDA leadership: Emporia RDA has created $1B+ capital investment and 2,800+ jobs since founding
  • Shovel-ready sites: 300 acres along I-35/I-335, well-positioned for recruitment
  • University partnership: Emporia State University provides workforce pipeline and research
  • Regional distribution hub: Direct access to Dallas–Minneapolis corridor via I-35
Competitive Weaknesses & Threats
  • Leadership transition: RDA hired external CEO search firm (July 2025) — suggests internal turnover/strategy shift
  • Animal health sector consolidation: Merck, Hills, Diamond all major employers, but pet food/pharma sector is mature
  • No water advantage: Unlike El Dorado, Emporia has no unique water rights; relies on municipal supply
  • Manufacturing diversification gap: Heavily dependent on ag/animal health; lacks aerospace or data center play
Threat to El Dorado?
MODERATE THREAT. Emporia is successful at attracting mid-market manufacturing and ag-related FDI, and it's a major regional hub. However, Emporia's focus on animal health is not directly competitive with El Dorado's (refining, advanced manufacturing, aerospace supply, data centers). The Natrium Reactor proposal (advanced nuclear) is strategic positioning by Emporia but requires federal approval and is speculative. Emporia is not recruiting in El Dorado's target sectors aggressively.

Dodge City, KS — Agricultural Processing Powerhouse

Population: ~26,000
County: Ford | County Seat
Distance from El Dorado: ~125 mi west (US-54)
Primary Industries: Meat Processing, Food Manufacturing, Dairy
Major Employer(s): Hilmar Cheese, Tyson Foods, Cargill
Recent FDI/Win: Hilmar Cheese ($600M, Oct. 2024, 270 jobs)
Competitive Strengths
  • Hilmar Cheese mega-win: $600M investment, 270 direct jobs. Largest single investment in western Kansas in recent years
  • Food & ag processing clustering: Tyson Foods, Cargill, plus Hilmar create ecosystem advantage for supply chain
  • Water infrastructure upgrade: City is expanding South Wastewater Treatment Plant to support Hilmar — major capex commitment
  • FTZ eligibility: Part of broader Kansas FTZ network; tariff advantages for food imports
  • Housing development pipeline: MIH and KHITC awards for workforce housing (OMA dev., United Village)
  • Downtown revitalization: $13M Downtown Streetscape Project — aggressive place-making
Competitive Weaknesses & Threats
  • Food processing sector vulnerability: Highly cyclical, dependent on commodity prices and trade policy
  • Water constraint risk: Expanding wastewater treatment for one facility exposes city to over-reliance on single tenant
  • Geographic disadvantage: Western Kansas location removes Dodge City from I-35 corridor logistics; isolated supply chain access
  • Population stagnation despite FDI: $600M Hilmar investment hasn't yet translated to sustained population growth
  • Limited workforce skilled labor pool: Meat processing/cheese manufacturing require less advanced skills than aerospace or high-tech mfg
Threat to El Dorado?
MODERATE-TO-HIGH THREAT. Dodge City has executed one of the largest FDI wins in rural Kansas in the past decade. The Hilmar Cheese deal proves Dodge City can win mega-investment. However, Dodge City and El Dorado target different sectors: Dodge focuses on food/ag processing; El Dorado targets advanced manufacturing, aerospace supply, refining, data centers. Dodge City's western location is a disadvantage for El Dorado's target FDI (German, Japanese, Korean companies prefer I-35 corridor access). Dodge City's success may also trigger increased state/federal attention to rural development, which could benefit El Dorado if El Dorado's story is told effectively.

Competitive Weaknesses & Threats
  • K-AIRES timing uncertainty: Construction began Aug. 2024, opening slated for 2026, but no confirmed opening date or operational status available

HIGH THREAT (SECTOR-SPECIFIC). Salina is the closest competitor for aerospace supply chain recruitment. K-AIRES and the expanded K-State Salina presence will make Salina more attractive to aerospace suppliers and aerospace-adjacent manufacturing companies. However, Salina's advantage is aerospace education/R&D, not industrial manufacturing. El Dorado can compete for aerospace suppliers by emphasizing: (1) I-35 direct access (vs. Salina's I-70 position), (2) lower land/labor costs, (3) refining infrastructure (important for advanced materials), and (4) FDI proof-of-concept (AMAZONE). Salina is a threat to El Dorado's "aerospace supply satellite" positioning but not to broader industrial recruitment.

El Dorado's AMAZONE win is a strategic advantage over McPherson in FDI recruitment. Key differentiator: El Dorado has water + lake tourism + I-35 direct access; McPherson has neither. El Dorado can win the regional "advanced manufacturing hub" competition if El Dorado invests in workforce development as aggressively as McPherson.

Regional Economic Trends 2025–2027

1. I-35 Corridor Logistics Boom (El Dorado's Strategic Advantage)

Finding: The I-35 corridor through Kansas is emerging as a critical freight, manufacturing, and logistics backbone. Federal data shows 37,800 freight generators within 3 miles of I-35, employing 713,048 people. Within 10 miles: 65,469 generators, 1.2+ million jobs.

  • BNSF Intermodal & Logistics Park (Newton, KS): Expected 17,000 vehicle trips/day; 7,000 commercial vehicles — game-changing logistics infrastructure opening 2025–2026
  • Kansas City Area Data Centers: 10+ hyperscale data center projects now operational or in development (Johnson County, KS area) — signals massive AI/cloud infrastructure investment flowing toward I-35
  • Food & Specialty Manufacturing: >1,000 private carriers, 350 intrastate for-hire carriers, 9,500+ licensed Kansas motor carriers — consolidated distribution corridor

Implication for El Dorado: El Dorado sits directly on I-35 (Exits 71/76), not on a bypass. This is a major advantage vs. Emporia (I-35/I-335 junction requires detour), Salina (off-corridor), Ponca City (off-corridor). El Dorado can position itself as a manufacturing + logistics node within the emerging I-35 mega-corridor. AMAZONE chose El Dorado partly for I-35 access.

2. Kansas BEAD Broadband Rollout (Critical for Data Center Recruitment)

Finding: Kansas BEAD program approved for $166.6 million (Dec. 2025, NTIA). Groundbreaking expected H2 2026. Twin Valley broadband alone investing $12.3M (private $4.7M + grants $7.6M) across rural Kansas in 2026.

  • Coverage: 26,673 eligible households/businesses across Kansas will receive fiber or fixed wireless access
  • Timeline: Deployment throughout 2026–2027; construction timelines vary by community
  • Co-investment model: State grants matched with private capital — public-private partnership approach
  • Regional equity focus: Eight rural counties receiving priority; Butler County (where El Dorado is seat) is likely included

Implication for El Dorado: Broadband is now a credible infrastructure story. Data centers require gigabit-speed connectivity; BEAD funding makes El Dorado a legitimate data center recruitment candidate. Remote worker attraction also becomes plausible. This is a 2–3 year advantage; once most rural Kansas has BEAD fiber, competitive advantage diminishes unless El Dorado pushes for "gigabit-ready data center zone" designation.

3. Data Center Water Requirements — Kansas as Attractive Alternative (El Dorado's Unique Advantage)

Finding: U.S. data centers consumed 17–20 billion gallons of water in 2024–2025 (direct cooling). Projections suggest 4x growth by 2030 as AI workloads scale. Kansas is addressing water concerns with legislation (SB 400 proposal: closed-loop cooling systems, municipal injunction rights).

  • Meta Kansas City precedent: Meta's large data center required $500M+ water recycling investment to secure permits — showing water is a deal-breaker issue
  • Kansas SB 98 incentives: 20-year sales tax exemption for data centers ($250M+ investment, 20+ jobs)
  • Closed-loop vs. evaporative cooling: Industry transitioning away from water-intensive evaporative cooling in water-stressed regions; air-cooled systems can achieve zero freshwater consumption
  • Legislative momentum: Kansas lawmakers proactively addressing data center water regulation — signals state commitment to responsible data center recruitment

Implication for El Dorado: THIS IS EL DORADO'S BIGGEST HIDDEN ADVANTAGE. El Dorado owns 23M gallons/day water capacity (unique among Kansas cities). City has contract with USACE for El Dorado Lake water rights — a 50+ year security advantage. For a data center seeking 100M+ gallons/year, El Dorado can offer unique water security that competitors cannot. No other Kansas town has this asset. This should be El Dorado's lead message in data center recruitment. "Only Kansas city with owned water rights. Zero worry about municipal water constraints."

4. Wichita Economic Gravity — Spillover Effect for Satellite Communities

Finding: Wichita (650K+ metro) is the economic engine of central Kansas. Wichita's downtown revitalization plan and regional economic development strategy signal continued growth momentum. However, Wichita is also experiencing increasing land costs, congestion, and workforce competition.

  • Downtown Wichita Action Plan: $X+ investment in downtown infill, cultural assets, neighborhood connections — signals Wichita is maturing downtown rather than expanding suburbs
  • Aerospace sector consolidation: Major Wichita aerospace/defense contractors (Textron, Spirit AeroSystems, Raytheon) face land/labor cost pressures
  • Labor shortage signals: Wichita metro unemployment low; competition for skilled workers intensifying
  • Satellite town opportunity: Companies seeking to decentralize manufacturing, logistics, or secondary operations are looking to satellite communities 30–60 mi from Wichita

Implication for El Dorado: El Dorado (29 mi south of Wichita) is perfectly positioned as a "Wichita aerospace supply satellite" — close enough for daily commutes or easy distribution, far enough to have lower land/labor costs. Target aerospace suppliers, logistics companies, and secondary manufacturing operations that need to be near Wichita's aerospace corridor but can't afford Wichita's real estate. Market positioning: "Aerospace supply hub within Wichita MSA, at 1/3 the cost."

5. Ag Tech & Advanced Manufacturing Sector Growth (Kansas State Focus)

Finding: Kansas Department of Commerce is actively promoting advanced manufacturing, ag tech, and specialty food processing as growth sectors. AMAZONE's El Dorado location (German agricultural machinery) is aligned with state development strategy.

  • State pipeline: K-State Salina (aerospace), K-State main campus (ag research), Emporia State (animal health) provide workforce + R&D infrastructure
  • FTZ network: Kansas has active FTZ infrastructure; FTZ #28 covers Butler County — customs duty savings are significant for agricultural equipment manufacturing
  • International recruitment: German companies (AMAZONE model) are increasingly interested in U.S. presence; Japan (JETRO), South Korea (KOTRA) actively recruiting U.S. sites

Implication for El Dorado: El Dorado's AMAZONE win proves El Dorado can compete for international agricultural equipment/machinery companies. Leverage AMAZONE as a proof point in recruitment messaging to JETRO (Japan), GTAI (Germany), KOTRA (South Korea). Agricultural machinery is less sensitive to I-35 logistics than data centers, but it's a growing sector with foreign investment appetite.

6. Rural Housing Shortage — Workforce Constraint Emerging

Finding: Multiple competitor cities report workforce housing shortages: Dodge City (MIH/KHITC awards for 22+ new homes), McPherson (40 homes via federal grant), Hutchinson (rural communities cooperating on housing). This signals a regional constraint.

  • Workforce housing gap: Many rural Kansas towns have 1980s–1990s housing stock; new construction lagging population/employment growth
  • Affordability pressure: Moderate-income households (80–120% AMI) struggling to find rentals or purchase homes
  • State response: Kansas launched MIH (Moderate Income Housing) and KHITC (Kansas Housing Investor Tax Credit) programs to incentivize new construction

Implication for El Dorado: Housing is a critical success factor for FDI recruitment. El Dorado's housing development strategy (North El Dorado district, workforce rental, senior living, manufactured homes) directly addresses this competitive constraint. Companies like AMAZONE choose locations partly on workforce housing availability. El Dorado's housing story — 1,100+ new units planned — is a competitive advantage vs. cities that haven't addressed housing.

El Dorado's Competitive Advantages (Undermarketed)

1. Unique Water Rights & Capacity (No Competitor Has This)

Fact: El Dorado is the ONLY Kansas city that owns water rights (contract with USACE). 23M gallons/day capacity, 8.5B gallons/year additional storage capacity.

Why it matters:

  • Data center recruitment: Water security is deal-maker/breaker; El Dorado is the only Kansas city with owned rights (no municipal supply uncertainty)
  • Advanced manufacturing: Energy-intensive industries (refining, chemical, advanced materials) require reliable water supply
  • Regional water authority revenue: El Dorado can export water to Butler County and beyond — strategic asset for 40-year revenue stream
  • Climate resilience: Owned rights = immunity from state/federal water rationing or allocation changes

Competitor comparison: Emporia, Dodge City, Salina, McPherson, Ponca City all rely on municipal supply or groundwater. None have owned rights. None can guarantee long-term water availability. El Dorado can.

Marketing action: Lead every FDI pitch with water. "Only Kansas city with owned water rights. Zero supply uncertainty. Unique advantage for data centers, manufacturing, ag processing."

2. I-35 Direct Node (Not a Bypass)

Fact: El Dorado has direct I-35 exits (71, 76). Not a bypass town. Direct access to primary NAFTA trade corridor (Dallas–Minneapolis).

Why it matters:

  • Logistics hub positioning: BNSF Intermodal Park (Newton, 30 mi north) + El Dorado node = seamless supply chain
  • FDI recruitment: German, Japanese, Korean companies prioritize I-35 corridor access. Exits 71/76 are valuable real estate.
  • Manufacturing clustering: Suppliers need quick access to Wichita (29 mi), Topeka (120 mi), Kansas City (200 mi). I-35 direct = competitive advantage
  • Bypass traffic monetization: 20,000+ vehicles/day pass El Dorado; EV charging, rest stops, truck services create secondary economy

Competitor comparison:

  • Emporia: I-35/I-335 junction — requires detour from corridor; less direct
  • Salina: Off I-35 (on I-70); 90+ min to I-35 node. Not corridor-adjacent
  • McPherson: Off I-35 (on US-54); similar distance disadvantage
  • Ponca City: On I-35 but south of El Dorado; part of different regional cluster

Marketing action: "Direct I-35 access between Wichita and Kansas City. Exits 71/76. BNSF-connected. Fastest route from Mexico to Minneapolis."

3. AMAZONE FDI Win — Proof of Concept for International Recruitment

Fact: December 2025, AMAZONE (German agricultural machinery company, founded 1883) opened 30,000 sq ft facility in El Dorado. 50K+ sq ft expansion plans announced. Employees 100+ (and growing).

Why it matters:

  • Proof of concept: El Dorado CAN attract German FDI. This is the hardest win to get; once you have it, subsequent German companies view El Dorado as "proven" location
  • Network effect: AMAZONE brings supply chain, logistics, and subsidiary services. Other German ag equipment companies (John Deere parts, Claas, Lemken) may follow
  • Story asset: No competitor city has a comparable recent FDI win. El Dorado's AMAZONE story is unique in Kansas (vs. Dodge City's Hilmar, Emporia's Natrium proposal)
  • Workforce signal: AMAZONE is actively hiring — visible job creation proves El Dorado's workforce pipeline works

Competitor comparison:

  • Dodge City: Hilmar Cheese (2024) is larger ($600M), but agricultural processing, not advanced manufacturing
  • Emporia: Natrium Reactor (Oct. 2025) is speculative (needs federal approval), not operational
  • Salina: K-AIRES is K-State expansion, not foreign company recruitment
  • McPherson, Ponca City: No recent FDI wins announced

Marketing action: Feature AMAZONE in every FDI pitch. "German company chose El Dorado for U.S. operations. Operational today. Expanding. Named Germany's Most Innovative Medium-Sized Company (2024–2025)."

4. FTZ #28 Enrollment — Customs Duty Savings (Severely Undermarketed)

Fact: Butler County (where El Dorado is seat) is enrolled in Foreign Trade Zone #28. Allows duty-free importation + re-export of goods.

Why it matters:

  • Cost advantage: Manufacturing companies importing components or raw materials save 5–15% tariff costs via FTZ enrollment
  • Competitive positioning: Companies choosing between El Dorado and non-FTZ locations save hundreds of thousands annually
  • Undermarketed advantage: Most site selectors don't know about FTZ. El Dorado can use as a surprise competitive win at late stage in FDI negotiations
  • Supply chain benefit: AMAZONE imports agricultural machinery parts from Germany; FTZ enrollment lowers their import costs

Competitor comparison: Some competitor cities (Emporia, Dodge City) may have FTZ access through their respective county networks, but FTZ #28's centrality to manufacturing is a key advantage. Not all Kansas cities actively promote FTZ benefits.

Marketing action: Add to every site selector pitch: "Enrolled in Foreign Trade Zone #28. Duty-free importation, re-export, assembly. Typical savings: $200K–$500K annually for manufacturing companies."

5. #1 State Park — El Dorado Lake State Park Gets 1M+ Visitors/Year

Fact: KDWP confirmed El Dorado State Park is the #1 most-visited Kansas state park, "by a landslide" — 1M+ annual visitors.

Why it matters:

  • Tourism asset: 1M+ visitors/year = massive unmet demand for dining, lodging, retail. El Dorado's 26-venue lake activation strategy captures this demand
  • Economic multiplier: State park visitors spend $150–$300/day in local economy (hotels, restaurants, attractions). Estimated $75M–$150M annual impact if El Dorado captures 30% of visitor spending
  • Place-making narrative: "Largest state park in Kansas attracts 1M+ visitors/year. El Dorado city is underselling this asset. Lake activation is low-hanging fruit."
  • Workforce attraction: Young professionals, families, remote workers choose places with outdoor recreation. El Dorado has #1 state park as a quality-of-life draw

Competitor comparison: No competitor city has a comparable state park asset. Emporia, Dodge City, Salina, McPherson, Ponca City have recreational opportunities but not a #1-ranked state park on their doorstep.

Marketing action: "El Dorado is home to Kansas's #1 state park, visited by 1M+ annually. City is positioned to capture tourism economic impact through lake-side dining, hotels, water sports, event hosting."

6. Refining Infrastructure & Energy Supply (HF Sinclair Asset)

Fact: HF Sinclair refinery (135,000 bbl/day, 400 employees) is one of largest in Kansas. Provides stable energy supply, petrochemical feedstock, skilled workforce.

Why it matters:

  • Supply chain clustering: Refineries attract downstream manufacturing (lubricants, greases, specialty chemicals, polymers). El Dorado can pursue "energy-adjacent" manufacturing
  • Energy cost advantage: Refinery waste heat can be captured for district energy systems. Industrial customers benefit from lower energy costs
  • Workforce ecosystem: Refinery employees have technical skills transferable to advanced manufacturing, maintenance, engineering roles
  • Infrastructure maturity: Refinery infrastructure (rail, utilities, storage) is available for co-use by related industries

Competitor comparison: McPherson has a 100K bbl/day refinery (ConocoPhillips), but El Dorado's HF Sinclair is slightly larger and has stronger growth trajectory. Emporia, Dodge City, Salina have no refining presence.

Marketing action: Target petrochemical, specialty lubricant, and energy-adjacent manufacturing. "Refining infrastructure, skilled workforce, energy supply security."

7. No Business License Fees + 72-Hour Permit Guarantee

Fact: El Dorado charges no business license fees (competitive advantage over most Kansas cities). Implementing 72-hour permit guarantee would be a market differentiator.

Why it matters:

  • Cost advantage: Eliminates recurring business license costs (often $100–$500+/year in competitor cities)
  • Speed to market: 72-hour permit guarantee = faster startup, faster expansion. Wichita takes 30–60 days; El Dorado can offer 72 hours
  • Business-friendly signal: Demonstrates city commitment to reducing regulatory friction

Competitor comparison: Dodge City, Hutchinson, Salina may have comparable policies, but it's not widely marketed. El Dorado can own this positioning.

Marketing action: "No business license fees. 72-hour permit turnaround guarantee. Fastest startup time in Kansas."

Competitive Threats & Regional Risks

Threat 1: Dodge City's Agricultural Processing Dominance

Hilmar Cheese ($600M investment, 270 jobs) is a watershed moment for rural Kansas development. It proves that mega-investments ARE possible in small towns. However, Hilmar is specific to food/agricultural processing — a sector where El Dorado is not competitive. The threat is not direct sector competition but rather state/federal attention being diverted to Dodge City, potentially reducing resources available for other rural development.

Mitigation: Frame El Dorado's AMAZONE win as equally significant (though different sector). Target advanced manufacturing, aerospace, data centers — sectors where Dodge City has no advantage.

Threat 2: Salina's Aerospace Supply Ecosystem

K-AIRES (K-State expansion, opening 2026) positions Salina as an aerospace R&D/education hub. For El Dorado's "aerospace supply satellite" positioning, Salina becomes a direct competitor. Aerospace suppliers may choose Salina (larger city, K-State workforce, established aerospace presence) over El Dorado.

Mitigation: Emphasize El Dorado's I-35 direct access, lower land/labor costs, water security, refining infrastructure. Market as "advanced manufacturing satellite to Salina/Wichita aerospace complex" rather than competing head-to-head with Salina. Partner with Salina rather than compete (suppliers can source from both).

Threat 3: Wichita Gravity — Brain Drain, Worker Migration

Wichita's downtown revitalization and aerospace sector growth create powerful economic gravity. Young professionals and skilled workers migrate toward larger labor markets (more jobs, higher salaries, more cultural options). El Dorado risks losing talent to Wichita as opportunities emerge.

Mitigation: Invest heavily in quality of life: lake tourism, downtown activation, cultural events, remote work infrastructure. Emphasize "lake city" identity vs. Wichita's "aerospace city" identity. Offer housing affordability, lower cost of living as counterbalance to Wichita's higher wages.

Threat 4: McPherson's Aggressive Workforce Development (Direct Local Competition)

McPherson's mechanical engineering program launch (2026), technical training expansion, childcare investment are direct competitive threats. McPherson is roughly same size, same industries, same labor market as El Dorado. If McPherson wins the "skilled workforce hub" narrative, El Dorado loses workers to McPherson.

Mitigation: Match McPherson's workforce development investments. Ensure El Dorado USD 490 has competitive technical programs. Partner with Butler CC to offer apprenticeships, certification programs. Emphasize El Dorado's higher salaries (via FDI recruitment) as incentive to stay/move to El Dorado.

Threat 5: Broadband Parity Will Erode El Dorado's Data Center Advantage

BEAD funding ($166.6M) will bring broadband to most of Kansas rural areas by 2027. Once every small Kansas town has gigabit fiber, broadband is no longer a differentiator. El Dorado's competitive advantage in data center recruitment will rest SOLELY on water rights + FTZ + I-35 access + existing AMAZONE proof-of-concept. Miss the 2-year window (2026–2028) to recruit a data center, and the advantage disappears.

Mitigation: Accelerate data center recruitment immediately. Create "Data Center Zone" designation. Offer tax incentives, pre-build spec data center facility, market aggressively to Meta, Google, Amazon, Anthropic, Microsoft (AI workloads). Close a deal by 2027 before broadband parity erodes advantage.

Threat 6: Energy Sector Transition (Affects Refining, Propane)

Long-term risk: Energy transition away from fossil fuels. HF Sinclair and refining sector face regulatory pressure, trade policy uncertainty, long-term demand contraction (electric vehicles, renewable energy adoption). If refining becomes less economically viable 15–20 years out, El Dorado loses its largest employer + infrastructure asset.

Mitigation: Diversify away from refining-dependent industries. Target advanced manufacturing, aerospace, data centers, healthcare, education — sectors less exposed to energy transition. Position El Dorado as "energy-independent through water security and diverse economy," not "energy-dependent refining town."

Conclusion: El Dorado's Path to Regional Leadership

El Dorado is not the largest competitor city (Salina 47K, Dodge City 26K), but it has the strongest unique structural advantages: water rights, I-35 direct access, FTZ #28, and proven FDI success (AMAZONE). These assets position El Dorado to compete and win in sectors where competitors have no comparable advantage.

The Competitive Reality:

  • Dodge City wins on agricultural processing (Hilmar Cheese), but that's not El Dorado's sector
  • Salina wins on aerospace education (K-State), but El Dorado can win aerospace supply logistics
  • Emporia wins on animal health, but El Dorado can win advanced manufacturing/data centers
  • McPherson competes locally, but El Dorado's water + FDI proof-of-concept are decisive advantages
  • Ponca City is declining due to energy sector contraction — not a threat

The 3-Year Playbook (2026–2028):

  1. Year 1 (2026): Launch data center recruitment (water + BEAD broadband); amplify AMAZONE; execute first phase of lake activation
  2. Year 2 (2027): Close data center deal OR close second major FDI win; lake has 3–4 anchor tenants operational; workforce development pipeline mature
  3. Year 3 (2028): Data center operational or under construction; regional water authority formed (revenue generation); El Dorado recognized as "advanced manufacturing + data center + tourism destination"

The Stake: This window is narrow. BEAD broadband parity will erode El Dorado's data center advantage by 2028–2029. If El Dorado doesn't close a data center deal by 2027, the best opportunity may pass. Similarly, if AMAZONE success is not leveraged for follow-on FDI recruitment, the momentum will dissipate. The next 24 months are critical.

Bottom Line: El Dorado has the assets to become the advanced manufacturing, data center, and lake tourism destination for central Kansas. The competition is real (Dodge City, Salina, Emporia are actively recruiting), but El Dorado's water rights, I-35 access, and FDI proof-of-concept are differentiators that no competitor can easily match. The path is clear. Execution matters.
