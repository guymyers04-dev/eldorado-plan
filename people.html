K–12 Education Excellence — USD 490 El Dorado Schools

El Dorado's future workforce begins in K-12 classrooms. USD 490 serves 2,133 students across 6 schools. This plan provides $25M+ in targeted investments to close achievement gaps, expand career pathways, and position USD 490 graduates for success in college, careers, and civic life.

The Opportunity & Challenge

Current Reality:

  • 79% Graduation rate (vs 89% statewide)
  • 24% Math proficiency (vs 31% statewide)
  • 22.8% Child poverty rate
  • 2,133 Students (6 schools)

Vision 2040 Goal:

  • 95% Graduation rate
  • 50%+ Math proficiency
  • All USD 490 grads College/career ready
  • 100% Pathway clarity by 8th grade

Root Causes: Child poverty, housing instability, limited parental work-flexibility, and a K-12 system stretched thin on resources. Vision 2040's residential development, childcare expansion, and workforce stability initiatives directly address these structural constraints. K-12 excellence is not separate from economic development — it is central to it.

Butler Community College & Workforce Development

Education as Economic Engine

Butler Community College is El Dorado's most powerful planning partner and most underutilized economic development asset. This plan places Butler CC at the center of every major sector — not as an afterthought, but as the primary workforce engine.

Butler Community College

Founded in 1927 as El Dorado Junior College, Butler CC is now Kansas's 2nd-largest community college and 6th-largest educational institution statewide — serving ~6,800 students (2024) across 7 campuses with 90+ programs. The main campus is right here in El Dorado. With a 31:1 student-teacher ratio and programs spanning liberal arts, nursing, IT, agriculture, fine arts, and business — Butler CC sits at the center of every sector in this plan.

The question is not whether Butler CC can support El Dorado's growth — it absolutely can. The question is whether El Dorado is bold enough to make it central to every recruitment conversation.

The Vision: Employer-Aligned Programs

For each major employer or sector recruited to El Dorado, Butler CC commits to designing or expanding a credential program that produces qualified graduates within 18–24 months of the employer's arrival. This is the model that has worked in manufacturing corridors across the Midwest — and it's how a community college becomes the most important line item in an economic development pitch deck.

Butler CC's Small Business Development Center (SBDC), already on campus, also provides free consulting, market research, and loan packaging to El Dorado entrepreneurs — directly supporting the downtown incubator, lake-based tourism businesses, and agri-startups.

K–12 Partnership: USD 490 El Dorado

USD 490 serves 2,133 students across 6 schools with a student-teacher ratio of 18.6:1. The district's current 79% graduation rate (vs. 89% statewide) and math proficiency of 24% (vs. 31% statewide) represent the most urgent long-term challenge in this plan. USD 490's own stated goal is to reach a 95% graduation rate and reduce chronic absenteeism to 10%. Vision 2040 supports that goal by attacking root causes: child poverty (22.8%), housing instability, and limited workforce opportunity. El Dorado's public schools (USD 490) are a direct pipeline into Butler CC and the local workforce. Strengthen that pipeline with structural programming — not just aspirational partnerships.

  • Dual enrollment: high school juniors/seniors earn Butler CC credits free of charge
  • Career & Technical Education (CTE) expansion at El Dorado High School
  • Paid internship program placing high school seniors with local employers
  • Industry tours and job shadowing at energy, aerospace, and medical employers
  • STEM curriculum alignment with Butler CC engineering prerequisites
  • Early college pathway: 30+ credit hours by graduation (two semesters saved)
  • Registered apprenticeship partnerships: 20+ paid apprenticeship slots per year for juniors/seniors — administered through the Kansas Office of Registered Apprenticeship (KDC) and Talent Ready grant program (KDC subsidizes employer apprenticeship costs); CareerWise Kansas (expanding from Colorado model) provides turnkey apprenticeship management for employers not set up to run programs independently
  • School-to-work pipeline agreements with every Vision 2040 anchor employer
  • Annual Career Expo at Butler CC — 500+ students, 40+ employers, live hiring
  • Financial literacy curriculum requirement — personal finance, credit, and homeownership
  • Entrepreneurship elective — product development, business plan competition with $5,000 seed grant
  • Athletic scholarship gap-fill program: El Dorado employer consortium offers partial scholarships to keep local athletic talent local

Employer Partnership Program

For Vision 2040 to succeed, Butler CC programs must be co-designed by the employers who will hire the graduates — not developed in isolation and then marketed to industry.

  • Employers co-write curriculum and provide adjunct instructors from their workforce
  • Industry-funded tool kits and lab equipment (reducing cost to Butler CC)
  • Paid co-ops and apprenticeships guaranteed to enrolled students
  • Employer seat on Butler CC Advisory Council for each new program
  • Annual skills gap report published jointly by city, Butler CC, and Chamber
  • Graduate retention incentive: $3,000 bonus for graduates who stay in Butler County 3 years
  • Employer hiring commitment letters signed before new program launches — graduates are not trained into unemployment
  • Rapid training track: 90-day emergency workforce programs when a new employer arrives and needs staff immediately
  • Incumbent worker training: existing El Dorado employees upskilled without disrupting current employment
  • National certification pathway: every program ends in a nationally recognized credential, not just a local certificate
  • Wage data publishing: annual report on starting wages by program graduate, maintaining program accountability
  • Joint butler CC–employer R&D projects: students apply classroom skills to real employer problems with employer funding

Growth without safety infrastructure is unsustainable. El Dorado's current police and fire capacity was designed for a city of 12,000. As population expands toward 18,000 and development extends north and south, response times and coverage become a genuine public safety concern — and a development liability.

  • 15 additional sworn officers by 2030 (phased hiring)
  • Citywide body camera program
  • New CAD (Computer-Aided Dispatch) system
  • K-9 unit expansion (1 additional handler)
  • School resource officer for El Dorado Middle School
  • Traffic enforcement expansion (US-54 corridor)
  • Community policing program in North Gateway
  • Butler CC Criminal Justice pipeline: 5 hires/year
  • Public-facing crime data dashboard: real-time incident transparency map
  • Mental health co-responder program: licensed clinician on select dispatched calls
  • De-escalation and crisis intervention training — all officers, required annually
  • Neighborhood watch expansion to North Gateway and Lakeside Village districts
  • Strategic traffic enforcement detail on US-54 and US-77 commercial corridors
  • Automated license plate reader network: 6 cameras at strategic city entry points
  • Records management system upgrade — real-time data sharing with Butler County
  • Youth Police Academy — 2-week summer program for USD 490 students
  • Succession planning: 5 internal promotion candidates formally identified annually
  • Community advisory board — quarterly public town halls with Police Chief

  • SRO (School Resource Officer) program expansion to El Dorado Middle School

🚒 Fire Department

El Dorado Fire Department

Current: Fire Station 1 downtown, mix of career and volunteer personnel. The existing station provides acceptable response times to the city core, but a second station is critical as development expands north (North Gateway District) and south (data center campus, south industrial park).

  • Fire Station 2 (North Gateway) — top priority
  • Reduces north response time from 12+ min to <5 min
  • New 100-ft aerial/platform ladder truck
  • 4 additional career firefighters (Station 2 crew)
  • HAZMAT capability upgrade (chemical/industrial response)
  • ISO rating improvement: Class 4 → Class 3
  • Improved ISO saves residential insurance premiums citywide
  • Mutual aid agreement expansion with Augusta, Andover, Benton
  • Butler CC Fire Science program pipeline: 3 hires/year
  • Technical rescue team — trench rescue, high-angle, and water rescue cross-training
  • Drone program for aerial incident assessment and active search operations
  • Wildland-urban interface training: Flint Hills prairie fire response protocols
  • Medical first responder (MFR) cross-training for all career firefighters
  • Pre-incident planning for critical facilities: refinery, data center, hospital
  • Community risk reduction: 500+ home fire safety visits annually by firefighters
  • Residential sprinkler retrofit incentive: $500 rebate for voluntary sprinkler installation
  • Community CPR and Stop the Bleed training: 1,000 residents certified per year
  • Apparatus replacement schedule: preventive replacement every 15 years
  • Annual community open house during National Fire Prevention Week
  • Knox Box program: emergency key access for all commercial and multifamily buildings
  • Gas detection and confined space rescue equipment upgrade
  • Training tower construction at Station 2 for live fire and rope training
Needs: Station 2 · Ladder truck · HAZMAT

🚑 Emergency Medical Services

Butler County EMS

EMS in Butler County is provided at the county level. As El Dorado grows and El Dorado Lake sees increased recreational use (water emergencies, trail accidents, event injuries), the city should advocate for dedicated EMS resources and invest in supportive infrastructure.

  • ALS (Advanced Life Support) unit dedicated to lake district
  • AED network expansion: 30 new public AEDs (parks, trails, lake)
  • Telemedicine dispatch for rural Butler County calls
  • Water rescue capability at El Dorado Lake
  • Event medical plan for amphitheater (5,000+ attendance events)
  • Butler CC EMT/Paramedic program: 10+ grads/year feeding EMS
  • Quick-response unit at Fire Station 2 (north district)
  • Drone-deployed AED trial program (Butler CC tech partnership)
  • Community paramedic program: home visits for high-frequency 911 callers to address root causes
  • Mobile integrated health (MIH): chronic disease management home visits for seniors
  • STEMI (heart attack) receiving center protocol formalized with SBA Hospital
  • Stroke response telemedicine consult initiated on every suspected CVA call
  • Mass casualty incident supply cache: equipment and triage supplies for 50+ patients
  • Pediatric-specific resuscitation equipment on all ALS units — infant through adolescent
  • Mental health crisis response team: trained co-responders for behavioral health calls
  • Annual EMS performance report: response time, patient outcomes, and call volume by type
  • EMS–SBA Hospital information system integration for seamless patient hand-off
  • Reserve corps program: 10 EMT volunteers trained, credentialed, and drill-ready
  • Mass gathering event medical plan for amphitheater events (5,000+ attendance)
  • Hazmat medical surveillance protocol for refinery and data center incidents
Good: County EMS structure

Fire Station 2 — North Gateway: The Non-Negotiable

Fire Station 2 is not a nice-to-have. It is the infrastructure prerequisite for North Gateway District development. Insurance underwriters assess residential construction risk based on ISO ratings and response times — a 12-minute response time from Station 1 to the north gateway will increase homeowner insurance premiums by 15–25%, reducing the financial viability of the $45M residential investment in that district. Station 2 costs approximately $4.5M (land + construction + equipment). It should be included in Phase 1 as an enabling investment, not deferred to Phase 3 after the problem becomes acute.
