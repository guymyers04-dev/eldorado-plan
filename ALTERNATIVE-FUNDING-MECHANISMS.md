# El Dorado Vision 2040 — Alternative Funding Mechanisms
## Beyond Grants: Creative Capital Structures for $510K+ Investment & Future Projects

**Purpose:** Deep-dive analysis of non-traditional funding mechanisms to supplement federal/state grants  
**Date:** June 28, 2026  
**Prepared For:** City Commission, EDA Board, El Dorado Inc., Capital Planning Committee  
**Scope:** Creative financing, public-private structures, impact investing, debt instruments, special districts

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

El Dorado's $900M+ Vision 2040 requires a **diversified capital stack** beyond federal/state grants. This document identifies **7 funding categories** with **40+ alternative mechanisms** that can:
- Unlock private capital ($400M+)
- Reduce municipal debt burden
- Attract impact investors & international capital
- Create revenue-generating structures
- Distribute risk across public/private partners

**Key Takeaway:** Successful cities layer 3–4 funding mechanisms per project, not relying on single grants. El Dorado should pursue an **80/20 strategy: 80% non-grant (private, debt, tax credits), 20% grants** for maximum capital deployment.

## PART 1: CAPITAL STRUCTURES & DEBT INSTRUMENTS

### 1. Municipal Bonds

#### General Obligation (GO) Bonds
- **What it is:** City-backed debt repaid from general tax revenue
- **Amount:** $50M–$250M (depends on city bond rating, debt capacity)
- **Terms:** 20–30 year maturity; 3–5% interest rates (if AAA rated)
- **Eligibility:** City infrastructure projects (roads, water, sewer, public buildings)
- **El Dorado Fit:** Water system upgrades, wastewater plant expansion, road improvements
- **Process:** 
  - City Council votes to issue
  - Public hearing required
  - Rating agencies assess credit worthiness
  - Bond underwriter markets to municipal bond investors
  - Funds available 60–90 days after issuance
- **Pros:** Low interest rates, 20–30 year amortization spreads burden
- **Cons:** Increases municipal debt ratio; requires voter approval in some states; affects future bonding capacity
- **Timeline:** 6–12 months from decision to funding
- **Contact:** Kansas Municipal Finance Officers Association; municipal bond underwriters (Goldman Sachs, Morgan Stanley, Raymond James)

#### Revenue Bonds
- **What it is:** Debt secured by specific revenue stream (utility sales, toll revenue, parking fees)
- **Amount:** $20M–$100M (sized to projected revenue)
- **Terms:** 20–30 years; 4–6% interest (higher risk than GO)
- **Eligibility:** Water/sewer systems, parking facilities, recreational facilities with revenue
- **El Dorado Fit:** 
  - Water system bonds (repaid from utility rates)
  - El Dorado Lake water sports revenue (paddle boat rentals, dock fees)
  - Regional water authority revenue (wholesale water sales)
- **Structure:** Revenue-backed pool (debt service coverage ratio [DSCR] typically 1.25–1.50)
- **Pros:** Doesn't count against city's GO debt limit; dedicated revenue stream reduces risk
- **Cons:** Higher interest rates; requires reliable revenue projections; underperforming projects can default
- **Timeline:** 6–12 months
- **Example:** Kansas City (MO) parking bonds ($100M+) backed by parking meter revenue
- **Contact:** KDOT (for transportation bonds); Kansas Water Authority (for water bonds)

#### Build America Bonds (BABs)
- **What it is:** Federal taxable municipal bonds with 35% federal subsidy
- **Current Status:** No new BABs issued post-2010; legacy BABs still trading; unlikely option
- **Historical Context:** Used 2009–2010 during financial crisis for infrastructure projects
- **Note:** Treasury subsidy program discontinued; commercial rates now available via direct borrowing
- **Skip This:** Focus instead on traditional GO/Revenue bonds or bank loans

### 2. Property Assessed Clean Energy (PACE) Financing

- **What it is:** City issues bonds for energy/water efficiency projects; repayment via property tax assessment
- **Amount:** $5M–$50M per municipality (depending on properties enrolled)
- **Terms:** 10–25 years; 5–7% interest
- **Eligibility:** Property owners (residential, commercial) with energy/water efficiency, renewable energy, or storm water projects
- **El Dorado Fit:**
  - Downtown historic building energy retrofits
  - City government building efficiency (LED, HVAC, solar)
  - Commercial district water conservation retrofits
  - Mega-site infrastructure (on-site solar/wind, energy storage)
- **Structure:**
  - City issues municipal bond
  - Offers PACE program to property owners
  - Property owners enroll eligible projects
  - City adds assessment to property tax bill (senior lien position)
  - Bond repaid from property tax assessments
- **Pros:** 
  - No city general fund impact (self-repaying)
  - Attracts private capital for climate projects
  - Property owner realizes energy savings (covers debt service)
  - Attractive to ESG (Environmental, Social, Governance) investors
- **Cons:** 
  - Property tax liens create risk if property values decline
  - Market volatility affects revenue
  - Requires property owner participation (adoption rate 15–30%)
- **Timeline:** 6–9 months to launch program; ongoing enrollment
- **Program Example:** California HERO (Home Energy Retrofit Opportunity), now adopted in Kansas
- **Contact:** PACE America; Kansas Energy Office (785-296-1840)

### 3. Kansas STAR Bond Districts

- **What it is:** State-designated tax increment financing district with accelerated rebate (6.5% annual)
- **Amount:** Typically $20M–$150M+ (project-dependent)
- **Terms:** 20 years; rebate paid annually from tax increment
- **Eligibility:** Large projects (generally $20M+) with significant tax increment generation
- **El Dorado Fit:**
  - Mega-site industrial development ($50M+ facility = $2–3M annual increment)
  - Boutique hotel + lake development ($15–20M = $500K–$800K annual increment)
  - Senior care campus ($25–30M = $700K–$1M annual increment)
- **Structure:**
  - Project generates new tax revenue (sales tax, property tax)
  - State calculates annual "increment" (new tax minus baseline)
  - 6.5% of increment rebated to developer/project for 20 years
  - Funds repay infrastructure or developer investment
- **Historical Data:**
  - Kansas Speedway (2004) used STAR Bond; $300M+ project, $50M+ rebates over 20 years
  - Wyandotte County casinos (2008–2010) used STAR Bonds
  - Success rate: 70–80% of designated projects break ground within 5 years
- **Pros:**
  - Predictable incentive stream (6.5% = ~$1.3M–$6.5M per $20M–$100M project)
  - No upfront city cash; rebated from new tax revenue
  - Attracts major developers/institutional investors
- **Cons:**
  - Requires state approval (competitive; 5–10 applications annually statewide)
  - Project must be truly "transformational" (state scrutiny)
  - STAR districts can't overlap (one per municipality typically)
- **Application Timeline:** 6–9 months (KDC review, state legislature review if needed)
- **Recommended Projects (Top Candidates):**
  1. Mega-site data center ($50M+) — best candidate
  2. Mega-site battery manufacturing ($75M+) — strong candidate
  3. Hotel + lake development ($20M) — secondary candidate
- **Contact:** Kansas Department of Commerce (STAR Bond Program Officer); (785-296-3481)

### 4. Tax Increment Financing (TIF) Districts (Local)

- **What it is:** City-designated district where property tax increment funds public improvements
- **Amount:** $5M–$50M (sized to projected property tax increment)
- **Terms:** 10–20 years (varies by city policy)
- **Eligibility:** Designated downtown, industrial, or mixed-use redevelopment areas
- **El Dorado Fit:**
  - Downtown historic district ($8–12M project = $200K–$400K annual increment)
  - North El Dorado housing development ($3–5M project = $100K–$200K annual increment)
  - Industrial park infrastructure ($2–4M project = $80K–$150K annual increment)
- **Structure:**
  - City designates TIF district; establishes "base year" property tax revenue
  - Any tax increase above baseline is captured
  - Captured taxes fund improvements (infrastructure, land acquisition, building rehab)
  - After district expires, all taxes return to baseline recipients (school, county)
- **Pros:**
  - No new tax; uses growth only
  - Funds improvements that drive district growth
  - Can leverage with private development
- **Cons:**
  - School/county lose future tax revenue (political risk)
  - Relies on property value appreciation (vulnerable to recession)
  - Requires City Council approval; may face community opposition
- **Timeline:** 3–6 months to establish TIF district
- **Example:** Wichita downtown TIF has funded $100M+ in improvements over 25 years
- **Note:** Kansas law allows TIF but heavily restricts it; requires careful legal review
- **Contact:** Kansas Department of Revenue (TIF program guidance); city attorney

### 5. Special Districts (Enhanced Services/Assessment Districts)

#### Enhanced 911 Districts
- **What it is:** Special taxing district for emergency services; tax added to property tax bill
- **Amount:** 1–3 mills ($1–3 per $1,000 property value)
- **Eligibility:** Any municipality with emergency services need
- **El Dorado Fit:** Enhanced 911 dispatch center, emergency response equipment, inter-agency coordination
- **Revenue:** $500K–$2M annual (depending on property base)
- **Pros:** Predictable revenue; dedicated to public safety; no voter approval required in Kansas
- **Cons:** Political sensitivity (tax increase perception); must justify expenditures
- **Timeline:** 4–6 weeks to establish (legislative action required)
- **Contact:** Kansas Association of Emergency Managers

#### Business Improvement Districts (BIDs)
- **What it is:** Voluntary assessment on commercial properties; funds district improvements
- **Amount:** 0.5–2% of assessed property value annually ($500K–$2M per district)
- **Eligibility:** Commercial/mixed-use areas (downtown, retail corridors)
- **El Dorado Fit:** Downtown BID ($500K–$1M annually) for streetscape, security, marketing
- **Structure:**
  - Property owners in district vote to establish
  - Assessment added to property tax bill
  - BID board (property owners + city rep) allocates funds
- **Uses:** Streetscape improvements, security, marketing, events, business support services
- **Pros:** 
  - Property owners control spending
  - Focused on district benefits
  - Majority support required (democratic)
- **Cons:** 
  - Requires property owner participation (70%+ vote threshold)
  - Additional tax (political resistance)
  - Administration overhead
- **Timeline:** 3–6 months to organize and vote
- **Examples:** Wichita Delano District ($2M+), Kansas City (MO) Power & Light District
- **Contact:** Kansas Downtown Development Association; city attorney

#### Utility/Stormwater Districts
- **What it is:** Special taxing district for infrastructure (water, sewer, stormwater)
- **Amount:** $1M–$10M+ (sized to infrastructure needs)
- **El Dorado Fit:** 
  - Stormwater management district (bioswales, detention ponds, green infrastructure)
  - Water/sewer expansion district (north El Dorado growth)
  - Mega-site utility infrastructure district
- **Structure:**
  - City establishes district; collects fees via utility bills or tax assessment
  - Dedicated to specific infrastructure maintenance/improvement
- **Pros:** Visible, dedicated revenue; property owners accept as necessary
- **Cons:** Political risk if rates feel too high
- **Timeline:** 6–12 months (requires approval, engineering studies)
- **Contact:** Kansas Department of Health & Environment (KDHE); utility directors

## PART 2: PRIVATE CAPITAL STRUCTURES

### 6. Public-Private Partnerships (PPPs)

#### Full Service Contracts (Design-Build-Finance-Operate-Maintain)
- **What it is:** Private company designs, builds, finances, operates public facility; city pays management fee
- **Amount:** $10M–$100M+ (project dependent)
- **El Dorado Fit:**
  - Water treatment plant expansion (private operator, 20-year contract)
  - Wastewater treatment plant (design-build-operate)
  - Public parking facility (design-build-operate, revenue-sharing)
  - Regional water authority (private management, city policy oversight)
- **Terms:** 15–30 years; private entity assumes operating risk
- **Structure:**
  - RFP process; city defines service requirements
  - Private partner designs most cost-efficient solution
  - Private finances (through project loans)
  - Private operates; city pays monthly/annual service fee
  - Typically fixed fee + performance incentives
- **Pros:**
  - Transfers design/operating risk to private sector
  - Often more efficient than public operation
  - Offloads financing from municipal budget
  - Performance-based (penalties for underperformance)
- **Cons:**
  - Higher long-term cost (private profit margin + financing)
  - Less city control over operations
  - Requires strong contract monitoring
  - Political resistance (privatization concerns)
- **Timeline:** 12–18 months from RFP to contract signature
- **Examples:**
  - Orlando (FL) water reclamation facility PPP
  - Chicago (IL) parking meter PPP ($1.1B upfront payment)
  - Kansas City (MO) water infrastructure PPP
- **Contact:** National Council for Public-Private Partnerships; Kansas PPP guidance (KDC)

#### Concession/Lease Agreements
- **What it is:** City leases asset to private operator for fixed term; city receives royalty/lease payment
- **Amount:** Annual payments $100K–$5M+ (depends on asset revenue potential)
- **El Dorado Fit:**
  - El Dorado Lake recreational facilities (dock concession, water sports school, beach club)
  - Downtown parking facility (city owns, private operates; profit-sharing)
  - City arenas/event spaces (managed by private promoters; revenue-share)
  - Historic theater (lease to restoration non-profit; city supports via tax abatement)
- **Terms:** 10–25 years; renewable
- **Structure:**
  - City retains ownership; private operator pays lease + possibly revenue-share
  - Operator assumes business risk
  - City receives guaranteed base revenue + percentage of profits
- **Pros:**
  - Predictable revenue stream
  - Transfers business risk to private sector
  - No upfront capital required
  - City retains ownership/control
- **Cons:**
  - Operator may fail (default risk)
  - Less revenue if operator under-invests
  - Public perception (privatization)
- **Timeline:** 6–9 months to negotiate and execute
- **Lake Concession Example:**
  - Boutique hotel concession: $15M private investment; city receives 5% of gross revenue
  - Restaurant concession: $3–5M build-out; city receives 8–10% of revenue
  - Water sports facility: $2–3M investment; city receives 6% of revenue
- **Contact:** National Council for Real Estate Investment Fiduciaries; commercial real estate consultants

### 7. Land Monetization Strategies

#### Land Lease (Ground Lease)
- **What it is:** City owns land; private developer leases for 50–99 years and builds
- **Amount:** Annual lease $50K–$500K+ depending on land value and development
- **El Dorado Fit:**
  - Mega-site land leases to data center companies
  - Lake development land (boutique hotel, restaurants, RV park)
  - Downtown parcels (office buildings, mixed-use development)
  - City-owned industrial park land
- **Structure:**
  - City retains land ownership
  - Developer leases for long term (50+ years)
  - Developer builds/operates improvements
  - Annual lease payment to city (typically $X/acre or % of revenue)
  - Improvements revert to city at lease end
- **Pros:**
  - City retains land value appreciation
  - Predictable long-term revenue
  - Developer incentivized to maintain property (value protection)
  - Tax benefits for developer (depreciation deduction)
- **Cons:**
  - Tenant retention risk (lease end may require renegotiation)
  - Land value may decrease
  - Less attractive to some developers (prefer fee simple ownership)
- **Lease Models:**
  - Fixed annual payment: $500K–$2M annually
  - Percentage of revenue: 3–5% of gross business revenue
  - Hybrid: Base rent + revenue-share
- **Timeline:** 4–6 months to negotiate and execute
- **Examples:**
  - Port authorities (Seattle, New York) use ground leases for terminal development
  - Denver Parks & Recreation ground leases (sports facilities, recreation centers)
- **Contact:** American College of Real Estate Lawyers; commercial real estate advisors

#### Development Rights Sale
- **What it is:** City sells air rights, density rights, or development rights for lump sum
- **Amount:** $1M–$10M (depending on market and rights value)
- **El Dorado Fit:** Downtown redevelopment (if city owns underutilized parcels)
- **Structure:**
  - City owns land with limited current development
  - City sells rights to increase density/height to private developer
  - Developer builds, private entity retains profits
  - City receives one-time lump sum payment
- **Pros:** Immediate capital injection; encourages private development
- **Cons:** City loses long-term revenue potential; buyer assumes all development risk
- **Timeline:** 3–6 months negotiation
- **Note:** Limited applicability for El Dorado (not high-density urban center)

#### Land Bank / Acquisition for Future Resale
- **What it is:** City buires distressed or tax-delinquent properties; resells to developers at premium
- **Amount:** Annual investment $500K–$5M; resale premium 10–30%
- **El Dorado Fit:**
  - Downtown vacant/neglected parcels
  - Tax-delinquent properties
  - Historic properties (acquire for restoration)
  - Strategic infill sites (acquire now, resell for development)
- **Structure:**
  - City acquires property (tax sale, distressed seller, or grant funding)
  - City holds, improves (remediation, demolition, site prep)
  - City resells to developer at fair market value
  - City profit funds next acquisition
- **Pros:**
  - Accumulates over time (sustainable model)
  - Improves neighborhood aesthetics (removes blight)
  - Attracts private development
  - Creates jobs (demolition, remediation, rehab)
- **Cons:**
  - Upfront capital required
  - Carrying costs (taxes, maintenance)
  - Market risk (property values may decline)
  - Political risk (perceived as government overreach)
- **Timeline:** Ongoing (acquire 5–10 properties annually)
- **Examples:**
  - Youngstown (OH) Land Bank ($50M+ properties acquired, $300M+ redevelopment catalyzed)
  - Pittsburgh (PA) Land Bank (similar model)
- **Contact:** National Community Land Trust Association

### 8. Developer Contributions / Exactions

#### Impact Fees
- **What it is:** Developers pay fee to offset infrastructure impacts of new projects
- **Amount:** $2,000–$10,000+ per housing unit; $1–5/sq ft for commercial
- **El Dorado Fit:**
  - Mega-site projects (water infrastructure cost offsets)
  - Residential developments (school, fire, police capacity impacts)
  - Commercial district (traffic, parking, utility impacts)
- **Structure:**
  - City calculates infrastructure cost per unit of development
  - Developer pays impact fee when platting/building
  - Funds used for growth-related infrastructure (new roads, schools, utilities)
- **Uses in El Dorado:**
  - Water/sewer capacity upgrades ($2–3M)
  - Road improvements for north El Dorado growth ($1–2M)
  - Traffic signal/intersection upgrades ($500K–$1M)
- **Pros:**
  - Developer bears growth costs (fairness principle)
  - Predictable revenue source
  - Legally justified (nexus to development)
- **Cons:**
  - May deter development
  - Requires careful legal analysis (must pass "nexus" test)
  - Complex to calculate and defend
- **Timeline:** 6–12 months to establish impact fee study and ordinance
- **Contact:** American Planning Association; Kansas City Planning and Development

#### Density Bonuses / Incentive Zoning
- **What it is:** Developer gets bonus density/height in exchange for public benefits or affordable housing
- **Amount:** 10–40% density increase in exchange for 10–25% affordable units or public space
- **El Dorado Fit:**
  - Workforce housing projects (developer gets 80–100 unit project instead of 60 units; must include 15–20 affordable units)
  - Downtown mixed-use (developer gets 6-story building instead of 4; must include public plaza or retail)
- **Structure:**
  - City defines bonus opportunity
  - Developer seeks density bonus
  - City reviews for public benefit (affordable housing, open space, parking, etc.)
  - City approves; developer builds additional density
- **Pros:**
  - Increases housing supply and affordability
  - Generates developer profit (incentive to participate)
  - Adds retail/public amenities
- **Cons:**
  - May require infrastructure improvements
  - Political controversy (density concerns)
- **Timeline:** 6–12 months to establish policy and negotiate projects
- **Contact:** American Planning Association; urban planning consultants

## PART 3: IMPACT INVESTING & ESG CAPITAL

### 9. Impact Investment Funds

#### Community Development Finance Institution (CDFI) Investments
- **What it is:** Specialized lenders/investors providing "patient capital" for underserved communities
- **Amount:** $1M–$20M per transaction (depending on fund size and project type)
- **El Dorado Fit:**
  - Workforce housing ($5–10M project)
  - Boutique hotel ($15–20M)
  - Food hall incubator ($3–5M)
  - Historic downtown rehab ($4–8M)
- **Structure:**
  - CDFI provides below-market-rate loans or equity investments
  - CDFI prioritizes social impact (jobs, housing, economic opportunity) alongside financial return
  - Loan terms: 5–15 years; 4–7% interest rates (vs. 7–10% market rates)
  - Equity: 5–10% annual return (vs. 15–20% VC target)
- **Pros:**
  - Favorable terms vs. commercial lending
  - Patient capital (tolerates slower revenue ramp)
  - Mission-aligned (CDFI wants El Dorado's success)
  - Tax-advantaged for CDFI investors (attracts institutional capital)
- **Cons:**
  - Smaller fund sizes (typically $10–50M per fund)
  - Longer underwriting (6–9 months)
  - Impact reporting requirements
- **CDFI Examples Working in Kansas:**
  - Rural Development Partners (Kansas-based CDFI; $90M+ assets)
  - American Opportunity Fund (Great Plains focus)
  - Enterprise Community Partners (nationwide, 40 years)
  - NeighborWorks America (housing-focused)
- **Timeline:** 6–9 months from initial contact to funding
- **Contact:** CDFI Fund (treasurydirect.gov/cdfi); Rural Development Partners (316-630-0210)

#### ESG/Sustainability Funds
- **What it is:** Investment funds prioritizing Environmental, Social, Governance returns alongside financial returns
- **Amount:** $5M–$50M+ (growing market; $12T+ in AUM globally)
- **El Dorado Fit:**
  - Renewable energy projects (solar, wind on mega-site)
  - Green building development (LEED-certified downtown, residential)
  - Water efficiency/conservation projects (stormwater management, recycled water)
  - Community development (workforce housing, childcare, health centers)
- **Structure:**
  - Investors (pension funds, insurance companies, foundations, family offices) seek 3–7% returns with impact
  - Fund managers deploy capital to climate/social projects
  - El Dorado projects compete for capital in annual funding cycles
  - Typically: Project financing ($2–20M range)
- **Advantages:**
  - Rapidly growing capital pool ($12T+ globally, $4T+ in US)
  - Lower return requirements (3–7% vs. 10–15% VC)
  - Long-term patient capital (10–20 year holds)
  - Large funds ($1B+) can accommodate El Dorado projects
- **Disadvantages:**
  - Competitive (thousands of projects seek capital)
  - Impact metrics required (must quantify jobs, emissions, housing units, etc.)
  - Geographic focus varies (some exclude rural, some favor rural)
- **ESG Funds with Agricultural/Rural Focus:**
  - Truterra Ventures (ag-tech, ESG, Kansas connections)
  - Twelve (climate tech)
  - DBL Partners (climate + social)
  - BrightRoll Impact Fund (Great Plains agriculture)
- **Timeline:** 6–12 months to present to fund, receive commitment
- **Contact:** Global Impact Investing Network (thegiin.org); Truterra Ventures

#### Gender/Minority-Focused Impact Funds
- **What it is:** Capital targeting women/minority-owned businesses or development benefiting underserved populations
- **Amount:** $500K–$10M per investment
- **El Dorado Fit:**
  - Women-owned manufacturing on mega-site
  - Minority-owned food hall incubator
  - Childcare facilities (primarily female workforce)
  - Senior care (workforce development for women)
- **Structure:**
  - Funds allocate capital to women/minority entrepreneurs or community projects
  - Financial returns: 5–10% (impact-focused)
  - Often include mentoring, business development support
- **Examples:**
  - Halogen Ventures (women founders)
  - Broad Green Capital (African American entrepreneurs)
  - Clearfield Foundation (rural women entrepreneurs)
- **Timeline:** 6–9 months
- **Contact:** Global Impact Investing Network (GIIN); local SBA office

### 10. Philanthropic Capital

#### Community Foundations
- **What it is:** Local/regional foundations with grantmaking programs
- **Amount:** $10K–$500K per grant (varies by foundation size and program)
- **El Dorado Fit:**
  - Downtown historic district improvements ($100K–$300K)

  - Childcare facility ($25K–$75K)

  - Multi-year grants possible
  - Mission alignment (communities want thriving communities)

  - Typically small amounts ($50K–$200K)

  - Grant cycle delays (12–18 months)
- **Timeline:** 6–12 months from LOI to funding
- **Contact:** Kansas Grantmakers Association; Foundation Center (foundationcenter.org)

#### Corporate Partnerships / Cause Marketing
- **What it is:** Local/national corporations fund projects for marketing/CSR benefit
- **Amount:** $50K–$2M+ (depends on corporation size and project fit)
- **El Dorado Fit:**
  - HF Sinclair (local refiner; $200K–$500K for clean energy, community projects)

  - Agriculture companies (ag-tech campus, research partnership; $100K–$1M)

- **Structure:**
  - Corporation seeks to enhance local reputation/market presence
  - Corporation funds project (grants or sponsorships)
  - Project includes corporation branding/recognition
  - Corporation benefits from PR, employee engagement, workforce development
- **Pros:**
  - Significant capital available
  - Multi-year commitments common
  - Often includes in-kind donations (equipment, employee volunteering)
- **Cons:**
  - Requires strong partnership
  - Corporation priorities may shift
  - Community perception risk (corporate influence)
- **Approach:**
  - Identify El Dorado projects aligned with corporation mission/strategy
  - Craft case (ROI, reputation, workforce benefit)
  - Propose partnership structure (grant, sponsorship, equity)
  - Executive-to-executive pitch
- **Timeline:** 3–6 months to develop partnership and secure commitment
- **Contact:** Corporate Social Responsibility officers; local chamber business development

#### Strategic Philanthropy / Donor-Advised Funds (DAFs)
- **What it is:** High-net-worth individuals create DAF to fund causes over time; can direct capital to El Dorado projects
- **Amount:** $100K–$5M+ (depends on DAF size and project fit)

  - Named endowments (river trail named for donor; $500K fund)

  - Wealthy individual creates DAF at community/national foundation

  - Create recognition/legacy narrative
- **Timeline:** 3–6 months to develop and pitch donor
- **Contact:** Community Foundation; development professionals (nonprofit consulting firms)

## PART 4: INTERNATIONAL CAPITAL

### 11. EB-5 Investor Visa Program

- **What it is:** International investors receive green cards for $1M ($500K in Targeted Employment Areas [TEAs]) investment in US projects
- **Amount:** $10M–$50M+ (teamed investors)
- **El Dorado Fit:** Multiple projects qualify:
  - Boutique hotel ($15–20M): $10–15M EB-5 financing
  - Senior care campus ($25–30M): $15–20M EB-5 financing
  - Food hall incubator + office ($5–7M): $3–5M EB-5 financing
  - Data center ($50M+): $30–40M EB-5 financing (if positioned as regional development)
- **Eligibility:**
  - El Dorado qualifies as "Targeted Employment Area" (rural + high unemployment)
  - Job creation requirement: 10 jobs per $500K investment (generous; $50K/job)
  - 5-year holding period; capital returns after (if project successful)
- **Structure:**
  - Project operator raises capital from EB-5 investors (typically $500K per investor)
  - Investors form REIT or syndicate
  - Capital deployed to project
  - Project creates documented jobs
  - Investor receives green card (2-year conditional, then permanent)
  - Capital returned after 5 years (if project viable)
- **Pros for El Dorado:**
  - Access to $10M–$50M+ foreign capital (minimal dilution vs. equity investors)
  - Investors motivated to succeed (green card dependent on documented jobs)
  - No EB-5 repayment obligation (different from loans)
  - Can combine with other debt/equity
- **Cons:**
  - High legal/placement fees ($100K–$300K per transaction)
  - 5-year lock-up risk (capital return uncertain)
  - Complex regulatory compliance (USCIS oversight)
  - Investor pool concentrated in Asia (China, Vietnam, India historically; more diversified now)
- **EB-5 Regional Centers (Kansas):**
  - EDB-5 Regional Centers (multiple Kansas focus areas)
  - Capital EB-5 Fund (operates in Kansas)
- **Top EB-5 Candidates in El Dorado:**
  1. **Boutique hotel** (Best candidate: $15M project, 30+ jobs, strong ROI)
  2. **Senior care campus** (Strong: $25M project, 50+ jobs, stable cash flow)
  3. **Data center** (If positioned as regional development, but likely too capital-intensive)
- **Timeline:** 9–18 months (RC approval, investor recruitment, USCIS processing)
- **Cost:** 1.5–2.5% of capital raised (placement + legal fees)
- **Contact:** EB-5 Regional Center operators; immigration attorneys specializing in EB-5

## PART 5: REVENUE-GENERATING MECHANISMS

### 13. User Fees & Utility Rate Strategies

#### Tiered Utility Rates
- **What it is:** Vary water/sewer rates by customer type (residential vs. commercial/industrial)
- **Amount:** 20–40% premium for commercial/industrial (cost-of-service difference)
- **El Dorado Fit:**
  - Industrial water rates: $0.50–$0.75/1,000 gal (vs. residential $1.50–$2.00/1,000 gal)
  - Sewer rates: Similar tiering
  - Stormwater fees: Based on impervious surface area
- **Revenue Impact:**
  - Mega-site industrial user (10M gal/year): $5K–$7.5K annual water fee
  - 5 similar users: $25K–$37.5K annually
  - Over 20 years: $500K–$750K in incremental revenue
- **Pros:**
  - Aligns costs with usage
  - Funds system maintenance
  - May deter wasteful industrial use
- **Cons:**
  - May deter industrial recruitment (competing cities may offer cheaper)
  - Political resistance (rate increases)
  - Requires rate study justification
- **Strategy:** Use tiered rates but offer discounts for high-efficiency/conservation companies
- **Timeline:** 6–12 months to study and implement
- **Contact:** American Water Works Association; rate consultant

#### Development Impact Fees (Advanced Model)
- **What it is:** Tiered fees based on project impact (not flat rate)
- **Amount:** Varies by project (see Part 2, Section 8)
- **El Dorado Fit:** Mega-site projects (1M+ gal water use = $50K–$100K+ impact fee)
- **Alternative:** Offer "impact fee waivers" in exchange for specific commitments (e.g., renewable energy, local hiring)

#### Parking & Facility Rental Fees
- **What it is:** City charges for parking, event space, facility rentals
- **Amount:** $50K–$500K annually (depending on volume)
- **El Dorado Fit:**
  - Lake recreation area parking ($5 per vehicle; 10K vehicles = $50K)
  - Downtown parking (metered parking if implemented)
  - Event space rental (performing arts center, pavilions)
  - Meeting room rental (city hall, public facilities)
- **Pros:** Predictable revenue; covers maintenance
- **Cons:** Political sensitivity; admin overhead
- **Timeline:** Ongoing once infrastructure in place

### 14. Monetization of City Assets

#### Water Rights / Water Export
- **What it is:** City sells or leases surplus water capacity to regional entities
- **Amount:** $500K–$2M annually (if 5–10M gal/day available)
- **El Dorado Fit:** 
  - Current capacity: 10M gal/day city supply + 23M gal/day additional available
  - Available for export/lease: 10M gal/day (after current + projected growth)
  - Price: $1,000–$2,000 per million gallons annually (market-dependent)
  - Annual revenue potential: $10M–$20M (if fully leveraged)
- **Structure:**
  - City enters master water supply agreement with regional water authority
  - City commits to supply X million gallons annually
  - Regional authority pays annual fee ($1.5–2K per million gallons)
  - 20–30 year contract
- **Pros:**
  - Recurring, predictable revenue ($10M–$20M annually if executed)
  - Uses El Dorado's unique asset (water ownership)
  - Minimal operating cost (gravity-fed system)
  - Diversifies city revenue
- **Cons:**
  - Limits future El Dorado growth capacity (opportunity cost)
  - Environmental concerns (ecosystem impact)
  - Regional politics (counties may oppose)
  - Requires regional water authority formation
- **Timeline:** 12–24 months to study, negotiate, and execute regional water authority agreement
- **Contact:** USACE (El Dorado Lake contract holder); Kansas Water Authority; regional water authorities

#### Air Rights Monetization
- **What it is:** City sells density/air rights over underutilized parcels for development premium
- **Amount:** $500K–$3M (depending on city-owned land)
- **Applicability:** Low for El Dorado (not high-density urban)
- **Skip:** Focus on land lease and development rights instead

#### Historic Preservation Tax Credits (City Leverage)
- **What it is:** City acts as syndicator/facilitator for Historic Tax Credit projects
- **Amount:** 20–45% of project cost (federal + state tax credits)
- **El Dorado Fit:**
  - Downtown historic district: 5–8 properties targeted
  - Total rehab cost: $8M–$12M
  - Tax credit value: $3.2M–$5.4M (leverages private capital)
  - City facilitation fee: 1–2% of tax credit value ($32K–$108K)
- **Structure:**
  - City identifies historic properties needing rehab
  - City partners with developer + LIHTC syndicator
  - Developer does restoration
  - Tax credits sold to investors; proceeds fund project
  - City takes 1–2% fee for origination/facilitation
- **Pros:**
  - Historic properties rehabilitated
  - City earns fee with minimal risk
  - Attracts investment capital
- **Cons:**
  - Requires partnership infrastructure (syndicators, investors)
  - Complex compliance (historic preservation standards)
- **Timeline:** 12–18 months per project
- **Contact:** Kansas State Historic Preservation Office (SHPO); Historic tax credit syndicators

### 16. Project Finance Vehicles

#### Opportunity Zone Fund
- **What it is:** Specialty fund investing in economically distressed census tracts
- **Eligibility:** El Dorado downtown qualifies (Census 2010 Opportunity Zone designation)
- **Tax Benefit:** Capital gains deferred + 15% excluded if held 10 years
- **Amount:** $2M–$20M per fund; multiple funds can invest in same project
- **El Dorado Fit:**
  - Downtown boutique hotel ($15M project)
  - Food hall incubator ($4M project)
  - Historic office/retail conversion ($6M project)

- **Timeline:** 6–9 months to market project to Opportunity Zone funds
- **Contact:** Opportunity Zones database (opportunityzones.ny.gov); Opportunity Zone fund managers

#### Project-Specific Revenue Bonds
- **What it is:** Bonds secured by specific project revenue (toll road, hotel revenue, water fees)
- **Amount:** $5M–$30M (sized to projected project revenue)
- **El Dorado Fit:**
  - Hotel revenue bonds: $10–15M (repaid from hotel room revenue)
  - Water revenue bonds: $5–10M (repaid from industrial water fees)
  - Recreation facility revenue bonds: $2–5M (repaid from concession revenue)
- **Structure:**
  - Project generates revenue (hotel rooms, water sales, recreation fees)
  - Revenue pledged to bond repayment
  - DSCR (Debt Service Coverage Ratio) typically 1.25–1.50
  - Investors get fixed interest rate + principal repayment
- **Advantage:** Doesn't count against city GO debt
- **Disadvantage:** Higher interest rates (5–7% vs. 3–4% for GO)
- **Timeline:** 6–9 months to issue (rating agency review, underwriting)
- **Contact:** Municipal bond underwriters (Goldman Sachs, Morgan Stanley, JP Morgan)

## PART 8: RISK MITIGATION STRATEGIES

### Market Risk
- **Risk:** Recession reduces investor appetite; projects stall
- **Mitigation:** 
  - Diversify project portfolio (don't bet on single project)
  - Target recession-resistant sectors (healthcare, housing, infrastructure)
  - Lock in long-term incentives (STAR Bond guarantees revenue)
  - Maintain operating reserves (12+ months)

### Political Risk
- **Risk:** City Commission rejects financing structure; public backlash
- **Mitigation:**
  - Build community consensus (public engagement early)
  - Structure deals transparently (avoid opaque financial engineering)
  - Emphasize job creation + tax base growth
  - Secure long-term contracts (insulate from election cycles)

### Development Risk
- **Risk:** Developer fails; project stalls; investors lose capital
- **Mitigation:**
  - Conduct developer due diligence (track record, financial strength)
  - Require performance bonds (contractor, developer)
  - Build in completion guarantees (parent company backing)
  - Stage capital release (milestones, not lump sum)

### Market Demand Risk
- **Risk:** Hotel sits empty; housing won't lease; industrial facility doesn't attract tenants
- **Mitigation:**
  - Pre-lease commitments (signed LOIs before breaking ground)
  - Market studies (third-party feasibility analysis)
  - Flexible design (adaptable space if primary use fails)
  - Anchor tenant strategy (secure 50%+ occupancy pre-opening)
