## KEY FINDINGS

### Finding 1: Grants Are Only 20% of Solution
- **Historical Reality:** Most successful cities use 80% non-grant funding (debt, equity, tax credits, public-private structures)
- **El Dorado Opportunity:** $200M+ in federal/state grants available, but insufficient alone
- **Implication:** Must layer debt, private capital, tax credits, and creative mechanisms

### Finding 2: Water Rights = Unique $10M–$20M Annual Revenue Stream
- **Competitive Advantage:** El Dorado owns water rights (USACE contract, city control)
- **Opportunity:** Lease surplus capacity ($10–15M gal/day) to regional entities
- **Revenue Potential:** $1,500–$2,000 per million gallons annually = $10–20M annually
- **Timeline:** 12–24 months to formalize Regional Water Authority + master agreement
- **Impact:** This single mechanism can fund $500K–$1M+ city programs annually (perpetually)

### Finding 3: EB-5 Visa Program = Gateway to $30M–$50M+ Foreign Capital
- **Mechanism:** International investors provide capital ($500K–$1M each) for US green cards
- **El Dorado Projects:** Hotel ($15M), Senior Care ($25M) both strong candidates
- **Capital Deployment:** $5–15M per project; 30+ investor pool per project
- **Timeline:** 9–18 months from project packaging to capital deployment
- **Key Risk:** Regulatory uncertainty; USCIS policy changes affect EB-5 program
- **Mitigation:** Start projects with EB-5 as secondary/mezzanine (bank debt primary); reduces exposure

### Finding 4: STAR Bond District = 6.5% Annual Incentive for $50M+ Projects
- **Mechanism:** State guarantees 6.5% annual rebate on tax increment for 20 years
- **Best Candidates:** Mega-site data center ($50M+), advanced battery facility ($75M+)
- **Financial Impact:** $50M project = $2–3M annual increment = $130K–$195K annual rebate × 20 = $2.6–3.9M cumulative incentive
- **Application:** State approval required; 6–9 month process; ~10 awards statewide annually
- **Strategy:** Apply early 2027 if mega-site project identified

### Finding 5: Public-Private Partnerships (PPPs) Reduce Municipal Debt Load
- **Concept:** Private sector designs, builds, finances, operates infrastructure; city pays service fee
- **Best Fit:** Water treatment plant, wastewater facility, parking structures
- **Benefit:** Offloads $20M–$50M from city bond capacity; transfers operating risk
- **Cost:** Higher long-term expense (private profit + financing); political resistance (privatization)
- **Timing:** Consider for Phase 2–3 projects (2028+)

### Finding 6: Development Fees & Tax Increment Generate $500K–$2M Annually
- **Mechanisms:** Impact fees, Special districts (Enhanced 911, BID), Sales tax growth, Property tax growth
- **Cumulative Potential:** $1–2M annual recurring revenue (once built out)
- **Advantage:** Predictable, long-term revenue (sustains operations beyond capital projects)

### Finding 7: Historic Tax Credits + New Markets Tax Credits = 45–80% of Project Cost
- **Historic Tax Credit:** 20% federal + 25% Kansas = 45% of rehabilitation costs (syndicated)
- **New Markets Tax Credit:** Additional 39% on $100K investment (syndicated)
- **Combined Potential:** Downtown $8–12M project gets $5–7M in non-dilutive credit value
- **Best Projects:** Central Avenue historic district (8–10 buildings); immediate candidates

### Finding 8: CDFI Lending = Patient Capital at Favorable Terms
- **Mechanism:** Community Development Finance Institutions (CDFIs) provide below-market loans
- **Terms:** 4–6% interest (vs. 7–10% commercial); 7–10 year amortization; flexible underwriting
- **El Dorado Partner:** Rural Development Partners ($90M+ assets, Kansas-based)
- **Loan Capacity:** $7–15M across hotel, housing, incubator projects
- **Advantages:** Faster underwriting (2–3 months vs. 6+ for banks); mission-aligned; patient with slower ramps

### Finding 9: Philanthropic Capital = $5M–$10M Available (Non-Dilutive)
- **Sources:** 
  - Wichita Community Foundation ($200M+ assets; $10M+ annual grantmaking)
  - Kansas Health Foundation ($4B+ assets; health focus)
  - Corporate sponsors (HF Sinclair, energy companies, ag companies)
  - Donor-advised funds (local high-net-worth individuals)
- **Best Uses:** Arts center, downtown improvements, parks, childcare, education
- **Advantage:** No repayment; no equity dilution
- **Disadvantage:** Long fundraising timelines; restricted to specific program areas

### Finding 10: FDI Recruitment Proven & Scalable (AMAZONE Case Study)
- **AMAZONE Success:** German company selected El Dorado (Dec 2025); $20M+ investment proof
- **Replicability:** Target 5–10 similar companies over 5 years
- **Capital Potential:** $100M–$200M+ from FDI (foreign direct investment) alone
- **Infrastructure:** JETRO (Japan), GTAI (Germany), KOTRA (South Korea) actively recruiting
- **Strategy:** Use AMAZONE as anchor proof; recruit with incentive packages ($2–7M per facility)

### Risk Mitigation Strategies

| Risk | Impact | Mitigation |
|------|--------|-----------|
| **Economic Recession** | Investors reduce appetite; projects stall | Diversify projects; target recession-resistant sectors (healthcare, housing, infrastructure); lock in multi-year commitments |
| **Political Changes** | City Commission rejects financing; incentives blocked | Build community consensus early; transparent public engagement; long-term contracts (resist election-cycle volatility) |
| **Federal/State Program Changes** | EB-5 visa cap; LIHTC allocation shifts; EDA priorities change | Monitor policy; have contingency funding sources; don't bet entire project on single program |
| **Developer Failure** | Hotel/housing developer defaults; project stalls; investor capital lost | Require performance bonds; stage capital (milestones, not lump sum); verify developer financials; require parent company guarantees |
| **Market Demand Risk** | Hotel empty; housing won't lease; industrial facility no tenants | Pre-lease commitments (signed LOIs before breaking ground); third-party market studies; flexible design (adaptable space if primary use fails) |
| **Cost Overruns** | Construction costs exceed budget; project underfunded | 10–15% contingency in budget; fixed-price contracts with penalties; value engineering reviews |

## COMPARISON: FEDERAL GRANTS vs. PRIVATE CAPITAL

| Dimension | Federal Grants | Private Capital (Debt/Equity/EB-5) |
|---|---|---|
| **Speed** | 12–18 mo (slow) | 3–9 mo (faster) |
| **Amount** | $100K–$5M typical | $1M–$50M+ (larger) |
| **Certainty** | 20–40% win rate (uncertain) | 60–80% approval (certain, if creditworthy) |
| **Cost** | $0 (free) | 4–7% interest + fees (10–15% all-in cost) |
| **Control** | Restrictions on use; reporting; compliance | Flexible; lender doesn't control operations |
| **Dilution** | None (non-dilutive) | Debt doesn't dilute equity; EB-5/equity does |
| **Politics** | Subject to policy changes; uncertain future | Commercial terms; private entities honored |

**Conclusion:** Grants are valuable but insufficient alone. **Optimal strategy: 20% grants + 80% private capital** (debt 50%, equity 20%, tax credits 10%) = fastest capital deployment, lowest political risk.
