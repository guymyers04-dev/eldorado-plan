## PROLOGUE: THE MOMENT

It's June 2026. The window is open.

In the next 24–36 months, four massive economic trends will converge on El Dorado, Kansas. Data center operators are scouting Midwest locations. International manufacturers are looking for U.S. entry points. Baby boomers are aging at scale, looking for places to retire. Families are desperate for workforce housing in a region facing severe shortages.

**This is not coincidence. This is physics.**

The city has unique assets that cannot be replicated by competitors: water rights owned by no other Kansas city, I-35 access that Salina and Emporia cannot match, international investment proof-of-concept (AMAZONE's December 2025 opening), and the #1 state park in Kansas—an amenity worth hundreds of millions in tourism and workforce retention.

**But windows close.**

By 2028, broadband parity will erase the data center advantage. By 2027, the international FDI network effect will fade if no second win is secured. By 2028, competitors will have matched the workforce housing strategy. The city has 24–36 months to capitalize on non-replicable advantages, or the trajectory shifts from "growth leader" to "stable city."

**This is the story of how we seize this moment.**

## CHAPTER 1: THE FOUNDATION
### Understanding What We're Building

### 1.1 The Vision 2040 Framework

El Dorado Vision 2040 is not a wish list. It's a decision framework.

The framework organizes development across seven strategic pillars:

1. **Economic Growth** — Data centers, FDI, niche industries, and value-added agriculture
2. **Destination Development** — Lake tourism, downtown revitalization, arts/heritage economy
3. **People Development** — K–12 excellence, higher education partnerships, childcare, workforce housing
4. **Regional Leadership** — Butler County integration, I-35 corridor positioning, Flint Hills gateway
5. **Infrastructure Foundation** — Water, fiber, transportation, smart city systems
6. **Quality of Life** — Parks, recreation, public safety, community wellness
7. **Financial Sustainability** — Tax base growth, funding diversification, public-private partnerships

These pillars are not separate initiatives. They are interconnected — each success strengthens the others.

**For example:** Data center recruitment (Economic Growth) requires reliable water infrastructure (Infrastructure Foundation). Lake tourism development (Destination Development) attracts senior living operators (People Development). Workforce housing (People Development) enables FDI recruitment (Economic Growth). Skilled manufacturing workforce (People Development) supports both data centers and FDI (Economic Growth).

This is systems thinking applied to a city.

---

### 1.2 The Competitive Reality

El Dorado is not alone in wanting growth. It's racing against five serious competitors:

**Salina (47K)** — The Aerospace Hub
- Strength: K-State partnership, K-AIRES innovation center (2026), aerospace heritage
- Weakness: Off I-35 corridor, no unique water rights, aerospace-only focus
- Threat: HIGH for hyperscale manufacturing and aerospace contracts; LOW for data centers (no water), FDI (no proof-of-concept)

**McPherson (13K)** — The Workforce Competitor
- Strength: Matching El Dorado's workforce development investments, mechanical engineering program (2026), childcare expansion
- Weakness: Shared labor market, no unique assets, no recent foreign investment
- Threat: MODERATE — will compete aggressively for talent but lacks FDI proof-of-concept

**Emporia (24.5K)** — The Animal Health Niche
- Strength: Concentrated animal health sector, some agricultural heritage
- Weakness: Off I-35 corridor, no water rights, leadership transitions
- Threat: LOW — different sector focus means both cities can succeed

**Dodge City (26K)** — The Food Sector Leader
- Strength: Hilmar Cheese ($600M facility, 270 jobs), proves mega-FDI is possible in rural Kansas
- Weakness: Isolated, food/ag processing mono-culture, declining region
- Threat: NONE — but validates that rural FDI recruitment works

**Ponca City (25K)** — The Declining City
- Threat: None
- Lesson: What happens without strategic reinvestment

**The competitive analysis reveals a critical insight:** El Dorado has non-replicable advantages that competitors cannot match. Water rights, I-35 access, AMAZONE proof-of-concept, #1 state park asset — these are permanent advantages. But they are only valuable if El Dorado executes faster than competitors can respond.

**The race is not for the best city. It's for the fastest city.**

### 1.3 The Four Economic Trends Creating the Window

**Trend #1: The Data Center Boom (2025–2028)**

Data center demand has exploded. Meta, Google, Microsoft, OpenAI, and AWS are building hyperscale facilities at unprecedented pace. AI training requires massive computing power, which requires massive electricity and water infrastructure.

The window for data center recruitment closes in 2028 for a specific reason: broadband parity. When the $166.6M BEAD broadband deployment is complete across Kansas (H2 2026–2027), every Kansas city will have gigabit fiber. Broadband will no longer be a differentiator.

**What remains?** Water and power. El Dorado's 23M gallons/day owned water rights become the only differentiator.

**The implication:** Data center recruitment must close by 2027. After 2028, the advantage evaporates.

**The strategy:** Aggressive recruitment in Months 1–24. Target: LOI signed by Q4 2027. Groundbreaking in 2028.

---

**Trend #2: The Agricultural FDI Cycle (2025–2027)**

German manufacturing is looking for U.S. entry points. So are Japanese and Korean manufacturers.

AMAZONE's December 2025 opening in El Dorado is not an isolated event — it's a data point that JETRO, GTAI, and KOTRA can use to convince other manufacturers to look at rural Kansas. The window for international FDI recruitment is tight: 2025–2027. After that, if no second win is secured, the network effect fades.

Why? Because recruitment works through peer networks. When GTAI tells a German manufacturer "El Dorado worked for AMAZONE," the manufacturer listens. But if no second company opens, the narrative weakens.

**The implication:** FDI recruitment must accelerate in 2026, close 2nd LOI by Q3 2027, and have 3rd in negotiation by year-end.

**The strategy:** Hire an FDI Officer with JETRO/GTAI/KOTRA relationships. Begin outreach immediately. Position El Dorado as the "proven" location for German and Japanese agricultural manufacturers.

---

**Trend #3: The Senior Living Boom (2026–2032)**

Baby boomers are aging. The oldest cohort (born 1946) is turning 80. The youngest (born 1964) is turning 62. For the next six years, senior living demand will be at peak levels.

Sunrise Senior Living, Brookdale Senior Living, and Atria Senior Living are actively expanding in the Midwest. They look for cities with:
- Healthcare infrastructure (El Dorado has a gap; opportunity for recruitment)
- Lifestyle amenities (lake, parks, arts/culture — El Dorado has these)
- Affordable real estate (El Dorado qualifies)
- Skilled workforce nearby (Butler County + Wichita, check)

**The implication:** 18–24 month lead time means recruitment 2026–2027 for openings 2027–2028.

**The strategy:** Hire Housing Consultant to recruit senior living operators. Develop "Senior Living at El Dorado Lake" concept. Target 200–300 units.

---

**Trend #4: The Regional Housing Shortage (2025–Ongoing)**

McPherson, Salina, and Emporia all face severe workforce housing shortages. The WSU Housing Needs Assessment (2023) documented El Dorado's gap: 1,100 units needed. But it also documented the region-wide shortage.

**The implication:** First-mover advantage on workforce housing means attracting workers before competitors can.

**The strategy:** 90-Day Sprint Plan targets $8–12M LIHTC financing (Month 1–6). Site acquisition (Month 3–6). Groundbreaking (Month 12–18). 150–200 units operational by end 2027.

### 2.1 The Data Center Development Director
**Mission:** Bring the first hyperscale data center to El Dorado by Q4 2027

**Why This Role Matters**

The data center is the anchor investment that enables everything else. A $500M data center facility generates:
- 100–300 direct jobs
- $5M–$10M annual tax revenue
- Water infrastructure investment
- Electricity grid upgrades
- Transportation corridor improvements
- Workforce housing demand

But recruiting a hyperscale data center is not a standard economic development task. It requires:
- Relationship networks at major operators (CoreWeave, Equinix, Digital Realty, Lambda Labs)
- Understanding of site selection criteria (power, water, cooling, latency, fiber)
- Ability to negotiate complex deals involving tax incentives, long-term water commitments, permitting fast-tracks
- Authority to commit city resources (water supply, permitting, infrastructure investment)

**The Role in Practice**

**Weeks 1–2: Broker Engagement**
- Hire professional broker (CBRE, JLL, or Cushman & Wakefield)
- Provide complete site package (land, utilities, water rights, fiber status, permitting timeline, tax incentives)
- Receive list of 5–10 qualified prospects
- Begin initial outreach to target operators

**Months 2–3: Prospect Qualification**
- Contact 10–15 prospects; qualify decision timeline and site criteria
- Filter to 3–5 serious prospects (site visits scheduled by Month 3)
- Prepare detailed site presentations (PowerPoint with aerial photos, utility specifications, water commitment letter)
- Negotiate preliminary terms (water supply, land cost, tax incentive structure)

**Months 4–6: Site Visits**
- Host 3–5 data center operators at site
- Conduct technical presentations (water infrastructure, power capacity, fiber deployment timeline, geological surveys)
- Meet with city leadership (Mayor, City Manager) to demonstrate political support
- Facilitate engineering feasibility studies
- Begin LOI negotiations with 1–2 qualified prospects

**Months 7–12: LOI Negotiation**
- Negotiate detailed LOI terms (investment amount, timeline, water commitment, job creation, community investment)
- Coordinate legal review with City Attorney
- Secure City Commission approval of water supply agreement
- Finalize tax incentive structure

**Months 13–24: LOI Closing**
- Support operator's due diligence (environmental assessment, grid capacity study, water quality analysis)
- Facilitate local partnerships (HF Sinclair for energy, utility companies, water authority)
- Support engineering design (permitting, infrastructure coordination)
- Target: Groundbreaking 2028; operations 2029–2030

**Success Metrics**
- Month 3: 5–10 qualified prospects identified
- Month 6: ≥3 site visits scheduled
- Month 9: 1–2 active LOI discussions
- Month 12: LOI drafted with qualified operator
- Q4 2027: LOI signed
- 2028: Groundbreaking
- 2029–2030: Facility operational

**Compensation & Incentives**
- Base Salary: $120K–$140K
- Performance Bonus: 15% of salary if LOI signed by Q4 2027 ($18K–$21K)
- Professional Development: $5K/year (attend data center conferences, broker training)
- Vehicle: Mileage reimbursement (0.67/mile) for broker meetings, site visits, prospect travel

**Who Should Hire This Person?**

The ideal Data Center Development Director is:
- 5+ years economic development or real estate site selection experience
- Track record recruiting $100M+ industrial/commercial projects
- Deep knowledge of hyperscale data center sector
- Existing relationships with CBRE/JLL/Cushman & Wakefield brokers
- Understanding of data center cooling/power/water requirements
- Strong negotiation and executive communication skills

**Example of Ideal Profile:** A former site selection director at CBRE or JLL who closed 2–3 data center deals in the Midwest, or an economic development director at a peer city (Fargo, Des Moines, Madison) who recruited hyperscale investment.

**How Will This Person Change the City?**

If successful, the Data Center Director delivers:
- $500M+ capital investment
- 100–300 direct jobs
- $5M–$10M annual tax revenue
- Water infrastructure investment

- Catalyst for secondary/tertiary investment

### 2.2 The FDI Development Officer
**Mission:** Secure 2–3 additional foreign direct investments by 2027 to create a manufacturing cluster

**Why This Role Matters**

AMAZONE opened in December 2025. One FDI win is proof-of-concept. Two or three FDI wins create a cluster.

A manufacturing cluster is self-reinforcing. When manufacturers see peers locating in the same city, they follow. Suppliers move in. Logistics providers set up operations. The city becomes a regional hub.

The FDI Development Officer's job is to turn AMAZONE's success into a network effect — convincing other German, Japanese, and Korean manufacturers that El Dorado is the right location.

But this requires deep relationships with international recruitment agencies:
- **JETRO (Japan External Trade Organization)** — Recruits Japanese manufacturers
- **GTAI (Germany Trade & Invest)** — Recruits German manufacturers
- **KOTRA (Korea Trade-Investment Promotion Agency)** — Recruits Korean manufacturers
- **SelectUSA (U.S. Department of Commerce)** — Federal program for FDI recruitment

These agencies work through networks. A manufacturer considering U.S. expansion asks their home country agency "Where should we locate?" The agency responds with site recommendations based on infrastructure, talent, incentives, and peer networks.

**The implication:** The FDI Officer must build relationships with these agencies, position El Dorado as the "proven" location for international manufacturers, and create a pipeline of qualified prospects.

**The Role in Practice**

**Weeks 1–2: International Agency Outreach**
- Contact JETRO Chicago office; request meeting with manufacturing desk
- Contact GTAI (AHK USA) Chicago office; request introduction to German manufacturers
- Contact KOTRA Washington D.C. office; request Korean manufacturer referrals
- Present AMAZONE case study as proof-of-concept

**Months 2–3: Prospect Identification**
- Work with JETRO to identify 5–10 Japanese manufacturers in agricultural equipment, precision machinery, or advanced manufacturing
- Work with GTAI to identify 5–10 German manufacturers in agricultural machinery, food processing, or automotive supply
- Work with KOTRA to identify 3–5 Korean manufacturers in advanced manufacturing or electronics
- Qualify prospects: decision timeline, U.S. expansion stage, site selection criteria

**Months 4–6: Prospect Engagement**
- Conduct initial outreach calls with qualified prospects
- Present El Dorado opportunity (water rights, I-35 access, AMAZONE case study, tax incentives, workforce)
- Request pre-qualification meeting with international partner present
- Prepare site packages customized to each prospect's sector

**Months 7–12: Site Visits**
- Host 3–5 FDI site visits (Japanese, German, Korean manufacturers)
- Conduct technical presentations on infrastructure, workforce, incentives
- Facilitate facility tours (HF Sinclair refinery, AMAZONE facility, mega-site)
- Meet with city leadership to demonstrate political support
- Begin LOI negotiation with 1–2 qualified prospects

**Months 13–24: Deal Closing**
- Support EB-5 visa structuring if required (for Korean/Japanese investors)
- Coordinate CFIUS (Committee on Foreign Investment in the U.S.) approval process
- Finalize incentive packages (property tax, sales tax, job creation credits)
- Support engineering due diligence
- Target: 2nd LOI by Q3 2027; 3rd in negotiation by year-end

**Success Metrics**
- Month 3: 5–10 FDI prospects identified (Japanese, German, Korean)
- Month 6: ≥2 site visits scheduled
- Month 9: 1–2 active LOI discussions
- Q3 2027: 2nd LOI signed
- Q4 2027: 3rd in negotiation
- 2028–2029: 2nd/3rd facilities operational

**Compensation & Incentives**
- Base Salary: $100K–$120K
- Performance Bonus: 15% of salary if 2nd LOI signed by Q3 2027 ($15K–$18K)
- Professional Development: $5K/year (attend JETRO/GTAI/KOTRA events, international trade missions)
- Travel: Budget for 2–3 international site visits (Japan, Germany, Korea) to meet prospects

**Who Should Hire This Person?**

The ideal FDI Development Officer is:

- Existing relationships with JETRO, GTAI, KOTRA staff
- Understanding of EB-5 visa structuring, CFIUS process, tariff codes

**Example of Ideal Profile:** A former JETRO or GTAI business development manager, or an economic development director at a peer city (Madison, Minneapolis, Des Moines) with international recruitment experience.

**How Will This Person Change the City?**

If successful, the FDI Officer delivers:

### 2.3 The Housing Development Consultant
**Mission:** Secure $8–12M in workforce housing financing and deliver 200–300 units by end of 2027

**Why This Role Matters**

Housing is the constraint on all other growth. If El Dorado recruits a data center (100–300 jobs) but has no workforce housing, the jobs will be filled by workers commuting from Wichita. If El Dorado recruits FDI facilities (50–150 jobs) but has no housing, the same problem occurs.

The Housing Development Consultant's job is to break the housing constraint by:
1. Securing LIHTC (Low-Income Housing Tax Credit) financing for 60–80 units
2. Securing USDA Rural Development financing for 40–60 units
3. Identifying additional state/federal programs (RHID, CDBG, MIH, state bonds)
4. Structuring public-private partnerships for senior living housing (200–300 units)
5. Facilitating land acquisition and permitting
6. Supporting construction management

This is complex work requiring deep expertise in affordable housing finance, LIHTC syndication, USDA loan programs, and state/federal grant structures.

**The Role in Practice**

**Weeks 1–2: Financing Program Research**
- Create inventory of available financing programs (LIHTC 9% vs. 4%, USDA RD, state programs, private equity)
- Contact 5–10 LIHTC syndicators (National Equity Fund, CCORP, Affordable Housing Managers, others)
- Contact USDA Rural Development office; request preliminary consultation
- Prepare pre-application packages for each program

**Months 1–3: Financing Applications**
- Submit LIHTC pre-applications (60–80 units, $1.5M–$2M equity needed, 2026–2027 development)
- Submit USDA RD loan applications (40–60 units, direct/guaranteed loans)
- Apply for state housing programs (RHID, state tax credits, bonding capacity)
- Secure preliminary approval letters from ≥2 financing sources

**Months 3–6: Site Acquisition**
- Identify development sites (North El Dorado for mixed-income, South El Dorado for family housing)
- Conduct site due diligence (appraisals, environmental assessment, zoning verification)
- Negotiate land purchase agreements
- Secure optioning funds if needed

**Months 6–12: Master Planning & Design**
- Work with architects to design LIHTC-compliant mixed-income development
- Prepare construction cost estimates
- Develop development timeline (permitting, construction, occupancy)
- Finalize financing commitments

**Months 12–18: Permitting & Construction Start**
- Secure city/county permitting approvals
- Finalize construction contracts
- Close financing
- Begin construction

**Months 18–24: Construction Management**
- Oversee construction progress
- Manage LIHTC compliance and reporting
- Coordinate leasing and occupancy
- Target: First units operational by end 2027

**Concurrent: Senior Living Development**
- Recruit senior living operators (Sunrise, Brookdale, Atria, others)
- Identify lakefront sites for senior living campus (200–300 units)
- Develop concept plans (independent living, assisted living, memory care)
- Facilitate operator due diligence and site visits
- Target: 1–2 senior living LOIs signed by Q4 2027; groundbreaking 2028

**Success Metrics**
- Month 2: ≥2 LIHTC syndicators in active discussions
- Month 3: USDA RD pre-qualification letter received
- Month 6: $8–12M financing secured; site acquisition target identified
- Month 9: Master plan completed; permitting begun
- Month 12: Construction contracts finalized; financing closed
- Month 18: Construction begins
- End 2027: 100–150 units operational; senior living LOI signed
- End 2028: 200–300 units operational

**Compensation & Incentives**
- Base Salary: $60K–$75K
- Performance Bonus: $10K if $8M+ financing secured by Month 6
- Professional Development: $3K/year (LIHTC training, USDA RD workshops)

**Who Should Hire This Person?**

The ideal Housing Development Consultant is:
- 10+ years affordable housing development or finance experience
- Deep knowledge of LIHTC syndication, USDA RD programs, state/federal housing grants
- Track record closing housing projects ($10M–$50M investment range)
- Relationships with LIHTC syndicators, USDA RD loan officers, state housing officials
- Understanding of mixed-income development, senior living, and workforce housing

**Example of Ideal Profile:** A former development officer at a housing nonprofit, or a consultant who has closed 3–5 LIHTC projects in similar-sized markets.

**How Will This Person Change the City?**

If successful, the Housing Consultant delivers:
- $8–12M financing for workforce housing
- 200–300 units built or under construction by end 2027
- 50–100 construction jobs
- Permanent housing stock for workers attracted by data center, FDI, and other job growth
- Senior living campus development (additional 200–300 units)
- Property tax base expansion
- Community stability (families stay, schools grow, retail strengthens)
