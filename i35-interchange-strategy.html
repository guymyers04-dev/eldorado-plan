I-35 Interchange Strategy

El Dorado's Southern Gateway to Wichita Metro

Positioning the 1st and 2nd I-35 exits from Wichita as a regional logistics hub, truck stop corridor, and commercial anchor

Why I-35 Matters: Regional Connectivity & Economic Position

The Opportunity

I-35 is Kansas's primary north-south commercial corridor, connecting the Oklahoma border, Kansas City, and beyond. El Dorado's position—45 miles south of Wichita—places it as the gateway to the region's largest metropolitan area.

Current reality: The 1st and 2nd exits from Wichita (near El Dorado) are underutilized compared to their potential.

Strategic vision: Transform these interchanges into Wichita's southern truck stop corridor and logistics service center, capturing $30–50M in commercial investment over 15 years.

📊 Key Metrics

  • I-35 Daily Traffic: ~12,000–15,000 vehicles/day (KDOT estimate)
  • Commercial Vehicle %: ~25–30% trucks
  • Exit Spacing: 10–12 miles between exits (optimal for truck stops)
  • Wichita Distance: 45 miles (1-hour drive)
  • Regional Market: 600,000+ people in Wichita MSA
  • Comparable Exit Value: $5–8M annual revenue per full-service truck stop

Competitive Context: Why El Dorado vs. Emporia/Newton?

Three exits compete for I-35 traffic in central Kansas:

Exit Distance from Wichita City Size Current Infrastructure Growth Potential
El Dorado (Exit 79/81) 45 miles 12,800 Limited truck services 🟢 HIGH
Emporia (Exit 127) 75 miles 24,400 Established truck stops 🟡 MEDIUM
Newton (Exit 30) 25 miles 18,500 Heavy competition 🔴 LOW

Analysis: El Dorado occupies the "Goldilocks zone"—close enough to Wichita for regular service traffic, far enough from Newton/Emporia to avoid direct competition, and positioned for growth.

15-Year Vision: From Underutilized Exits to Regional Truck Stop Corridor

Phase 1: Foundation (Years 1–3)

Goal: Establish El Dorado as a primary truck stop destination.

  • Recruit/develop 1 full-service truck stop (TA/Petro, Love's, Pilot) with fuel, restaurant, showers, parking
  • Improve interchange exit ramps (safety, signage, ease of access)
  • Establish truck-friendly services: mechanic shops, tire services, quick-lube facilities
  • Target investment: $8–12M
  • Expected jobs: 80–120 direct, 200+ indirect

Phase 2: Expansion (Years 4–7)

Goal: Build a complete commercial services ecosystem.

  • Develop secondary commercial node: quick-serve restaurants (QSR), convenience stores, retail
  • Create micro-industrial park for logistics/distribution services
  • Potential: small hotel/motel (50–75 rooms) for long-haul driver layovers
  • Target investment: $12–18M (cumulative)
  • Expected jobs: 250–350 cumulative

Phase 3: Integration (Years 8–15)

Goal: Connect interchange corridor to city center; create integrated economic ecosystem.

  • Develop arterial corridors linking I-35 exits to downtown / industrial areas
  • Potential 3PL (third-party logistics) headquarters or regional distribution center
  • Tourism overlay: "Welcome to El Dorado" branding, regional gateway positioning
  • Target investment: $18–25M (cumulative)
  • Expected jobs: 450–600 cumulative (direct + indirect)

💡 The "Welcome Corridor" Concept

Rather than isolated truck stops, position the I-35 exits as a coordinated gateway experience:

  • Signage: "Welcome to El Dorado — Home of [anchor industry: Precision Agriculture / Advanced Manufacturing]"
  • Branded Services: El Dorado Truck Stop with local coffee, local products, regional tourism info
  • Safety: Well-lit, well-maintained exits; good sightlines; modern infrastructure
  • Experience: More than transactional—create reasons to stop and stay (restaurants, rest areas, attractions)

Economic Impact: Jobs, Tax Base & Regional Multiplier Effects

Direct Economic Benefits

  • Tax Base Growth: $18–25M cumulative business investment = $45K–65K annual tax revenue
  • Employment: 450–600 jobs (truck stop staff, mechanics, logistics, retail, hospitality)
  • Payroll: $18–22M annually (at maturity, Year 15)
  • Sales Tax: $2–3M annually (fuel, retail, restaurant sales)

Regional Multiplier Effects

  • Supply Chain: Truck stops source from local suppliers (food, maintenance, utilities)
  • Hospitality Spin-off: Increased travelers = demand for local restaurants, lodging, attractions
  • Logistics Agglomeration: Truck stops attract distribution centers, 3PL companies, warehouse operators
  • Total Economic Impact (3x multiplier): ~$60–75M cumulative by 2040

📈 Comparable Case Study: Emporia I-35 Corridor

Emporia's I-35 exits (Exits 127–133) generate an estimated $12–15M annual economic impact, with 300+ direct jobs and 600+ indirect jobs. El Dorado, with less competition and better positioning, could achieve 60–70% of Emporia's impact.

What Needs to Happen: Stakeholder Roles & Timeline

🏛️ City of El Dorado

Action Items:

  • Adopt "I-35 Corridor Development Strategy" (planning document)
  • Rezone interchange areas for commercial/industrial use (if needed)
  • Coordinate with County Commission on utility extensions
  • Identify potential truck stop operators; issue RFP
  • Timeline: 6–12 months (Year 1)

🛣️ Kansas Department of Transportation (KDOT)

Action Items:

  • Assess exit ramp safety and signage
  • Coordinate with Wichita MPO on I-35 corridor maintenance
  • Support local improvements (access roads, traffic flow)
  • Timeline: Ongoing (coordinate with City plans)

💼 Private Sector (Truck Stop Operators)

Action Items:

  • Evaluate El Dorado I-35 exits for truck stop development
  • Negotiate lease/purchase agreements with City or private landowners
  • Develop site plans, environmental assessments, operating plans
  • Timeline: 12–18 months (Years 1–2)

📊 Butler County Commission

Action Items:

  • Support City planning efforts; coordinate on county infrastructure
  • Extend utility services (water, sewer) if needed
  • Provide information on land availability and ownership
  • Timeline: Coordination role (Years 1–3)

🎯 Immediate Priority Actions (Next 12 Months)

  1. Phase 1a (Months 1–3): City Commission approves I-35 Corridor Strategy; identifies preferred sites
  2. Phase 1b (Months 4–6): Issue RFP to truck stop operators; begin site assessments
  3. Phase 1c (Months 7–12): Select operator; negotiate lease/development agreement; begin design
  4. Phase 2 (Year 2–3): Construct primary truck stop; open for business
