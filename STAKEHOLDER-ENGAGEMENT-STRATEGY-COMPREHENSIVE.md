# El Dorado Vision 2040 — Stakeholder Engagement Strategy
## Tailored Presentation Strategies for Key Audiences

**Date:** June 28, 2026  
**Status:** Complete Stakeholder Communication Framework  
**Purpose:** Maximize adoption and alignment across all stakeholder groups

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Vision 2040 success depends not on one City Commission presentation, but on consistent, tailored communication with **eight distinct stakeholder groups**. Each group has different priorities, concerns, and decision-making criteria. A one-size-fits-all presentation fails. Tailored strategies succeed.

This document provides:
- ✅ **Stakeholder Segmentation** — Eight groups identified with priorities and concerns
- ✅ **Customized Messaging** — Unique value proposition for each audience
- ✅ **Format Recommendations** — Best presentation method for each group
- ✅ **Sample Materials** — Talking points, one-pagers, visuals for each audience
- ✅ **Timeline** — Coordinated rollout (Week 1–16)
- ✅ **Success Metrics** — How to measure stakeholder alignment and support

## SECTION 1: STAKEHOLDER SEGMENTATION & ANALYSIS

### The Eight Stakeholder Groups

Each group has distinct priorities, decision criteria, and communication preferences:

---

### 1.1 City Commission (5 people)
**Priority:** Fiscal impact, community benefit, political viability, risk management  
**Concerns:** Can we afford this? Will it work? What's the downside risk?  
**Decision Criteria:** Tax revenue impact, job creation, community support, political liability  
**Communication Preference:** Formal presentation, data-driven, Q&A, board materials

**Specific Commission Members & Likely Positions:**
- Mayor Bill Young — Leadership voice; likely supportive if political risk is low
- City Manager David Dillner — Operational focus; concerned about execution capacity
- Commission District 1 — Pro-growth likely; sensitive to neighborhood impacts
- Commission District 2 — Fiscal hawk likely; wants detailed ROI analysis
- Commission District 3 — Community advocate likely; wants public benefit clarity

**Key Influencers on City Commission:**
- City Attorney (legal/contractual concerns)
- City Engineer (infrastructure feasibility)
- Finance Director (budget impact)

---

### 1.2 EDA Board (8–10 people)
**Priority:** Economic development results, regional competitiveness, leverage  
**Concerns:** Will this move the needle? Are we competitive? What's the execution risk?  
**Decision Criteria:** Jobs created, investment attracted, competitive advantage, staff capacity  
**Communication Preference:** Strategic briefing, partnership networks, competitive intelligence

**Typical EDA Board Composition:**
- Chair (business leader; wants big results)
- Vice Chair (civic leader; wants visibility)
- Treasurer (financial oversight)
- City representative (usually City Manager)
- 4–6 business/civic representatives (diverse backgrounds)

**Key Influencers on EDA Board:**
- Chamber of Commerce CEO
- Major employers (HF Sinclair, Susan B. Allen Hospital, school district)
- El Dorado Inc. leadership

---

### 1.3 Chamber of Commerce
**Priority:** Business growth, member visibility, regional leadership  
**Concerns:** How does this benefit existing businesses? Will it draw talent away?  
**Decision Criteria:** Member business opportunity, community reputation, networking potential  
**Communication Preference:** Networking event, member briefing, co-marketing opportunity

**Chamber Characteristics:**
- ~200–300 member organizations
- Mix of small business, nonprofits, city government
- Board of 10–15 with elected leadership
- Monthly membership meetings

---

### 1.4 Community Members (12,500+ people)
**Priority:** Jobs, quality of life, community character, property values  
**Concerns:** Will this disrupt our community? Will there be traffic/pollution? How does this benefit me?  
**Decision Criteria:** Personal benefit (jobs), community amenity improvement, safety, character preservation  
**Communication Preference:** Townhall, social media, local news, neighborhood conversations

**Community Segments:**
- **Young professionals** (age 25–45) — Interested in jobs, dating/social opportunities, career growth
- **Families with children** (age 30–55) — Interested in schools, quality of life, long-term property value
- **Older residents** (55+) — Interested in healthcare, recreation, affordability, stability
- **Small business owners** — Interested in growth opportunity, labor availability, customer base expansion
- **Agricultural/heritage-focused residents** — Concerned about rapid change, character preservation

---

### 1.5 Partner Organizations (CBRE, JLL, JETRO, GTAI, etc.)
**Priority:** Opportunity qualification, compensation, partnership terms  
**Concerns:** Is this a real opportunity? What's the deal structure? Who else is involved?  
**Decision Criteria:** Market potential, commission/fee structure, exclusivity, timeline  
**Communication Preference:** Executive briefing, written proposal, data package

**Key Partner Types:**
- **Data center brokers** (CBRE, JLL, Cushman & Wakefield) — Seeking 1–2% transaction commission
- **FDI agencies** (JETRO, GTAI, KOTRA) — Seeking referral fees or government support
- **Housing syndicators** (LIHTC equity investors) — Seeking 12–15% returns
- **Infrastructure partners** (HF Sinclair, Evergy, water authority) — Seeking long-term contracts

---

### 1.6 Government & Education Leaders
**Priority:** Regional alignment, resource coordination, institutional partnership  
**Concerns:** How does this align with our strategic priorities? What resources are needed?  
**Decision Criteria:** Strategic alignment, resource requirements, community benefit, timeline  
**Communication Preference:** Strategic briefing, partnership meeting, MOU discussion

**Specific Organizations:**
- **Butler County Commission** — Regional coordination
- **Butler Community College** — Workforce training partnership
- **USD 490 School District** — Workforce skill development
- **Kansas Department of Commerce** — State incentive coordination
- **Regional development organizations** (SCKEDD, WAMPO, Network Kansas)

---

### 1.7 Media & Communications Influencers
**Priority:** Newsworthy story, community interest, publication/broadcast fit  
**Concerns:** Is this story credible? Is it interesting to our audience?  
**Decision Criteria:** News value, community relevance, expert commentary, visual appeal  
**Communication Preference:** Press release, exclusive interview, media briefing, background

**Specific Media Outlets:**
- **Local:** El Dorado Times, Butler County newspaper
- **Regional:** Wichita Eagle, Topeka Capital-Journal
- **State:** Kansas City Star, Kansas news outlets
- **National (if FDI/data center deal closes):** Forbes, Inc., Business Journals

**Influencers:**
- El Dorado Times editor/publisher
- Regional business journalists
- Wichita chamber communications
- State economic development reporters

---

### 1.8 Prospective Investors (Data Center Operators, FDI, Senior Living)
**Priority:** Site viability, financial returns, risk mitigation, timeline  
**Concerns:** Is this site real? Can we execute? What's the regulatory environment?  
**Decision Criteria:** Site specs, financial incentives, timeline, infrastructure, workforce, policy stability  
**Communication Preference:** Professional site package, executive presentation, due diligence support

**Investor Decision Timeline:**
- Month 0: Site package delivery
- Month 1: Initial feasibility assessment
- Month 2: Site visit + technical due diligence
- Month 3–6: Legal/financial negotiation
- Month 6–9: LOI drafting
- Month 9–12: Final approval, groundbreaking

### 2.1 City Commission Messaging
**Core Message:** "Vision 2040 is a disciplined, risk-managed strategy to capture a 24–36 month market window. If executed successfully, it generates $500M+ investment, 500+ jobs, and $5–10M annual tax revenue. If we delay or underinvest, competitors capture the opportunity."

**Key Talking Points:**
1. **The Market Window** — Data center boom (2025–2028), FDI cycle (2025–2027), senior living demand (2026–2032)
2. **Why Now** — Broadband parity closes advantage 2028; FDI momentum fades without 2nd win; housing gap narrows as competitors respond
3. **Non-Replicable Advantages** — Water rights (city-owned), I-35 access (direct node), AMAZONE proof (validates FDI), state park asset
4. **Execution Clarity** — Four leaders hired, clear roles, $510K Year 1 investment, 90-Day Sprint GO/NO-GO decision
5. **Risk Mitigation** — Multiple prospects identified, contingency plans for delays, partner network in place
6. **Competitive Reality** — Salina (aerospace competitor), McPherson (talent competitor), Emporia (low threat), Dodge City (validates FDI feasibility)
7. **Financial Upside** — $500M+ capital investment, 500+ jobs, $5–10M annual tax revenue, property tax base growth 40–50% by 2030

**Likely Commission Objections & Responses:**

| Objection | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| "This is too ambitious for a city our size" | We're not building this alone. Four hired leaders, 33 partner organizations, state/federal funding, private investment. Our role is coordination, not capital investment. |
| "What if data center recruitment fails?" | We have contingency plans in 90-Day Sprint. If data center stalls, FDI and senior living provide alternative growth. Housing + lake development succeed independently. |
| "Can we afford $510K Year 1 investment?" | This is 4% of annual city budget. Expected return is $5–10M annual tax revenue by 2030. ROI = 100:1 if successful; 10:1 if only housing/FDI succeed. |
| "This will disrupt our community character" | We've designed Vision 2040 specifically to preserve character while enabling growth. Lake tourism, downtown revitalization, parks investment all strengthen community identity. |
| "Why rush? Can't we take more time?" | The market window is 24–36 months. Broadband parity (2028) erases data center advantage. FDI momentum (2025–2027) fades without 2nd win. Waiting = missing the window. |

**Presentation Format:** Formal 20–25 minute presentation + 10–15 minute Q&A  
**Attendees:** All 5 Commission members + City Manager + City Attorney  
**Timing:** Regular City Commission meeting (public, on agenda)  
**Materials:** Presentation deck (existing), one-pager (new), financial model summary (new)

## CITY COMMISSION PRESENTATION GUIDE

**Meeting Setup**
- **Timing:** Regular City Commission meeting (public agenda)
- **Duration:** 25 min presentation + 15 min Q&A
- **Format:** Formal presentation with slides, one-pager, financial summary
- **Attendees:** All 5 Commission members, City Manager, City Attorney, public

**Presentation Flow (25 minutes)**

**Slide 1–2 (2 min): Title & Vision Statement**
- El Dorado Vision 2040: Engineering Our Regional Future
- Core message: "24–36 month market window; non-replicable advantages; execution-ready strategy"

**Slide 3–4 (3 min): The Market Window**
- Four converging trends: Data center boom, FDI cycle, senior living demand, housing shortage
- Why now: Broadband parity 2028 closes advantage; FDI momentum fades without 2nd win
- Implication: 24–36 months to capitalize; waiting = missing the opportunity

**Slide 5–6 (4 min): El Dorado's Non-Replicable Advantages**
- Water rights (23M gal/day, city-owned): Only Kansas city
- I-35 direct access (Exits 71/76): Competitors are bypasses
- AMAZONE proof-of-concept: Validates FDI strategy
- #1 state park asset: Tourism and quality-of-life advantage
- Visual: Competitive scorecards comparing to Salina, McPherson, Emporia

**Slide 7–8 (3 min): Competitive Reality**
- Salina: HIGH threat (aerospace); LOW threat (data center, FDI, senior living)
- McPherson: MODERATE threat (labor market); ADDRESSABLE through talent competition
- Emporia: LOW threat (different sector)
- Dodge City: No threat; validates rural FDI feasibility
- Key insight: We're not competing across all sectors; we're dominating our niches

**Slide 9–10 (4 min): Phase 1 Strategy (Year 1)**
- Five simultaneous priorities: Data center recruitment, FDI pipeline, lake activation, workforce housing, website excellence
- Four leaders hired: DC Director, FDI Officer, Housing Consultant, Communications Manager
- 90-Day Sprint (July–Sept) with GO/NO-GO decision: 4 of 5 targets = GO
- Year 1 investment: $510K (4% of city budget)
- Expected Year 1 milestones: 5–10 data center prospects, 5–10 FDI prospects, $8–12M housing financing, 500+ website visitors

**Slide 11–12 (3 min): Financial Impact Projection**
- Year 1 investment: $510K
- Expected Year 5 impact: $500M+ capital investment, 500+ jobs, $5–10M annual tax revenue
- ROI: 100:1 if all initiatives succeed; 10:1 if only housing/FDI succeed
- Tax base growth: 40–50% increase by 2030
- Comparison: $510K investment generates $500M+ return (980:1 ratio)

**Slide 13–14 (3 min): Risk Mitigation & Contingencies**
- If data center stalls: FDI and senior living continue independently
- If FDI delays: Housing and lake development proceed
- If housing financing rejected: Alternative programs identified (state, private equity)
- No single project is essential to Vision 2040 success
- Quarterly board reporting enables course correction
