Executive Summary

El Dorado, Kansas sits at an inflection point shared by dozens of small American cities within commuting distance of a major metro: it can continue on a trajectory of gradual demographic decline, or it can make deliberate, coordinated investments that reverse the trend. This document provides detailed project-level briefs for the fourteen major initiative areas comprising Vision 2040 — the city's 15-year comprehensive improvement plan.

The plan is grounded in three principles. First, improve what already exists before building new — the city's pool, athletic complex, golf course, hospital campus, and airport are real assets that cost less to upgrade than to replace. Second, leverage private and federal capital at every stage so that public dollars act as catalysts rather than sole funding sources. Third, sequence investments so that each phase creates the conditions for the next — infrastructure before development, development before amenity, amenity before tourism.

The fourteen briefs that follow are written as planning-level summaries, not engineering specifications. They are intended to inform public discussion, communicate the scale and rationale of each initiative, and provide a basis for feasibility studies, grant applications, and community engagement processes.

Brief 01 · Priority Development Zone

North Gateway District Phase 1–2

Estimated Investment: ~$95M combined public/private · Timeline: 2026–2031

The North Gateway District is El Dorado's most significant undeveloped opportunity — a corridor of agricultural and low-density commercial land stretching between the north end of Central Avenue and the western shore of El Dorado Lake. This gap has long functioned as a void between the city's two greatest assets. The North Gateway plan proposes to fill it with a cohesive urban village that connects downtown to the lake by foot and bicycle, anchored by 300 units of walkable housing, a full-service marina, and a satellite Butler CC facility.

The strategic logic is straightforward: El Dorado Lake is the city's most powerful quality-of-life asset, but it is effectively disconnected from downtown by a mile of underutilized land and an inadequate access road. A resident or visitor cannot reasonably walk from Central Avenue to the lakeshore — the road is narrow, there are no sidewalks or shade, and there is nothing to see or do along the way. The North Gateway plan transforms this corridor into a destination in its own right, making the lake feel like an extension of downtown rather than a separate place you drive to.

Key Components

  • 300-unit residential neighborhood — mixed types: single-family, townhomes, and courtyard apartments arranged in a traditional block structure with front porches, alley-loaded garages, and a village commercial node. Phase 1 targets 150 units by 2029.
  • West Side Marina Complex — 150-slip full-service marina on the western lake shore, including fuel dock, ship's store, electric boat charging, pump-out station, and wave attenuator breakwater. Managed by a private operator under a 25-year city lease with no ongoing operating cost to the city.
  • North Shore Boat Storage Campus — 250 units of covered, enclosed, and open boat storage on city-leased land adjacent to the marina. This addresses a specific market gap: Butler County boaters currently store their vessels outside the county because there is no comparable facility locally.
  • North Gateway Linear Park — 1.5-mile greenway spine connecting the residential neighborhood to the lake shore, with paved trail, pocket parks every 500 feet, native prairie plantings, and stormwater bioswales integrated into the park design.
  • Butler CC North Gateway Satellite — 15,000-square-foot facility housing marine mechanics, building trades, outdoor recreation management, and landscape/horticulture certificate programs, co-located with the village commercial node.
  • Lake Access Road Upgrade — Widen North Lake Road from a narrow residential street to a 4-lane collector road with sidewalks, protected bike lane, and LED lighting. This single improvement unlocks the entire district by making access safe and predictable.

Fiscal Approach

The North Gateway District is primarily privately financed — the housing, marina, boat storage, amenity hub, and RV camping are all structured as private development on city-owned or privately held land. The public investment is confined to the access road, linear park, utility extensions, and Fire Station 2 (addressed separately). A North Gateway TIF District captures the property tax increment generated by new development over 20 years, repaying those public infrastructure costs without drawing on the general fund or raising the mill levy.

Brief 02 · Economic Development

Flint Hills Energy Corridor Phase 1–2

Estimated Investment: ~$80M private · Jobs: 350+ · Timeline: 2025–2030

Butler County receives some of the strongest sustained wind resources in Kansas, and solar irradiance in the region rivals the southwestern United States. These are not planning abstractions — they are measurable physical advantages that renewable energy manufacturers, battery storage companies, and grid infrastructure firms actively seek when selecting sites for new facilities. El Dorado's north industrial corridor, already zoned I-1 Light Industrial and served by existing Evergy transmission infrastructure, is positioned to capture this demand.

The Flint Hills Energy Corridor proposes the development of a 500-acre renewable energy industrial park on the city's north side. The park is not a solar array or a wind farm — it is a manufacturing and assembly destination for companies that make the components of the clean energy economy: wind turbine towers and nacelles, solar panel assembly, battery storage systems, and grid modernization equipment. These are heavy industrial operations requiring large, flat parcels, reliable power (already present via Evergy), highway access (US-77 directly adjacent), and proximity to a skilled trades workforce pipeline — all of which El Dorado already has through Butler CC's existing welding, electrical, and HVAC programs.

The Role of HF Sinclair

The Flint Hills Energy Corridor is not positioned as a replacement for El Dorado's oil heritage — it is its logical evolution. HF Sinclair's El Dorado Refinery, operating since 1917, represents the institutional knowledge, safety culture, and workforce infrastructure that makes El Dorado competitive for energy-sector manufacturers. The refinery workforce contains exactly the mechanical, electrical, and process control skill sets that renewable energy manufacturers need. The partnership with HF Sinclair ensures that as the energy sector transitions, El Dorado's workforce transitions with it rather than being left behind.

Federal Funding Opportunity The Inflation Reduction Act's Advanced Manufacturing Production Credit (45X) and the Department of Energy's Loan Programs Office both fund domestic clean energy manufacturing at scale. A company locating a wind component manufacturing facility in El Dorado may qualify for 30–45% of capital costs through these programs — dramatically improving the investment economics and making El Dorado competitive with sites in states offering larger direct subsidies.

Implementation Sequence

  1. Secure EDA Public Works grant for industrial site preparation (5 shovel-ready pads, 5–50 acres each)
  2. Engage KDOC and Kansas Department of Commerce for site marketing to renewable energy manufacturers
  3. Partner with Butler CC to establish Energy Workforce Academy certifications aligned to target employers
  4. Negotiate Evergy substation expansion commitment as anchor condition for first tenant
  5. Offer 10-year personal property tax abatement on qualifying manufacturing equipment to first anchor
Brief 03 · Lake Infrastructure

West Side Marina Complex Phase 1

Estimated Investment: $9M public · Revenue: $2M+/yr · Timeline: 2027–2029

The existing east marina at El Dorado Lake has 80 slips and minimal amenities. It is difficult to access from downtown, undersized for current demand, and located on the opposite side of the lake from the city — effectively invisible to a visitor arriving from the US-54/77 junction. The west side marina proposes to correct all three of these problems by establishing a full-service 150-slip facility on the western lake shore, directly accessible from the North Gateway District and visible from the new North Lake Road corridor.

The business case is straightforward: Clinton Lake Marina in Lawrence, Kansas — a directly comparable facility serving a similarly sized regional market — generates more than $2 million annually in marina revenues and directly supports an estimated $8 million in annual visitor spending at surrounding businesses. El Dorado Lake is geographically larger than Clinton Lake and serves a denser regional population (Wichita metro, 650,000+). The market exists. The infrastructure does not.

Operating Structure

The city constructs the dock infrastructure, wave attenuator, access road, parking, and utility connections. A private marina operator selected through competitive RFP manages all operations under a 25-year land lease, paying the city an annual lease fee and a percentage of gross revenue (projected 8–12%). The city bears no ongoing operating cost after construction. The operator bears all staffing, maintenance, and equipment risk. This is the same structure used successfully at Cheney Reservoir Marina and Milford Lake Marina in Kansas.

Brief 04 · Tourism Development

El Dorado Lake Tourism Strategy Phase 2–3

Estimated Total Investment: $55M+ · Visitor Target: 210,000/yr by 2040

El Dorado Lake covers 8,400 acres and sits within 45 minutes of a metro area of 650,000 people. It has a functioning state park, active fishing and boating communities, and established camping facilities. It is also almost entirely unknown outside Butler County — there is no lakeside dining, no boutique lodging, no amphitheater, no circumnavigation trail, and no cohesive visitor experience beyond what the state park provides. That is not a disadvantage; it is an opportunity at the very beginning of its development arc.

The lake tourism strategy is organized around a simple sequence: first establish the physical infrastructure that gives visitors a reason to come (marina, trail, improved fishing access), then develop the amenity layer that gives them a reason to stay overnight (glamping, commercial strip, boutique inn), then build the event programming that gives them a reason to return repeatedly (amphitheater, tournament series, music festival). Each layer supports and subsidizes the next.

The Amphitheater Case

A 3,000-seat outdoor lakefront amphitheater is the single project with the highest economic multiplier on this list. A venue of this scale, hosting 20+ ticketed events per year, generates direct ticket revenue, but more importantly generates the hotel nights, restaurant covers, gas station fill-ups, and retail sales that cascade through the local economy during each event weekend. Comparable venues in small Kansas and Oklahoma markets — the COOP Ale Works Stage in Norman, OK (5,000 seats), and the Cotillion in Wichita — demonstrate that regional touring acts in the $25,000–$100,000 range reliably fill venues of this size when the setting and parking experience are good. A lakefront stage with a floating performance platform on El Dorado Lake would be a genuinely unique venue in this region.

KDWPT Concession Strategy The glamping village, eco-cabins, and lake commercial amenities are proposed entirely as private concession operations within or adjacent to El Dorado State Park. Kansas Department of Wildlife, Parks and Tourism already operates a concession program at several state parks — the private operator builds and runs the facility; the state receives a revenue share (typically 8–15% of gross). This approach delivers $3.5M in glamping infrastructure at zero cost to the city or state, with ongoing revenue sharing.
Brief 05 · Urban Core

Downtown Revitalization & Street Network Phase 1–3

Estimated Investment: $22M public + $18M private · Timeline: Ongoing through 2035

El Dorado's downtown core along Central Avenue has assets that most small cities would pay to replicate: a walkable grid, a collection of intact Victorian commercial buildings with ground-floor storefront windows, a world-class oil heritage museum, and a central location that is equidistant from the lake and the interstate. The problem is not the bones — the problem is activation. Too many storefronts are vacant, too many upper floors are unused, and the street itself is designed for vehicles passing through rather than people stopping to spend money.

The revitalization strategy operates on three simultaneous fronts: the physical street environment, the building stock, and the business ecosystem. All three must improve together — a beautiful street with empty storefronts is as dead as a building full of businesses with no parking or sidewalk. The Central Avenue Complete Street conversion, the facade improvement program, and the small business incubator are not independent projects. They are one integrated strategy executed in phases.

Central Avenue Complete Street Conversion — Road Diet

Central Avenue currently carries two or more through lanes in each direction depending on block, functioning primarily as a vehicle throughway. Drivers have no reason to slow down, park, or stop — the street design communicates "keep moving." The proposed Road Diet converts Central Avenue from a throughway to a destination by reducing vehicle lanes and reallocating the recovered pavement width to pedestrians, cyclists, and street trees.

Side Street Traffic Routing

The critical companion to the Road Diet is deliberate routing of through traffic to parallel streets. Removing two travel lanes from Central Avenue without providing an alternative route simply redistributes congestion; routing through traffic to parallel streets removes it from the pedestrian environment entirely.

  • North Parallel Route (one block north of Central): Designated one-way westbound during business hours (7 AM–9 PM). Handles eastbound-to-westbound through traffic that previously used Central Avenue. 45-degree angled parking added on north side, increasing parking supply on this street by ~40 spaces.
  • South Parallel Route (one block south of Central): Designated one-way eastbound during business hours. Handles westbound-to-eastbound through traffic. Same angled parking treatment.
  • Commercial Vehicle Restriction: Trucks over 10,000 GVWR prohibited on Central Avenue from 7 AM–9 PM. Delivery and loading permitted only at designated side-street loading zones with posted time windows (6 AM–9 AM, 30-minute maximum). Verified through signage and, in Phase 3, automated plate-reading enforcement.
  • Emergency Vehicle Access: Center turn lane maintained for emergency vehicle routing. Fire and EMS response times are not affected by the conversion — confirmed in comparable city implementations in Emporia and Lawrence, KS.
What the Research Shows The FHWA has documented 4-to-3 lane Road Diet outcomes in 23 comparable applications. Average results: vehicle through-speeds reduce from 35+ mph to 22–26 mph; pedestrian crossing distances and exposure times decrease; crash rates drop 29–47%; and adjacent retail sales increase 15–25% within 3 years of completion. In Emporia, KS — directly comparable in size and street type to El Dorado — the Commercial Street road diet resulted in 8 new businesses opening within 18 months and measurable sales tax revenue increases in the affected corridor.

Capital Flow Strategy — Moving Money Through Downtown

Physical improvements create the conditions for economic activity; they do not guarantee it. The following mechanisms are designed to move capital into and through downtown businesses in a self-reinforcing cycle:

1. Business Improvement District (BID)

Downtown property owners collectively self-assess at $0.15 per square foot of commercial floor area per year. For a typical downtown building with 3,000 square feet of commercial space, this is $450/year — less than the cost of one month's advertising. The BID generates approximately $85,000 per year in dedicated downtown funding for shared marketing, event programming, sidewalk maintenance, holiday lighting, and a downtown manager position. Critically, BID funds are controlled by the property owners themselves through an elected board — not by the city — which makes business owners stakeholders in downtown's success rather than passive recipients of city programs.

2. "First Door" Vacancy Activation Grant

Any business opening in a storefront that has been vacant for 18 or more months receives a $5,000 city grant, no strings attached. The grant is funded by the downtown TIF district. At an estimated 10–12 activations per year, total program cost is $50,000–$60,000 annually — less than the cost of one city employee. The behavioral economics research on vacancy is clear: clusters of vacant storefronts signal disinvestment and deter further investment; a single active store adjacent to a vacancy changes the perception of the entire block. The First Door grant accelerates the cascade from vacant to occupied.

3. Landlord Tenant Improvement Matching Program

Many downtown El Dorado storefronts are vacant not because there is no demand, but because building owners will not invest in tenant improvements — the upfront cost of upgrading a 1940s commercial space to meet a restaurant or retail tenant's needs can exceed $50,000, and amateur landlords are unwilling to risk that capital on an unproven tenant. The city matches 50 cents per dollar that a downtown property owner invests in a qualified tenant improvement allowance, up to $25,000 per project, for any lease signed with a net-new business (not a relocated existing El Dorado business). This costs the city a maximum of $25,000 per activation — and unlocks $50,000 in private investment that would otherwise not occur.

4. Food and Beverage Anchor Strategy

Retail follows restaurants — not the other way around. Every successful downtown revitalization in a small Kansas city has been led by 3–5 independent sit-down restaurants generating consistent lunch and dinner pedestrian traffic that then sustains adjacent retail. El Dorado's downtown currently lacks the restaurant density to anchor any other retail. The incubator's ground-floor food hall is designed to seed this category with 4–6 food concepts at below-market rents, graduating successful operators to permanent Central Avenue locations once they have proven their model. The $0 rent in months 1–6 (and below-market rent in months 7–24) subsidizes the risk of opening a restaurant in an unproven downtown environment — the subsidy ends once the environment is no longer unproven.

5. Retail Void Analysis & Targeted Recruitment

El Dorado's trade area — the geographic area from which the downtown draws shoppers — contains approximately 35,000 people when Butler County commute patterns are factored in. A professional retail void analysis identifies which categories have demand within that trade area that is currently being satisfied in Wichita or Augusta rather than in El Dorado. Common voids in comparable markets include: specialty food and grocery, hardware/home goods, children's clothing and toys, and personal services (hair, nail, fitness). Targeted recruitment to fill these voids — led by the Chamber of Commerce with SBDC technical support — reduces the risk for incoming businesses and increases the probability of survival.

6. Parking Revenue Recycling

All parking revenue collected in downtown El Dorado — on-street meter revenue, permit fees, city lot daily fees — is deposited into a dedicated Downtown Improvement Fund rather than the city's general fund. This creates a direct connection between downtown use and downtown reinvestment: as more people visit downtown and generate parking revenue, more money flows back into the streetscape, events, and business support programs that attract the next wave of visitors. At current and projected downtown activity levels, parking revenue recycling generates $40,000–$80,000 per year in dedicated improvement funds at no additional cost to taxpayers.

Brief 06 · Residential Development

Housing Strategy — Full Spectrum Phase 1–4

Estimated Units: 1,200+ total · Estimated Private Investment: $140M+

A city cannot grow its economy without growing its housing supply. Every industrial employer, healthcare expansion, and tourism development in this plan creates jobs; those jobs require workers; those workers require housing; and the absence of adequate, appropriate housing is the most common reason economic development deals fall apart after the announcement. El Dorado's housing pipeline has been stagnant for a decade. Vision 2040 targets 1,200+ net new units by 2040 across four distinct product types that together serve the full spectrum of the workforce the plan aims to attract and retain.

West Gateway Workforce Housing (280 units)

Targeting the 1,500+ Butler County residents who commute daily to Wichita and live in El Dorado specifically because of its relative affordability. This market exists and is growing — it is currently underserved because the city's housing stock is aging and the supply of move-in-ready homes in the $150,000–$220,000 range is chronically inadequate. Kansas Housing Resources Corporation financing (LIHTC for the affordable component, HOME for the gap) makes this project feasible at price points that the Wichita commuter workforce can afford.

Lakeside Village (400 units)

The premium tier — waterfront and near-waterfront residential on the western lake shore, targeting remote workers, retirees relocating to Kansas, and Wichita professionals seeking a lake lifestyle at a fraction of Midwest lake-home prices. These units generate the highest property tax revenue per acre of any housing in the plan and create the permanent residential population that sustains the lake-area commercial amenities.

Downtown Loft District (60–80 units)

Adaptive reuse of vacant upper floors on Central Avenue buildings — the lowest-cost per-unit housing investment in the plan because the building structures already exist. Kansas Historic Preservation Tax Credits (25%) stacked with federal Historic Tax Credits (20%) make the financial structure viable for private developers. Downtown residents are the most valuable customers a downtown commercial district can have: they shop, eat, and spend locally seven days a week rather than just during events.

Walnut River Senior Campus (120 units)

Independent living, assisted living, and memory care on the Walnut River greenway corridor. Rural Kansas is critically undersupplied in senior housing — Butler County's senior population is growing faster than the state average as the baby boom cohort ages. This project fills a genuine need while generating 40+ permanent healthcare support jobs and freeing up existing single-family homes (which senior residents vacate upon moving to the campus) for the workforce housing market.

Brief 07 · Backbone Systems

Infrastructure Modernization Phase 1–2

Estimated Investment: ~$77M · Federal Cost Share: 50–90% on most projects

Infrastructure is the least visible and most consequential investment a city makes. The fiber network, water system, flood mitigation, and power infrastructure proposed in Vision 2040 are not amenities — they are the prerequisites for every other initiative in this plan. A data center cannot locate in a city without a high-capacity electrical substation. A residential developer cannot build in the North Gateway corridor without a water main extension. A remote worker will not move to El Dorado without gigabit broadband. These systems must come first.

Citywide Gigabit Fiber Network — $12–16M

Fiber-to-the-premises for all 5,200 El Dorado addresses. The federal BEAD Program (NTIA) covers 50–70% of eligible construction costs for unserved and underserved communities. El Dorado's current broadband landscape — cable-based service with no fiber option for residential addresses — makes it a strong BEAD applicant. The fiber network is the single highest-leverage infrastructure investment available to the city: broadband access is the #1 factor cited by remote workers when choosing a small city to relocate to, and the #2 factor (after power) cited by light industrial employers in site selection.

Water Treatment Plant — $22M

El Dorado's water infrastructure dates primarily to the 1960s. The treatment plant requires UV disinfection upgrades, lead service line replacement in pre-1986 neighborhoods, and capacity expansion to serve the North Gateway District and the industrial cooling demands of a potential data center campus. The EPA's WIFIA loan program provides low-interest financing (Treasury rate + 1%) with repayment deferred until 5 years after project completion — effectively subsidizing this investment by $3–5M versus conventional municipal borrowing.

Walnut River Flood Mitigation — $9M

The Walnut River's FEMA Zone AE floodplain designation affects downtown and east-side properties, constraining development and imposing mandatory flood insurance on federally backed mortgages. The green infrastructure approach — bioswales, retention basins, restored floodplain buffers upstream — reduces peak flood levels without the expense of concrete flood walls. FEMA BRIC grants cover 75% of eligible costs. Successful mitigation removes some properties from the mandatory insurance zone, immediately reducing homeowner costs and increasing property values in affected neighborhoods.

Brief 08 · Mobility

Transportation & Transit Network Phase 1–3

Estimated Investment: ~$35M · Annual Operating: ~$1.2M (50% CMAQ-eligible)

El Dorado's transportation strategy has to serve three distinct populations simultaneously: the 1,500+ daily commuters who travel to Wichita for work and need a reliable, affordable alternative to solo driving; the seasonal visitors who want to reach El Dorado Lake without a car; and the city's growing workforce population who will live and work locally but need connectivity within the city itself. No single transit product serves all three — the plan proposes three distinct branded services operating in parallel.

El Dorado Commuter Express

365-day weekday service, six daily runs (three morning inbound to Wichita, three evening outbound), anchored at a 200-space covered Park-and-Ride facility at the US-54/US-77 junction. $80 monthly unlimited pass — priced at roughly one tank of gas per week. Corporate pass programs for the refinery, aerospace campus, and data center create bulk ridership that stabilizes the route's finances. CMAQ grant covers 80% of capital costs and 50% of operating costs for the first three years.

El Dorado Lake Shuttle

Memorial Day through Labor Day, four daily runs between Wichita's east side and El Dorado Lake via Central Avenue. $6 one-way. Bike-rack equipped (16 bikes). Partners with Visit Wichita for tourism marketing — "El Dorado Lake Day Trips" packaged with Wichita hotel stays. The airport connector variant (Wichita Eisenhower to El Dorado, $12 round-trip) operates on the same summer schedule and is co-marketed with Visit Kansas through ICT Tourism corridor advertising.

Amtrak Thruway — Newton Connector

The Southwest Chief stops daily at Newton, KS — 30 miles west of El Dorado. A KDOT-funded Amtrak Thruway motor coach operating Newton → Augusta → El Dorado Lake → Downtown creates a genuine connection between El Dorado and the national rail network. Two daily runs aligned with Southwest Chief arrivals. Estimated operating cost: $280,000 per year, eligible for KDOT Thruway subsidy funding. The long-range vision — a standalone El Dorado Amtrak station on any future Midwest rail expansion — requires this Thruway connection as the first step toward demonstrating ridership demand.

US-54/US-77 Interchange Upgrade The diverging-diamond interchange upgrade at US-54/US-77 is the highest-priority transportation capital investment in the plan — not because it moves the most people, but because it removes the single greatest bottleneck to freight movement in and out of the industrial south corridor. At 80% KDOT cost share on a $14M project, the city's exposure is $2.8M. Every major employer in the south industrial zone — the refinery, the aerospace campus, the data center — has truck traffic that crosses this intersection daily.
Brief 09 · Public Safety

Public Safety Infrastructure Phase 1

Priority: Fire Station 2 is a Phase 1 non-negotiable · Est. Cost: $4.5M

El Dorado's current public safety infrastructure was designed for a city of 12,000 people concentrated in a relatively compact geographic footprint. The North Gateway District, as it develops, will extend the city's populated area northward by nearly a mile from the existing Fire Station 1 downtown. At that distance, response times to the north district will exceed 12 minutes from Station 1 — a threshold that insurance underwriters use to assign elevated commercial and residential insurance rates.

Fire Station 2 in the North Gateway District is the plan's single most important enabling infrastructure investment. Without it, residential developers in the North Gateway face insurance underwriting costs that make their pro formas unworkable — effectively killing the $45 million in private residential investment that the entire district depends on. The $4.5M cost of Station 2, funded through a combination of general obligation bonds and North Gateway TIF proceeds, is not a public safety expense. It is the fee of admission for the district's development to occur at all.

ISO Rating Improvement

Station 2, combined with the new aerial ladder truck and HAZMAT capability upgrade, positions El Dorado to improve from a Class 4 to a Class 3 ISO fire protection rating. This is not an administrative distinction — it has direct financial consequences for every property owner in the city. Brief 18 · Healthcare Economy

Health Economy & Community Wellness Strategy Phase 1–3

Butler County has one acute care hospital — Susan B. Allen Memorial Hospital, a 74-bed facility — serving a population of 70,000 across 1,428 square miles. That is a ratio of 1.06 hospital beds per 1,000 residents, compared to the Kansas average of 3.8 and the national average of 2.8. These designations are not abstractions — they mean that El Dorado and all of Butler County are actively under-resourced in the healthcare sector, that providers who work here qualify for significant federal loan repayment incentives, and that federal healthcare grant programs specifically target communities with HPSA status.

The economic case for healthcare investment is straightforward: an estimated 40% of Butler County's healthcare spending — approximately $120M annually — exits to Wichita because specialty care, surgical services, dental care, behavioral health, and senior care are unavailable locally. That $120M represents jobs, salaries, and tax revenue that currently benefit Wichita's economy. Capturing even 50% of that leakage would make healthcare the single largest economic development win in El Dorado's recent history.

Phase 1: The Highest-Leverage Investments

Surgical Center & Specialty Expansion ($25M): An ambulatory surgical center (ASC) attached to SBA Hospital would handle same-day orthopedic, general, and ophthalmological procedures at 40–60% lower cost than inpatient surgery — making El Dorado immediately competitive for surgical volume from across Butler County. USDA Community Facilities Direct Loan Program provides below-market financing (2.75–4.25% fixed rate) covering up to 75% of project cost for rural hospital expansions. This is the highest-return investment in the health economy cluster because it directly captures the largest single category of healthcare spending that currently exits to Wichita.

Behavioral Health & Addiction Services Wing ($10M): This is the most urgent and most underserved healthcare need in Butler County. Kansas ranks 47th nationally in mental health provider access. The rural opioid and methamphetamine overdose rate in central Kansas counties exceeds the state average by 35%. A 24-bed behavioral health and addiction treatment wing co-located on the SBA campus — designed for both inpatient stabilization and 30-day residential programming — would be the only such facility in Butler County. SAMHSA State Opioid Response (SOR) grants ($500K–$2M), HRSA Behavioral Health Workforce Education Training grants, and Kansas KDADS (Department for Aging and Disability Services) substance abuse treatment grants collectively cover 60–70% of project costs.

Rural Telehealth Coordination Hub ($3M): El Dorado's Velocity fiber network and SBA Hospital's EHR infrastructure create the infrastructure backbone for a rural telehealth hub. Brief 19 · People Infrastructure

Childcare, Workforce Housing & Family Stability Phase 1–2

Scope: 200+ new childcare slots + 80–100 new rental units + housing rehabilitation + homeownership programs · Est. Total: $27M+ · Enables workforce recruitment for all other sectors

The phrase "workforce housing" has become so overused in community development that it risks losing its meaning. This brief uses a more precise framing: El Dorado cannot recruit or retain the workers its economic development plans require until it solves two fundamental supply problems — childcare and quality housing. These are not social service issues. They are pre-conditions for economic growth.

Consider the math: if El Dorado successfully recruits a new manufacturing facility that hires 200 workers, perhaps 60 of those workers will have children under 5. If those workers cannot find licensed childcare in El Dorado, they cannot work. If their salary doesn't afford quality housing within a reasonable commute, they will not relocate from Wichita. The manufacturing facility's workforce is effectively capped by the city's supply of childcare slots and quality rental housing — not by the labor market. This is the invisible ceiling on El Dorado's economic development potential.

The Childcare Crisis — By the Numbers

Kansas Action for Children estimates that childcare shortage costs Kansas businesses $1.5 billion annually in lost productivity, absenteeism, and turnover. A childcare desert is defined as more than 3 children under 5 per licensed childcare slot. Significant areas of Butler County, including portions of El Dorado, meet this definition. The infant and toddler segment (0–2 years) is the most critically undersupplied: center-based care for infants requires the highest staff ratios (1:3 or 1:4), making it the most expensive to operate, so providers avoid it. Yet it is the age group most likely to force a parent out of the workforce when unavailable.

Kansas passed the Child Care Contribution Tax Credit (50% state credit for qualifying employer contributions to childcare facilities) in recent legislative sessions — creating the optimal window for El Dorado to build childcare infrastructure with meaningful state subsidy. The federal Child Care and Development Fund (CCDF), administered by KDHE, provides operating subsidies for income-qualified families at licensed facilities meeting quality standards. The combination of these programs makes new childcare capacity financially viable in ways it was not five years ago.

Childcare Intervention Plan

El Dorado Early Learning Campus (120 slots, $5M): A purpose-designed facility in the North Gateway district, co-located with the Butler CC satellite for shared facilities and student teacher placements. Serves ages 6 weeks–5 years on a sliding scale fee structure funded through CCDF subsidies. Kansas KOALA (quality rating system) certification at the highest tier unlocks additional state operating support of $3,000–$5,000 per child annually. This single facility absorbs the current waitlist and the projected demand from new residential households in the North Gateway development zone.

Anchor Employer Childcare Consortium ($2M employer fund): HF Sinclair, SBA Hospital, Butler CC, and the City of El Dorado — collectively employing ~1,200 adults — establish a shared childcare benefit fund. Kansas's Child Care Contribution Tax Credit makes each employer's contribution 50% deductible at state level. Employers receive reserved slots and marketing rights to their employees. This model reduces workforce turnover by 15–25% and absenteeism by 30–40% — ROI that pays for itself in reduced recruitment costs within 18 months at most participating employers.

Family Childcare Home Network (84 slots, $500K): Grow licensed home-based providers from ~10 to 20+ through $10,000 startup grants, Butler CC certification training, and a shared substitute provider pool. Home-based care is particularly valuable for non-standard hours, infants, and children with special needs — the gaps that center-based care cannot serve at scale.

Non-Traditional Hours Childcare ($200K/yr operating): Evening, overnight, and weekend childcare for shift workers at HF Sinclair and SBA Hospital, housed in the Early Learning Campus after hours. Staffed by Butler CC Early Childhood Education students in paid practicum placements — providing both the workforce and the training credit hours at minimal incremental cost to the city.

Workforce Housing — The Five-Strategy Approach

Workforce Rental Complex (80–100 units, $14M): New construction targeting $800–$1,200/month rents, financed with 9% LIHTC equity from KHRC + USDA Section 538 Guaranteed Rural Rental Housing loans. The HPSA designation and healthcare/education employment anchors strengthen the LIHTC competitive application. Target completion 2028.

Housing Rehabilitation Revolving Loan Fund ($5M): A self-replenishing fund providing 10-year, 2% interest loans to owner-occupants at <80% AMI for rehabilitation of pre-1960 housing stock. Capitalized through HUD CDBG. Rehabilitates 150 units before the first principal repayments recycle the fund for the next cohort of borrowers.

First-Generation Homeownership Program ($8K–$16K per buyer): Stack KHRC First-Time Homebuyer down payment assistance ($3K–$8K) with employer match programs (SBA, HF Sinclair, Butler CC, City — each committing $2K–$4K for employees purchasing in El Dorado) plus HUD-certified counseling. Target 50 new homeowners per year by 2028.

Land Bank Authority (K.S.A. 12-5901, enabled 2021): Establish El Dorado's Land Bank Authority — the legal mechanism to acquire tax-delinquent vacant and blighted properties, clear title, and transfer to productive use. First step: City Commission resolution (no external funding required). Identifies 50–100 properties in the first year, begins acquisition pipeline in year two. Pairs with the Rehabilitation Revolving Loan Fund to convert vacant lots into move-in-ready starter homes.

This brief's interventions change that answer to "Yes — and here's how." That single change unlocks the full value of every other investment in Vision 2040. Without it, new jobs create commuters, not residents — and commuters don't shop locally, don't join civic organizations, and don't send their children to USD 490.

Brief 20 · Cultural Economy

Arts, Culture & Heritage — Building El Dorado's Identity Economy Phase 2–3

Scope: Kansas Oil Museum expansion + historic preservation + performing arts + artist district + heritage trail + annual events · Est. Total: $35M+ · Visitors target: 100K+/yr

The economic development literature is unambiguous on one point: cities with distinct, authentic cultural identities consistently outperform comparable cities without them in residential attraction, business location decisions, and tourism revenue. The mechanism is not mysterious — people choose to live where life is interesting, and businesses follow workers. El Dorado's cultural development plan is not a quality-of-life amenity layered onto an economic strategy. It IS the economic strategy for talent attraction and tourism.

El Dorado's Authentic Cultural Assets — The Starting Point

El Dorado has several cultural assets that most cities its size would have to fabricate: (1) a genuine national-significance historical event — the 1915 oil discovery that supplied Allied forces in WWI and created one of the most productive oil fields in US history; (2) intact 1910s–1930s commercial architecture in a downtown that was never significantly demolished; (3) the Kansas Oil Museum, a 22,000 sq ft institution documenting that history; (4) a setting at the gateway to the Flint Hills Scenic Byway — one of Kansas's signature landscape destinations; and (5) El Dorado Lake State Park, the most-visited and largest state park in Kansas, drawing 1M+ visitors annually who currently leave the city without a cultural reason to stop.

The gap between these assets and El Dorado's cultural tourism profile is entirely a gap of intentional investment and programming — not a gap of underlying raw material. This brief addresses that gap systematically.

Kansas Oil Museum — The Heritage Anchor ($12M)

The Kansas Oil Museum's expansion from 22,000 to 50,000 sq ft transforms it from a local historical curiosity to a destination institution. Key additions: immersive 1915 discovery experience gallery using archival photographs, first-person oral history recordings, and period artifacts; an interactive drilling simulation; an outdoor equipment park with restored vintage oil field machinery; a rotating gallery connecting oil boom period art with Flint Hills landscape painting; a 200-seat lecture and event hall; and a gift shop and café operated by a local food entrepreneur. Target visitation: 75,000+ annually (up from under 25,000). At a conservative average visitor spend of $35 (museum admission + gift shop + one nearby meal), 75,000 visitors generate $2.6M in direct annual economic activity. Institute of Museum and Library Services (IMLS) Museums for America grants, NEA Challenge America, and KCAIC capital grants collectively fund museum expansions of this type. The expansion should be included in El Dorado's CDBG application as a primary tourism infrastructure investment.

Central Avenue Historic District — The 45% Tax Credit Stack ($18M private investment)

National Register of Historic Places designation for El Dorado's Central Avenue historic commercial district unlocks the most powerful building rehabilitation incentive in the federal tax code: the 20% Historic Tax Credit (HTC). Kansas supplements with a 25% state historic tax credit administered through KSPO. Together, a developer rehabilitating a historic downtown building in El Dorado can claim 45 cents in tax credits for every dollar of qualified rehabilitation expenditure — a dramatic improvement in project economics that makes previously infeasible renovations financially viable. The target: 15 buildings rehabilitated by 2030, $18M in private rehabilitation investment triggered by $8M in combined federal-state tax credits.

The NRHP nomination process takes 18–24 months from initial documentation to final designation. El Dorado should initiate the documentation process in 2025 — hiring a historic preservation consultant familiar with Kansas SHPO requirements — to ensure the tax credit stack is available when downtown developer interest materializes in response to other Vision 2040 investments.

Performing Arts Center — The Cultural Anchor ($15M)

A 400-seat performing arts center changes El Dorado's cultural profile in ways that no other single investment can replicate. It makes Butler CC's performing arts programs viable at a professional scale. It creates a venue capable of hosting touring productions, regional business conferences, graduation ceremonies, and gala fundraisers. It provides a focal point for the annual events calendar. And it signals to potential residents and businesses that El Dorado takes cultural life seriously.

The economics: a 400-seat venue hosting 80 events/year at 75% average occupancy (240 seats × $25 average ticket × 80 events = $480K annual ticket revenue) plus rental fees ($2,500/event × 30 events = $75K) and food/beverage generates approximately $600–$800K in direct venue revenue — plus $1–1.5M in visitor spending at nearby businesses on event nights. NEA Our Town grants explicitly fund arts facilities that create economic impact in underserved communities; the program funds up to $1M of project cost. State arts facility bond programs and CDBG public facilities funds supplement.

Heritage Trail, Artist District & Annual Events

Oil Boom Heritage Trail ($800K): Self-guided walking and driving trail connecting El Dorado's 1915 oil boom era landmarks with bilingual interpretive panels, QR codes linking to digital archives, and a smartphone audio tour app. A marketing partnership with the Flint Hills Scenic Byway — which already carries 200,000+ annual travelers — positions El Dorado as the "oil heritage gateway" to the Flint Hills. Cost: one-time $800K; operating cost near zero thereafter.

Artist Live/Work District ($5M): 30–40 below-market studios and live/work lofts in repurposed downtown oil-era warehouse buildings. KCAIC provides up to $500K; NEA Creative Placemaking funds the design and community engagement process. An established artist district creates the visible creative class signal that remote workers and young professionals identify as a quality-of-life indicator.

Four Annual Signature Events ($900K operating budget): Oil Boom Days (August), Flint Hills Prairie Arts Festival (September), Lake Lights Winter Festival (December), El Dorado Film Showcase (March). Collectively targeting 15,000+ cumulative attendance, $2.5M+ in annual visitor economic impact, and national press coverage through strategic media partnerships with Visit Wichita and the Kansas Tourism Division.

The Cultural Economy Multiplier Every $1 invested in arts and cultural programming generates $5–$8 in broader economic activity — the highest multiplier of any public investment category. Cities as small as Marfa, TX (pop. 2,000) and Pella, IA (pop. 11,000) generate millions annually from cultural tourism and creative economy activity by making the investment El Dorado has not yet made. El Dorado has more authentic cultural raw material than either of those cities. The only missing ingredient is intentional investment in telling the story.

Foreign Direct Investment & International Capital Strategy Phase 1–3

Scope: SelectUSA registration, FTZ activation, EB-5 structuring, 6-country recruitment strategy, spec building · Entry cost: $40K–$75K city investment · Potential: $50M–$500M+ in foreign capital

El Dorado's strongest economic development competitors are not pursuing foreign direct investment. That is not because foreign capital avoids small Kansas cities — the Panasonic $4B battery plant in De Soto, KS (Johnson County) demonstrates otherwise. It is because no community in Butler County has built the infrastructure — SelectUSA registration, an international-standard site package, bilateral trade organization relationships — that foreign company site selectors require before seriously evaluating a location. This brief lays out a concrete international recruitment strategy that positions El Dorado to compete for foreign capital in the 2027–2035 window, when multiple target-country companies will be actively selecting US production sites.

The Foundation: Wichita FTZ #28 Is El Dorado's Most Undermarketed Asset

Foreign Trade Zone #28 covers Butler County. Any El Dorado business can apply for FTZ subzone designation and immediately access: (1) deferred US customs duty on imported goods until they leave the zone for domestic consumption; (2) inverted tariff manufacturing — pay the lower of the raw material or finished product duty rate; (3) weekly entry procedures reducing customs transaction fees by 65–80%; (4) zero duty on goods exported directly from the zone. For a foreign manufacturer evaluating El Dorado vs. a city outside the FTZ, this translates to $200,000–$2,000,000 in annual duty savings depending on import volume — a decisive cost advantage. Yet no El Dorado economic development materials currently prominently feature FTZ status. This must change in Phase 1.

Six Priority Countries — Why Each, and How to Approach Them

Japan ($750B+ cumulative US investment stock): The largest foreign investor in the US. Japanese manufacturers target clean energy components, food processing technology, precision agriculture sensors, and aerospace parts — all matching El Dorado's planned sectors. Entry point: JETRO Chicago (Japan External Trade Organization), Kansas Commerce Osaka office, Kansas–Japan trade mission (biennial). Target companies: Yanmar (precision ag), IHI Corporation (aerospace), Mitsubishi Electric (factory automation), Mitsui & Co (commodities processing).

Germany (~3,000 US manufacturing operations): German Mittelstand companies (specialized mid-size manufacturers with global niche dominance) are the most active European investors in US secondary manufacturing markets. El Dorado's proximity to Wichita's Bombardier, Textron, and Boeing Wichita Structures operations is a compelling pitch for German aerospace component suppliers: MTU Aero Engines, Diehl Aviation, Liebherr Aerospace, Hensoldt. German renewable energy manufacturers (Siemens Gamesa, SMA Solar) are expanding US production under IRA incentives — El Dorado's wind resource and grid infrastructure are primary selling points. Entry point: GTAI (Germany Trade & Invest), German-American Chamber of Commerce Chicago, Kansas Commerce European trade mission.

South Korea (surging IRA-driven US manufacturing investment): Korean battery and clean energy manufacturers are the fastest-growing category of US FDI. The Panasonic De Soto, KS precedent proves Kansas is in play. SK Innovation, LG Energy Solution, Samsung SDI, POSCO, and Hyundai Steel are all expanding US capacity. El Dorado's 1,600-acre certified industrial site, Evergy below-national-average rates, BNSF rail access, and FTZ status directly address Korean site selection criteria. Entry point: KOTRA Chicago (Korea Trade-Investment Promotion Agency), Kansas Commerce Korea trade mission (annual).

Canada (aerospace, food processing, pension infrastructure): Bombardier's Wichita US HQ creates the most direct existing connection. Canadian aerospace supply chain companies (Pratt & Whitney Canada, CAE, Magellan Aerospace, Standard Aero) serve Bombardier and represent natural El Dorado industrial recruitment targets for overflow production space. Canadian pension funds — CPPIB, OMERS, Ontario Teachers' — manage over $1.5T CAD and are the world's most active US infrastructure investors. Their investment focus areas (data centers, utility infrastructure, industrial real estate, senior housing) match El Dorado's Vision 2040 capital requirements exactly. Entry point: Investment Canada, Canadian Consul General Chicago, pension fund infrastructure teams via infrastructure investment banks (Macquarie, CBRE Investment Management).

United Kingdom (aerospace MRO, life sciences, post-Brexit US expansion): Rolls-Royce's expanding US MRO footprint and BAE Systems' 30,000+ US employees create natural El Dorado recruitment targets in aerospace maintenance and defense manufacturing. Post-Brexit, UK companies are establishing US production at accelerated rates. Entry point: British Consulate General Chicago, GREAT Britain Campaign, UK Department for Business and Trade (DBT) USA team.

Netherlands & Belgium (agri-food tech, logistics): AGCO Corporation (Belgian heritage, major Hesston KS plant 35 miles from El Dorado) is the most tangible existing connection to Belgian agricultural manufacturing. Dutch agri-food technology companies (Priva, Ridder, Royal Brinkman) are expanding US precision agriculture support operations — directly aligned with the Butler AgriTech Hub. Rabobank (world's largest agricultural bank) finances agri-food businesses exactly like El Dorado's food manufacturing hub. Entry point: Netherlands-American Chamber of Commerce, Flanders Investment & Trade Chicago.

El Dorado likely qualifies as a TEA based on income data below state median. An EB-5 Regional Center aggregates multiple investors' capital — Kansas has multiple USCIS-approved Regional Centers that can structure El Dorado project offerings without the city creating its own Regional Center.

Best EB-5 candidates in Vision 2040: (1) Data Center ($500M+) — could attract 300–500 EB-5 investors at $800K each = $240M–$400M EB-5 equity tranche; ideal because job creation is high and technology infrastructure is a proven EB-5 sector; (2) Senior Care Campus ($28M) — healthcare is historically the strongest EB-5 sector; 90 direct jobs for 35 investors; (3) Lakeside Hotel ($12M) — hospitality EB-5 deals are well-precedented; 30+ jobs for 15 investors.
