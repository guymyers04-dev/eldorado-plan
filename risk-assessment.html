Risk Assessment

Vision 2040 challenges, market risks, and mitigation strategies. Transparency about what could derail this plan—and how we'll respond.

Why Risk Assessment Matters

Every strategic plan carries risks. Vision 2040 is no exception. This section identifies the biggest challenges, their probability and impact, and our mitigation strategies.

This is not a guarantee that all projections will be achieved. Rather, it's a realistic acknowledgment that market cycles, policy shifts, and unforeseen events can derail plans. By identifying risks upfront and planning responses, El Dorado maximizes the probability of success.

Each risk is assigned a probability (Low/Medium/High) and impact (Low/Medium/High). The most critical risks (High probability × High impact) receive the most attention and the most robust mitigation strategies.

Highest Priority

Critical Risks (High Probability × High Impact)

These risks pose the greatest threat to Vision 2040 success. Mitigation strategies are in place.

Mega-Site Industrial Recruitment Fails

High Risk

The 1,131-acre mega-site is central to Vision 2040's economic diversification. If major industrial tenants don't materialize (due to labor shortage, logistics changes, or supply chain shifts), the plan's financial foundation cracks.

Probability

Medium-High (40%) — Industrial recruitment is unpredictable; neighboring states compete aggressively.

Impact

Very High — Could reduce projected job growth by 500–800 jobs and $200M+ in investment.

Mitigation Strategy

  • ✅ AMAZONE win (2025) validates site market fit; use as reference case for future prospects
  • ✅ Diversify target sectors — Don't rely solely on one industry. Pursue precision ag, batteries, aerospace, logistics equally
  • ✅ Accelerate Phase 1 infrastructure — Complete road, rail, and utility infrastructure 2026–2027 to reduce prospect risk perception
  • ✅ Create incentive reserve — Establish $3–5M incentive fund (tax abatement, infrastructure rebates) to close deals
  • ✅ Weekly prospect tracking — El Dorado Inc. maintains pipeline of 20+ active prospects; quarterly reviews with City Council
  • ✅ Contingency pivot — If major industrial deal delays 2+ years, redirect focus to housing and small business growth as Plan B

GO/NO-GO Decision Point

If by end of 2028: No secondary major industrial commitment beyond AMAZONE → Review and possibly revise manufacturing targets. Shift to logistics, health, education sectors as primary growth drivers.

Macro Recession Delays Housing & Consumer Spending

High Risk

Housing demand and disposable income drive retail, restaurant, and service sector growth. A national recession (2027–2029?) could delay residential development and consumer spending in El Dorado.

Probability

Medium-High (50%) — Economic cycles are inevitable; recessions occur roughly every 7–10 years.

Impact

High — Could delay housing starts 2–3 years and reduce tax base growth by 15–25%.

Mitigation Strategy

  • ✅ Counter-cyclical investing — Accelerate public infrastructure investment during recession (roads, parks, downtown) to create jobs locally
  • ✅ Workforce stability programs — Support unemployment insurance, job retraining for laid-off workers to maintain consumer spending
  • ✅ Lending support for small business — Partner with SBA and local banks to maintain credit availability for business expansion
  • ✅ Affordable housing priority — In recession, shift focus to LIHTC-funded affordable units over luxury development to serve recession-impacted residents
  • ✅ 15-year flexibility — Plan extends to 2040; if recession delays 2–3 years, we have room to recover. Targets shift right on timeline, not down.

GO/NO-GO Decision Point

If recession occurs 2027–2029: Extend Phase 1 timeline to 2029. Don't force housing development; let market recover naturally. Maintain public investment in infrastructure to support recovery.

Regional Labor Shortage Persists

High Risk

Vision 2040 projects 1,600+ net new jobs. But if the regional labor shortage continues (workers migrate to coasts, birth rates stay low), employers may not find workers to fill jobs—limiting growth.

Probability

Medium-High (55%) — Demographic trends (low birth rates, urban migration) are strong and difficult to reverse short-term.

Impact

Very High — Could limit job growth to 800 (vs. 1,600 target) and slow population growth to +15% (vs. +41% target).

Mitigation Strategy

  • ✅ Remote worker attraction — Market El Dorado to remote workers (cost of living 30% below coasts, quality of life high). Target 200–300 remote workers by 2030.
  • ✅ Youth retention relentlessly — Higher-wage jobs + housing affordability + quality of life investments explicitly designed to keep 20–35 year-olds in El Dorado
  • ✅ Immigrant-welcoming strategy — Partner with regional immigrant services to attract workers and their families. Expand ESL programs in USD 490.
  • ✅ Automation & productivity — Encourage employers to invest in automation and productivity gains rather than pure headcount growth
  • ✅ Workforce training partnerships — Expand BCC health, manufacturing, and trade programs. Partner with employers for pre-hire training (CDL, welding, CNC).

GO/NO-GO Decision Point

If by 2030: Labor shortage persists and job creation lags (600 jobs vs. 1,000 target) → Shift strategy to higher-wage/fewer-job growth model. Quality-over-quantity emphasis.

External Factors

Market & External Risks (Medium Probability)

Market conditions and external events beyond our control that could impact Vision 2040.

Lake Tourism Market Shifts

Medium Risk

El Dorado Lake drives <$50M+/year in visitor spending. If technology (remote work reduces weekend travel) or demographics (younger people prefer urban experiences) shift leisure patterns, lake tourism could plateau.

Probability

Medium (30%) — Leisure patterns do change, but lake destinations remain popular nationally.

Impact

Medium — Could reduce projected tourism revenue by 20–30% but doesn't derail overall plan.

Mitigation Strategy

  • ✅ Diversify beyond lake — Invest equally in downtown, arts, and heritage tourism
  • ✅ Event programming — Create year-round events (races, festivals, concerts) to drive off-season visitation
  • ✅ Experiential tourism — Focus on authentic El Dorado experiences (oil history, agricultural heritage) vs. generic lakeside resorts

Energy Transition Disrupts Oil Industry Base

Medium Risk

HF Sinclair (local refinery) is a major employer. Faster-than-expected energy transition could reduce refinery demand. However, Vision 2040 deliberately diversifies away from oil dependency.

Probability

Low-Medium (25%) — Transition is happening but refineries will operate 20+ more years.

Impact

Medium — Could reduce refinery employment 10–20%, but plan targets diversified economy, so impact is limited.

Mitigation Strategy

  • ✅ Economic diversification is the primary mitigation — Plan targets 6 industries, not oil
  • ✅ Workforce transition support — If refinery reduces workforce, prioritize transition training and placement for workers
  • ✅ Refinery diversification conversation — Partner with HF Sinclair on alternative biofuel and renewable projects to extend viability
Internal Factors

Execution & Leadership Risks

Risks related to implementation, leadership, and organizational capacity.

Leadership Turnover Stalls Progress

Medium Risk

City Manager, El Dorado Inc. director, or key department heads leave before Vision 2040 is embedded in organizational culture. Replacement may not have same commitment.

Probability

Medium (40%) — Staff turnover in small cities is common; 15-year plan spans multiple administration changes.

Impact

Medium — Could delay initiatives 12–18 months if transition is poor, but won't derail long-term vision.

Mitigation Strategy

  • ✅ Governance institutionalization — Vision 2040 is adopted by City Council, not dependent on single leader
  • ✅ Leadership succession planning — Identify and mentor internal leaders 2–3 years before expected departures
  • ✅ Annual public reporting — Vision 2040 progress is presented publicly each January so community holds leadership accountable
  • ✅ Quarterly City Council updates — Keeps vision in front of elected officials regardless of management changes

Funding Constraints Delay Infrastructure

Medium Risk

Infrastructure (roads, utilities, fiber) costs $18–25M in Phase 1. If grants don't materialize or bond capacity is limited, projects delay.

Probability

Medium (35%) — Federal/state grant funding is competitive and unpredictable.

Impact

Medium — Could delay infrastructure 1–2 years but doesn't stop private development.

Mitigation Strategy

  • ✅ Diversify funding sources — Don't rely solely on grants. Mix bonds, TIF, developer contributions, county support.
  • ✅ Grant program manager hired — Dedicate staff to pursue federal/state grants (B.A.S.E., RAISE, ARPA, etc.)
  • ✅ Developer contributions — Require new developments to contribute to infrastructure (traffic impact fees, utility extensions)
  • ✅ Phased infrastructure — Build roads/utilities in sequence, not all at once. Private development funds later phases.

Community Opposition to Growth

Low-Medium Risk

Some long-term residents may resist growth—new traffic, schools crowding, changing community character. Organized opposition could delay zoning or development approvals.

Probability

Low-Medium (25%) — Growth-friendly communities usually have low resistance, but some friction is normal.

Impact

Low-Medium — Could delay specific projects 6–12 months but unlikely to stop overall plan.

Mitigation Strategy

  • ✅ Early & continuous community engagement — Quarterly community forums, not just approvals meetings
  • ✅ Transparency on growth impacts — Proactively communicate traffic, school, and service impacts + mitigation plans
  • ✅ Quality-of-life investments upfront — Parks, trails, downtown improvements show commitment to livability alongside growth
Policy & Compliance

Regulatory & Policy Risks

Changes in state/federal policy or regulation that could affect plan implementation.

Tax Credit/Incentive Policy Changes

Medium Risk

Plan relies on LIHTC (housing), Historic Tax Credits (downtown), and SB 98 (industrial). State/federal policy changes could reduce availability or competitiveness of these tools.

Mitigation Strategy

  • ✅ Diversify incentive tools — Mix tax credits, direct grants, TIF, and developer contributions so plan doesn't depend on single tool
  • ✅ Advocacy participation — El Dorado Inc. joins advocacy groups (ICMA, EDAC) to lobby for tax credit preservation
  • ✅ Contingency programs — If tax credits sunset, develop alternative funding mechanisms (municipal bonds, enterprise zones)

Environmental Regulation Impacts

Low Risk

Stricter environmental regs (water quality, air quality, stormwater) could increase development costs or delay projects. However, El Dorado's water quality is a strength, not a vulnerability.

Mitigation Strategy

  • ✅ Proactive compliance — Exceed environmental standards for water, air, and stormwater to build buffer against future regulations
  • ✅ Green development incentives — Encourage LEED certification and renewable energy as competitive advantage

GO/NO-GO Decision Framework

How we'll know if Vision 2040 is on track or needs major revision.

Annual Assessment (January Each Year)

Vision 2040 is assessed annually against Phase-specific targets. If 80%+ of targets are met, the plan continues. If 60–80%, course corrections are made. If <60%, major strategy revision is considered.

Phase 1 Targets (2026–2027)

  • ✅ North Gateway infrastructure 50% complete (roads, utilities, parks spine)
  • ✅ 1 major industrial prospect advanced (signed letter of intent)
  • ✅ Downtown Phase 1 (5 buildings rehabbed via Historic Tax Credits)
  • ✅ 150+ housing units permitted (North Gateway + Mixed-Income)
  • ✅ City Council commitment to 3-year capital plan ($500K+ annually)

Threshold: 4/5 targets met = GO | 3/5 met = CAUTION | <3 met = REVIEW

Phase 2–3 Targets (2028–2033)

  • ✅ Population growth tracking at 2%+ annually (200+ new residents/year)
  • ✅ 2+ additional industrial tenants committed or under negotiation
  • ✅ 500+ net new housing units occupied
  • ✅ Median household income growth 3%+ annually
  • ✅ Tax base growth 4%+ annually (inflation-adjusted)

Threshold: 4–5/5 targets met = ON TRACK | 3/5 met = COURSE CORRECTION | <3 met = STRATEGY REVISION

Decision Process

January Annual Review: City Manager presents Vision 2040 progress report to City Council. Data on all Phase targets presented publicly.

GO (80%+ targets met): Plan continues. Celebrate wins, identify gaps, maintain course.

CAUTION (60–80% targets met): Course corrections made. What's blocking progress? Remove obstacles. Adjust timeline for off-track initiatives.

NO-GO (<60% targets met): Major strategy review. Convene community forum. Update targets based on new market conditions. Revise if warranted.
