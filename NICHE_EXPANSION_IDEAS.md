# El Dorado Vision 2040 — Niche Expansion Ideas
**Comprehensive Brainstorm Across 8 Thematic Sections**  
**Target Audience:** Small-city economic development, community wellbeing, sustainability, civic engagement  
**Updated:** June 29, 2026

---

## 📋 How to Use This Document

Each section contains 5-10 **actionable, specific expansion ideas**. Each idea has:
- **Title** — Catchy, memorable name
- **Description** (2 sentences) — What it is and why it matters
- **Why It Matters for El Dorado** — Specific relevance to a 12K-person Kansas city
- **Implementation Example** — Concrete first step or proof-of-concept

**Philosophy:** Ideas are grounded in small-city realities (limited budget, tight-knit community, rural context), not Silicon Valley hype.

## 🏭 SECTION 1: ECONOMY

### 1.1 **Artisan & Maker Cooperative Network**
**Description:** Create a cooperative structure for local makers (woodworkers, ceramicists, metalworkers, bakers) to share studio space, equipment, and marketing. This overcomes the "one person can't afford a full studio" barrier that prevents creative entrepreneurs from launching.

**Why It Matters:** El Dorado's rural location attracts artisans seeking low cost of living and small-town community. A shared maker hub (potentially in a converted historic downtown building) creates employment, tourism draw, and cultural identity without heavy subsidy.

**Implementation Example:** Partner with USD 490's vocational program to locate a 3,000 sq ft "El Dorado Makers Guild" in the empty theater annex. Start with woodworking + ceramics. Charge $150/month per maker for studio access + shared equipment. Target: 12 makers, $18K annual revenue by year 2.

---

### 1.2 **Agritourism & Farm-to-Table Ecosystem**
**Description:** Develop a cluster of farm-based experiences (u-pick strawberries, hay rides, farm dinners, CSA pickup points) tied to local restaurants and B&Bs. This leverages El Dorado's agricultural heritage and transforms farmers into hospitality entrepreneurs.

**Why It Matters:** Small farms (50–200 acres) struggle with commodity prices. Agritourism adds 20–40% margin by selling direct to tourists. El Dorado has 3–5 farms within 5 miles of downtown that could participate immediately.

**Implementation Example:** Launch "El Dorado Farm Trail" map featuring 4 pilot farms offering u-pick or farm visits. Coordinate with Prairie Wolf Brewing for monthly "Farm Dinner Series" (farm hosts dinner, brewery provides beer, El Dorado Inc. handles marketing). Year 1 target: 8 farm dinner events, 400 attendees, $12K in farm sales impact.

---

### 1.3 **Remote Worker Attraction & Retention Program**
**Description:** Create a structured program (community events, co-working space partnership, tax incentives, networking platform) to recruit remote workers from larger metro areas and integrate them into El Dorado's economy. This expands the tax base and injects digital talent.

**Why It Matters:** COVID normalized remote work. 15–20% of tech/finance/creative workers now live in smaller cities by choice. El Dorado's low housing costs ($120K median home price) are compelling vs. Austin ($450K) or Denver ($550K).

**Implementation Example:** Launch "El Dorado Remote Workers" membership community (free first year). Perks: $25/month co-working credits at Chamber office, monthly social events, discount on commercial property for startups. Create landing page: "Move to El Dorado for $XX/month rent + community." Partner with RemoteOK and WeWorkRemotely to list El Dorado as a "remote-first city." Target: 25 remote workers in year 1, $5M new income influx.

### 1.4 **Niche Manufacturing Recruitment: Custom Medical Devices**
**Description:** Target small medical device manufacturers (implants, diagnostic tools, prosthetics components) seeking alternative to expensive coastal hubs. El Dorado's low cost of labor and land, highway access (I-35 corridor), and proximity to Kansas State engineering school create a competitive pitch.

**Why It Matters:** Medical device is a $150B industry with high growth. Average facility salaries ($45K–$65K) are 3x local employment. One 50-person facility = $2.5M annual payroll impact. Also: long employee tenure, recession-resistant.

**Implementation Example:** Partner with Kansas Department of Commerce and Wichita State's engineering program to create a "Med Tech Corridor" RFP targeting 3–5 device manufacturers. Highlight: Kansas tax incentives + El Dorado's available buildings (old school buildings, warehouses) + workforce training subsidy from USD 490. Target: 1 facility visit per quarter, 1 commitment within 18 months.

### 1.5 **Entrepreneurship Bootcamp for Gen Z & Millennials**
**Description:** Launch a 12-week intensive bootcamp (part online, part in-person) teaching business fundamentals, product-market fit, and Go-to-Market strategy to 20–30 young people annually. Graduates launch within El Dorado or remotely but remain connected.

**Why It Matters:** Brain drain = lost talent and energy. A bootcamp creates a "cool reason to stay" and retains 60–70% of participants in El Dorado. Low cost: $500 per person per bootcamp (grants + sponsorships cover it).

**Implementation Example:** Launch "El Dorado Startup Academy" (January intake). Partner with Ewing Marion Kauffman Foundation for curriculum + seed funding. Recruit from USD 490, Butler CC, and Facebook groups. Demo day at City Commission with investor judges. Graduates get $5K business incubation grant if they launch in El Dorado. Year 1 target: 30 participants, 8 businesses launched.

### 1.6 **Workforce Development: Skilled Trades Shortage**
**Description:** Expand vocational programs (HVAC, plumbing, electrical, welding) through partnerships with trade unions, apprenticeship subsidies, and salary transparency. Address the critical shortage of skilled workers in rural Kansas.

**Why It Matters:** El Dorado has chronic shortage of electricians and plumbers. Average wait time for service: 3–4 weeks. High school grads choosing trades earn $50–$75K without debt. Yet only 2–3% of USD 490 students pursue trades.

**Implementation Example:** Partner with IBEW Local 304 and Plumbers Union to offer 2-year paid apprenticeships (first 6 months at high school). City commits to hiring apprenticeship grads for municipal projects. USD 490 creates marketing campaign: "Start at $35K, earn $65K in 3 years." Create online apprenticeship directory visible at all schools. Year 1 target: 15 students in apprenticeship pipeline.

### 1.7 **Niche Retail: Vintage & Second-Hand Market**
**Description:** Encourage multiple vintage shops, thrift stores, and consignment boutiques in downtown by offering rent subsidies and shared logistics. This creates a "Vintage District" identity, attracts weekend shoppers from 50 miles away, and leverages El Dorado's existing antique dealers.

**Why It Matters:** Vintage retail is recession-resistant, high-margin (60–80%), and appeals to both millennials seeking sustainability and retirees/families seeking value. A 3-store cluster on a single block transforms that block into a destination.

**Implementation Example:** Launch "El Dorado Vintage District" with 3 anchor stores in downtown buildings (partner with building owners for rent subsidies). Create cross-promotional marketing: "Spend a Saturday in El Dorado's Vintage District." Coordinate with local cafes for "Vintage Passport" rewards (buy at 3 stores, get free coffee). Target: 3 stores open by Q4 2026, 500+ monthly visitors by year 2.

**Description:** Produce a bi-weekly podcast (15–20 min episodes) featuring El Dorado business owners, startup founders, and economic development leaders. Distribute on Spotify, Apple Podcasts, and YouTube. This amplifies local business visibility and attracts talent/customers to El Dorado's economy.

**Why It Matters:** Storytelling drives engagement and brand perception. A podcast positions El Dorado as "entrepreneurial & forward-thinking" vs. "dying rural town." Can be produced cheaply (home studio, ~5 hours/month).

**Implementation Example:** Launch "Made in El Dorado" podcast (produced by marketing intern + volunteer host). Episode 1: Prairie Wolf Brewing founder (how craft beer came to a small town). Episode 2: Artisan baker launching online sales. Episode 3: Remote worker moving to El Dorado from Austin. Aim for 500–1K downloads/month by month 6. Sponsor: El Dorado Inc. ($2K/year production budget).

### 1.9 **Supply Chain Localization Initiative**
**Description:** Map the "supply chain footprint" of El Dorado's largest employers (schools, hospital, city government, manufacturing) and systematically redirect 5–10% of procurement to local suppliers. This keeps dollars circulating locally and creates new business opportunities.

**Why It Matters:** USD 490 spends $40M annually; only ~5% goes to local suppliers. Hospital spends $8M; city government $2M. A 10% shift = $5M to local economy. Forces existing suppliers to grow or creates openings for new local ventures (janitorial, catering, maintenance, IT services).

**Implementation Example:** Create "El Dorado Supplier Directory" with 30+ local businesses (construction, food, cleaning, IT, accounting). Meet with USD 490 procurement director to audit current supplier base. Identify "easy wins" (bottled water, office supplies, janitorial) where local alternatives exist. Set target: 5% local procurement in 2 years, 10% in 4 years. Estimated impact: $3–5M additional annual spending to local economy.

### 1.10 **Digital Skills Training for Displaced Workers**
**Description:** Partner with remote companies to offer free or subsidized digital skills training (Google Ads, social media marketing, data analytics, basic coding) to workers displaced by automation or industry shifts. Graduates can stay in El Dorado and work remotely.

**Why It Matters:** Manufacturing automation and consolidation have eliminated 200+ jobs in Butler County over 10 years. Mid-career workers (age 35–55) need a pathway to remote-friendly skills. Training is low-cost and remote companies gain vetted candidates.

**Implementation Example:** Launch "El Dorado Digital Skills Academy" (partner with Google Digital Garage + remote-first company sponsors). Offer 8-week courses: Google Ads Specialist, Data Analytics, Social Media Marketing. Free for displaced workers (funded by workforce development grants + corporate sponsors). Placement: 70% of grads get remote jobs. Year 1 target: 25 participants, $500K in new annual remote income.

## 🏠 SECTION 2: HOUSING

### 2.1 **Empty Nester Downsize-in-Place Program**
**Description:** Target retirees in large inherited houses with a "Downsize & Share" program that converts a 4-BR home into a 2-BR primary + 1-BR accessory dwelling unit (ADU). Homeowner gets rental income; younger families get affordable housing. City streamlines ADU permitting (30 days vs. 6 months).

**Why It Matters:** El Dorado has 400+ homes occupied by 1–2 people age 65+. These houses sit underutilized while young families struggle to find rentals under $800/month. ADUs solve both problems and keep seniors in their neighborhoods.

**Implementation Example:** Partner with Butler County Extension to pilot 5 "Downsize & Share" conversions in year 1. Provide: free architectural consultation (sketch 1-BR ADU layout), expedited permitting (14-day review), property tax relief ($150/year for 5 years). Market through senior centers and churches. Show ROI: $400/month rental income = $4,800/year. Year 1 target: 5 ADUs permitted, creating 5 new rental units.

### 2.2 **Workforce Housing Voucher Program**
**Description:** City/non-profit partners provide $200–$300/month rental subsidies for essential workers (nurses, teachers, firefighters, grocery store managers) whose salaries don't match El Dorado's housing costs. Keeps essential workforce from leaving to higher-wage markets.

**Why It Matters:** USD 490 struggles to retain teachers (many go to Johnson County schools for 10% higher pay). Hospital has nursing vacancy rate of 8% (national average: 4%). A $200/month subsidy costs the city $24K/year but retains 20–30 workers worth $15M in annual institutional payroll.

**Implementation Example:** Launch "Essential Worker Housing Support" (pilot with 20 nurses + 10 teachers in year 1). Requirement: work in El Dorado for 2 years. City partners with medical center + USD 490 to fund vouchers ($300K/year combined). Marketing: "Live in El Dorado for $600/month (with support)." Year 1 target: 30 workers enrolled, 25 retained who would have left.

### 2.3 **Intergenerational Housing Co-Ops**
**Description:** Enable 2–3 family groups to purchase a large house together, dividing it into semi-independent units with shared common areas (kitchen, living room, laundry). Legal structure (co-op ownership) and financing (community bank partnerships) make it affordable and legally clear.

**Why It Matters:** Single-family housing is expensive for young families. Multi-generational living (grandparent + parent + children in one house) is growing trend. Co-op structure provides legal framework + emotional/financial support.

**Implementation Example:** Create "El Dorado Family Co-op Housing" program. Partner with Cornerstone Bank for "co-op friendly" financing (lower down payment if 3 families share). Identify 2–3 large homes (3K–4K sq ft, $150K–$200K) suitable for conversion. Provide legal templates from National Cooperative Housing Federation. Year 1 target: 1 pilot co-op (3 families, $50K down payment per family vs. $60K–$75K individually).

### 2.4 **Neighborhood Character Preservation & Incentives**
**Description:** Identify 2–3 historic neighborhoods (Victorian homes, bungalow districts) and provide tax credits ($2K–$5K per property per year for 5 years) for authentic restoration. Create a "Character District" designation that limits demolition and guides infill development.

**Why It Matters:** El Dorado has beautiful historic housing stock (1880–1920 architecture) but faces demolition pressure from absentee landlords and deferred maintenance. Preservation protects cultural identity, supports tourism, and increases property values (+8–12% in character districts nationally).

**Implementation Example:** Designate "Victorian Neighborhood Historic District" (50–70 homes, concentrated area). Partner with Kansas Preservation Alliance for design standards. Offer tax credit: $5K first year for $20K+ restoration work. Create before/after photo gallery highlighting restored homes. Year 1 target: 5 properties restored, 20+ more applying for credits.

### 2.5 **Mixed-Income Housing Development: Young Professionals Complex**
**Description:** Build a 40–60 unit mixed-income apartment complex (market-rate + affordable units) targeting young professionals (age 25–40) staying in El Dorado or relocating remotely. Include co-working space, event space, and rooftop garden to create community.

**Why It Matters:** Young professionals moving to El Dorado can't find suitable rental housing (current stock is 2-BR single-family homes or elderly low-income apartments). A "community living" model attracts and retains this demographic, which drives consumer spending + tax base + cultural vitality.

**Implementation Example:** Partner with Kansas Housing Resources Corporation for Low-Income Housing Tax Credits (LIHTC) + New Market Tax Credits (NMTC) to finance 40-unit complex. Design: 24 market-rate units ($750/month), 16 affordable units ($550/month). Include: co-working space (work-share), community kitchen, rooftop garden, event space. Developer ROI: 9–12% via tax credits + rental income. Year 1 target: Land acquisition + design; groundbreaking year 2.

### 2.6 **Housing Transition Support for Life Changes**
**Description:** Create a coordinated "Life Transition Housing" service connecting residents through major life changes (divorce, job loss, empty nest, move to assisted living) with temporary affordable housing, counseling, and landlord matching. Non-profit partner manages case management.

**Why It Matters:** Housing instability during life crises cascades (homelessness, job loss, health decline). A $30K/year intervention program (housing subsidy + case management) prevents $100K+ in emergency room and social service costs per person.

**Implementation Example:** Partner with Catholic Charities (Butler County) to launch "Life Transitions Housing Program." Offer: 10 subsidized rental units for 6–12 months (for transitions), case management, job coaching, financial counseling. Eligibility: residents facing job loss, divorce, health crisis, etc. Funding: $150K/year from city + state grants + private donations. Year 1 target: 15 families served, 85%+ successful housing retention.

### 2.7 **Accessory Dwelling Unit (ADU) Fast-Track Permitting**
**Description:** Streamline ADU permitting to 14 days (vs. current 90+ days) by pre-approving architectural designs and reducing zoning variance requirements. This removes friction for homeowners who want to add rental income or house extended family.

**Why It Matters:** El Dorado has zoning rules written in 1970s; ADUs are cumbersome. 30% of homeowners express interest in ADUs if permitting were simple. Fast-track permitting could generate 50+ ADUs in 3 years = 50 new rental units.

**Implementation Example:** Create "ADU Toolkit" (pre-approved designs, sample contracts, financing guide). Designate one city planner as "ADU specialist" (4 hours/week). Publish target: 14-day permit review. Market to homeowners via senior centers, neighborhoods. Year 1 target: 12 ADU permits, 12 new rental units created.

### 2.8 **Senior Living Continuum: Aging in Place Support**
**Description:** Develop a coordinated continuum from independent senior living (ADUs or age-restricted apartments), to assisted living (in-home care), to skilled nursing (medical care). Each stage is linked with services and support, preventing forced relocations.

**Why It Matters:** El Dorado's population is aging rapidly (22% age 65+). Family often moves seniors to distant cities for care (Kansas City, Wichita). A local continuum keeps seniors connected to community and relationships.

**Implementation Example:** Partner with medical center + nonprofits to create "El Dorado Senior Living Continuum." Components: (1) 20-unit age-restricted apartment complex (market-rate, with support services); (2) in-home care coordination (help seniors modify homes); (3) adult day program (social + health activities); (4) partnership with nursing homes in region. City role: zoning changes + seed funding for coordination infrastructure. Year 1 target: Plan + land acquisition; open units year 2–3.

### 2.9 **Rent-to-Own Program for First-Time Homebuyers**
**Description:** Partner with Cornerstone Bank + non-profit to create "Rent-to-Own" pathway for income-qualified first-time buyers (age 25–40, income $35K–$55K). Buyer rents for 3 years, builds credit, then purchases with down payment assistance.

**Why It Matters:** First-time buyers in El Dorado struggle to save down payment (homes $130K–$180K, need $15K–$25K down). Rent-to-own provides path to homeownership while seller builds sweat equity.

**Implementation Example:** Create "El Dorado Homeowner Pathway" with 15 properties identified. Model: Buyer rents 3 years at $600/month (15% goes to down payment fund). After 3 years, buyer has $32K in down payment + improved credit. Bank provides $98K mortgage at favorable terms. Seller gets stable tenant + eventual sale. Non-profit provides financial counseling + homeownership training. Year 1 target: 5 properties enrolled, 3 closings by end of year 3.

### 2.10 **Housing Affordability Dashboard & Transparency**
**Description:** Create a public, real-time dashboard showing housing inventory, prices, affordability ratio, and vacancy rates across El Dorado. This enables data-driven planning and helps residents understand housing market dynamics.

**Why It Matters:** Most residents lack visibility into housing supply/demand. Dashboard shows where affordable housing exists, which neighborhoods are appreciating, and what price points are available—enabling smarter planning.

**Implementation Example:** Partner with DataUSA + local MLS (Linda Kelley real estate database) to create "El Dorado Housing Dashboard" (public-facing, updated monthly). Show: average price by neighborhood, rental vacancy rate, price trends (3-year history), affordability index by income level. Embed on El Dorado Inc. website. Use data to guide affordable housing initiatives. Year 1 target: Dashboard live by Q3 2026, used to guide 2 new housing initiatives.

## 🏞️ SECTION 3: PLACE

### 3.1 **Micro-Mobility Network: E-Bike Share Program**
**Description:** Deploy 25–40 e-bikes at 5–6 "hubs" (downtown, library, high school, medical center, parks) enabling residents to borrow bikes for $2–$5/day. Free first-time membership. Encourages active transportation and reduces car dependency.

**Why It Matters:** El Dorado is bike-friendly (flat terrain, low traffic) but lacks infrastructure. E-bikes overcome age/fitness barriers and weather concerns. Increases downtown foot traffic (bikes parked at businesses = customers).

**Implementation Example:** Launch "El Dorado E-Bike Share" (partner with dockless bike company like Bluebikes or local nonprofit). Fund: $60K for bikes + hubs (grant from state transportation authority or city capital improvement budget). Marketing: "Get around El Dorado in 10 minutes." Sponsor: Prairie Wolf Brewing or hospital (health incentive). Year 1 target: 2,000 trips/month, 15% regular users.

### 3.2 **Public Spaces Redesign: "Parklets" & Pop-Up Seating**
**Description:** Convert 3–5 unused parking spaces or vacant lots into tiny public seating areas (parklets) with tables, chairs, plants, and art. Cost per parklet: $2K–$5K. Increase downtown social life and outdoor dining/gathering.

**Why It Matters:** Parklets transform dead space into vibrant public life. Studies show 30–50% increase in foot traffic + adjacent business revenue when parklets introduced. Costs almost nothing compared to payoff.

**Implementation Example:** Install 3 parklets on downtown Main Street (partner with property owners for space). Design: movable tables + chairs, raised gardens, public art (student art from USD 490). Activate with: weekly "Parklet Coffee Mornings," "Lunch & Learn" sessions, Art walks. Community sponsors adopt each parklet ($500 annual maintenance). Year 1 target: 3 parklets, $30K in adjacent business revenue growth.

### 3.3 **Heritage Walking Tour & Digital Wayfinding**
**Description:** Create a 2-mile self-guided walking tour highlighting El Dorado's architectural and cultural heritage (historic buildings, civil rights sites, founder stories). Combine with smartphone app (free, offline map) + physical signage. Attracts tourists and educates residents.

**Why It Matters:** El Dorado has rich history (1870s settlement, Victorian architecture, agricultural heritage) but stories are invisible. Walking tours drive tourism + cultural pride + foot traffic.

**Implementation Example:** Partner with Kansas Humanities Council + local historians to research & write 8–10 heritage stops. Create app using free tool (Google My Maps or custom React app). Install 8 bronze/aluminum plaques along route with QR codes linking to audio/photos. Launch: "Heritage Saturday" walking tours (monthly, led by volunteer guide). Year 1 target: 500+ digital map users, 200+ participants in guided tours.

### 3.4 **Community Gardens & Edible Public Spaces**
**Description:** Establish 3–4 community garden sites + edible landscaping in parks (fruit trees, berry bushes, herb beds). Free plots for residents; produce goes to households + donated to food pantry. Teaches gardening, builds community, improves food security.

**Why It Matters:** Addresses food insecurity (18% of El Dorado children food-insecure) + connects residents across social boundaries. A 20-plot garden produces $3K+ in food/year; cost per plot ~$30.

**Implementation Example:** Partner with Master Gardeners program (K-State Extension) to establish "El Dorado Community Gardens" at 3 city parks. Provide: tilled plots, irrigation, tool shed, compost. Fee: $25/year (covers water + maintenance). Free plots for low-income residents (20% allocation). Year 1 target: 50+ gardeners, 1,500 lbs produce, 200 lbs to food pantry.

### 3.5 **Gathering Venues Expansion: Outdoor Concert Series**
**Description:** Program monthly outdoor concerts (May–September) at rotating parks/downtown. Partner with local and regional bands (free or $5 suggested donation). Build atmosphere with food trucks, beer garden (Prairie Wolf), community picnics.

**Why It Matters:** Creates "reason to be downtown" on weekends, drives retail spending, builds community identity. Regionally, outdoor concerts attract 500–1K attendees + $20K–$30K in economic activity per event.

**Implementation Example:** Launch "El Dorado Soundstage" (monthly outdoor concerts). Logistics: partner with parks dept for stage/lighting setup ($5K one-time). Programming: country + local bands (months 1–3), rock/indie (months 4–6), family-friendly (months 7–9). Sponsors: Prairie Wolf, medical center, radio station (KVOE). Marketing: posters + Facebook + local media. Year 1 target: 6 concerts, 2,000+ total attendance, $15K+ retail impact.

### 3.6 **Historic Preservation & Adaptive Reuse Showcase**
**Description:** Highlight 3–5 successful historic building renovations (theater, old schools, storefronts) through documentation, photo exhibitions, and reuse case studies. Market to other building owners as "Look what's possible."

**Why It Matters:** Historic buildings are often seen as "liability" (high maintenance, unknown costs). Successful reuse projects prove otherwise and inspire other owners to invest. One successful reuse project increases adjacent property values 8–15%.

**Implementation Example:** Document "El Dorado Reuse Stories" (video series + photo exhibition). Project 1: Former movie theater now community event space. Project 2: Vacant school building becoming mixed-use (office + apartments). Project 3: Historic storefront renovated as artisan studios. Create 10-minute case study videos. Display exhibition at library + El Dorado Inc. offices. Share with other small-town preservation groups. Year 1 target: 3 case studies documented, 2 new reuse projects initiated.

**Description:** Commission 5–10 temporary and permanent public art installations (murals, sculptures, interactive art) across downtown and parks. Cost-share: City funds ($2K per piece), local artists receive honorarium ($500–$2K), community votes on locations.

**Why It Matters:** Public art transforms streetscape, attracts creative class, increases foot traffic, and builds cultural identity. Every $1 in public art generates $8–$20 in economic activity (retail, tourism, property appreciation).

Artists paid $1,500–$2,500 per project. Year 1 target: 3 installations, $50K+ in adjacent property appreciation.

### 3.8 **Farmers Market Expansion & Year-Round Activation**
**Description:** Grow existing Saturday farmers market (add 10–15 vendors, move to central downtown location) + launch winter market (January–March, indoors). Add prepared food, live music, kids' activities to create weekly gathering.

**Why It Matters:** Farmers markets are economic & social hubs. Studies show $1 vendor revenue = $2–$3 in community economic activity (customers spend at nearby shops). Year-round activation keeps downtown vibrant.

**Implementation Example:** Expand "El Dorado Farmers Market" from 20 to 40 vendors. Summer (May–October): Saturdays 8am–12pm on Broadway (closed to traffic). Winter (Jan–March): Indoors at community center, Saturdays 10am–2pm. Additions: 3 food vendors (tamales, kettle corn, fresh juice), live music (local musicians, $100/week stipend), kids activities (face painting, crafts). Budget: $15K/year. Year 1 target: 35+ vendors, 800+ weekly attendees, $2M+ in annual market revenue.

### 3.9 **Pocket Parks & Tactical Urbanism**
**Description:** Identify 5–10 underutilized "pocket" spaces (alleyways, abandoned lots, dead-end streets) and temporarily activate them with art, seating, gardens, and events. Low-cost experiments that test what makes places stick before permanent investment.

**Why It Matters:** Pocket parks cost $500–$5K to activate temporarily vs. $50K+ for permanent infrastructure. Proves feasibility and builds community buy-in. 70% of tactical urbanism becomes permanent after initial success.

**Implementation Example:** Launch "El Dorado Tactical Urbanism" project. Identify 5 pocket spaces: (1) alley behind Main Street (paint mural + add benches), (2) vacant lot near library (pop-up garden + market), (3) dead-end street (temporary street fair setup), (4) underpass (LED art installation), (5) side street (parklet setup). Budget: $2K per space, funded by grants + sponsorships. Activate 2–4 spaces in year 1 based on feasibility.

**Description:** Position El Dorado as a "creative small town" destination for weekend visitors (artists, foodies, families seeking authentic rural experiences). Marketing campaign, website, social media, partnerships with regional tourism boards.

## 👥 SECTION 4: PEOPLE

### 4.1 **Youth Retention & Brain Drain Prevention Program**
**Description:** Create a comprehensive retention strategy targeting high school graduates (age 18–22) with: mentorship networks, internship programs, entrepreneurship support, and social connection events. Connect them to local jobs, remote work, or education with intention to return.

**Why It Matters:** El Dorado loses 40–50% of high school graduates to larger cities. Each lost young adult = $1.5M–$2M in lifetime earning/tax potential. Retention focus on 50–100 strategically important graduates can reverse trend.

**Implementation Example:** Launch "El Dorado Futures Initiative" (partner with USD 490, Butler CC, Chamber). Pillars: (1) Internship program (20–30 internships with local employers, summer, paid $15/hour), (2) Mentorship network (40 mentors matched to high school juniors/seniors), (3) "Stay or Return" social events (monthly gatherings for young adults 18–30), (4) Remote work support (subsidized co-working for young professionals working remotely), (5) Start-up grants ($5K seed funding for business ideas). Year 1 target: 25 retention commitments, 15 internships placed.

### 4.2 **Immigrant & Refugee Integration Program**
**Description:** Create a structured welcome & integration pathway for immigrants and refugees relocating to El Dorado (housing, job placement, ESL classes, community connection, cultural events). Partner with faith communities and non-profits.

**Why It Matters:** Immigration is demographic opportunity for rural Kansas. El Dorado's empty housing + labor shortage make it ideal for refugee resettlement. 1 refugee family = 2–3 additional jobs created; $40K–$60K annual economic impact per family.

**Implementation Example:** Partner with Lutheran Family Services (already active in Kansas refugee resettlement) to welcome 20–30 families/year. Services: (1) Housing assistance (landlord partnerships, first-month subsidy), (2) Job placement coordination (partner with employers), (3) ESL classes (free, at library + USD 490), (4) Buddy system (local volunteers provide cultural orientation), (5) Monthly cultural celebrations (potlucks, celebrations of home countries at City Hall). Budget: $100K/year (grant-funded). Year 1 target: 25 families, 50+ jobs created indirectly.

### 4.3 **Mental Health & Wellness Hub**
**Description:** Establish a community mental health resource center (drop-in, low-cost counseling, peer support groups, crisis line, wellness classes) addressing depression, anxiety, and substance use disorders. Partner with medical center, non-profits, and state mental health department.

**Why It Matters:** Rural mental health is crisis (40% of suicides in rural areas, substance abuse epidemic, limited counselor availability). Centralized hub makes care accessible and reduces stigma.

**Description:** Partner with state/federal infrastructure programs to expand high-speed broadband access to rural El Dorado County + surrounding areas (Fiber or fixed wireless to 95%+ of residents). Enable remote work, distance learning, telemedicine.

**Why It Matters:** Broadband access = economic opportunity. El Dorado County has 20–25% unserved/underserved residents (vs. 8% nationwide). $5M investment in broadband infrastructure generates $50M+ in economic returns (remote work, e-commerce, education, healthcare).

**Implementation Example:** Partner with USDA Rural Development + Kansas Broadband Initiative to develop "Butler County Broadband Master Plan" ($50K study). Plan: (1) Coverage maps (identify unserved areas), (2) Technology options (fiber vs. fixed wireless tradeoffs), (3) Business model (public-private, subsidies needed), (4) Implementation roadmap (3-year build-out). If approved, pursue $5M–$10M in federal grants (Broadband Infrastructure Program, USDA). Year 1 target: Plan adopted, grant applications submitted.

### 7.8 **Agricultural Preservation & Right-to-Farm**
**Description:** Protect working agricultural land (farmland + ranches) from conversion through: conservation easements, purchase of development rights, right-to-farm ordinances, and farmland preservation policy.

**Why It Matters:** Rural land conversion is 1.5M acres/year nationally. Preserved farmland stays productive, maintains agricultural heritage, protects rural character. Conservation easement cost $200–$400 per acre vs. $2,000–$5,000 per acre for public land acquisition.

**Implementation Example:** Launch "Butler County Agricultural Preservation Trust" (non-profit board). Mechanisms: (1) Conservation easements (partner with state program to purchase development rights, $300/acre), (2) Right-to-farm ordinance (protects farmers from nuisance suits), (3) Agricultural zoning (limits non-farm development), (4) Succession planning (train next generation farmers). Target: Preserve 5,000 acres over 10 years. Year 1 budget: $500K. Year 1 target: Trust established, 10 conservation easements on 2,000 acres.

### 8.2 **Participatory Budgeting Program**
**Description:** Allocate $200K–$500K of city budget annually through participatory budgeting where residents vote on priorities (parks, trails, public art, community programs). Democratizes funding decisions.

**Why It Matters:** Participatory budgeting increases civic engagement (participation rates 3–5x higher than typical voting), improves public satisfaction with government (70%+ approval), and ensures funding reflects community priorities.

**Implementation Example:** Launch "El Dorado Participatory Budget" (allocate $300K annually). Process: (1) Community ideation (residents propose projects: parks, public art, programs), (2) Project development (city staff refines proposals, estimates costs), (3) Community voting (1 vote per resident, in-person + online), (4) Implementation (city executes top-vote projects). Year 1 examples: $100K downtown park redesign, $80K public art, $75K community programs, $45K trail improvements. Year 1 target: 2,000+ residents vote, 8–10 projects funded.

Budget: $25K annual. Year 1 target: 1,000+ survey respondents, 200+ suggestions submitted, 10+ suggestions implemented.
