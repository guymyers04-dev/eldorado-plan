How it works: Teachers earning $52K spend ~42% locally on housing, groceries, retail, dining. Building maintenance contractors hire local laborers. Utilities go to local co-ops. Multiplier effects compound through supply chains.

Community Impact Areas

Employment Anchor

650 jobs = 3.5% of El Dorado's workforce. 95% are full-time with benefits, averaging $59K annual compensation.

Multiplier: Each job supports 1.2 additional jobs in retail, food service, construction, professional services.

Tax Base Support

$52.8M operating budget supports local property tax base. Teacher housing purchases add $18–22M annually to residential market.

Impact: 35–40 new teacher households = $6–8M property tax revenue annually.

Supplier Ecosystem

$4.2M annual procurement from local contractors, food vendors, IT services, professional services.

Businesses served: 80–100 local vendors depend on USD 490 contracts.

Youth Development Pipeline

3,850 K–12 students in vocational, STEM, and career-focused programs. 85–90% of HS grads attend college or trade school.

Local retention: 40–45% return to El Dorado post-graduation (reversed brain drain).

Community Facilities

USD 490 facilities (stadiums, pools, auditoriums, gyms) host 12,400+ community events annually.

Economic value: $2–3M in avoided infrastructure costs for city.

Competitive Advantage

High-rated schools (USD 490 ranked top 25% in Kansas) attract remote workers, young families, executives.

Impact: Estimated 15–20% premium on new residential development due to school quality.

USD 490's Strategic Role in Vision 2040

  • Workforce development anchor: Vocational programs prepare 150–200 workers/year for logistics, manufacturing, health-tech jobs identified in Vision 2040
  • Talent attraction: High-rated schools (top 25% in Kansas) essential for recruiting remote workers, executives, young families
  • Community stability: 650 stable, well-paid jobs insulate El Dorado from economic downturns
  • Economic engine: $84M+ annual economic impact represents 12–15% of regional economy
  • Retention lever: Youth development programs keep 40–45% of high school graduates local (reversed brain drain)
